### Literary Movements: The Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood (PRB)

The Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood was a revolutionary group of young English painters, poets, and art critics founded in **1848**. The movement began in the visual arts but had a profound and lasting impact on Victorian poetry and design. It was a rebellion against what its members saw as the dull, academic, and overly formal art of their time, which they believed was based on a slavish imitation of the High Renaissance master, **Raphael**.

Their name, "Pre-Raphaelite," signifies their artistic ideal: they wanted to return to the art of the **early Italian Renaissance (before Raphael)**, which they admired for its vibrant color, intricate detail, and sincere, heartfelt expression.

---

#### **Core Principles and Philosophy**

The PRB laid out four main principles for their art and literature:

1.  **To Have Genuine Ideas to Express:** They rejected art that was merely decorative or sentimental. They believed art must have a serious purpose, whether moral, religious, or emotional.

2.  **To Study Nature Attentively, So as to Know How to Express Them:** This is their most important principle. They advocated for a meticulous, almost photographic, **realism**. They believed in painting directly from nature with intense attention to detail and truthfulness. This is a key link to the broader Victorian interest in realism, but the Pre-Raphaelites applied it with a unique intensity.

3.  **To Sympathize with What is Direct and Serious and Heartfelt in Previous Art, to the Exclusion of What is Conventional and Self-Parading and Learned by Rote:** This is their "Pre-Raphaelite" creed. They wanted to discard the rigid, classical poses and generic compositions of the Royal Academy (the official art institution of the time) and return to the perceived "purity" and sincerity of earlier art.

4.  **Most Indispensable of All, to Produce Thoroughly Good Pictures and Statues:** A commitment to high-quality craftsmanship.

---

#### **Key Characteristics of Pre-Raphaelite Art and Literature**

*   **Intense Detail and Realism:** Both paintings and poems are filled with rich, precise, and abundant detail. Every leaf, flower, and strand of hair is rendered with clarity.
*   **Vibrant Color and Sensuousness:** In rebellion against the drab colors of conventional Victorian painting, their works are startlingly bright and colorful. Their poetry is highly **sensuous**, appealing to the senses with vivid descriptions of sight, sound, and touch. This sensuousness was often seen as radical and even immoral by conservative Victorian critics.
*   **Symbolism:** Despite their realism, their works are also heavily symbolic. Every detailed object often carries a deeper moral, religious, or emotional meaning.
*   **Medievalism and Literary Subjects:** They were fascinated by the medieval past, Arthurian legends, and literary subjects, especially the works of Shakespeare, Keats, and Dante. Their paintings and poems often tell a story, full of narrative detail.
*   **The "Fleshly School of Poetry":** This was a critical term of abuse hurled at the Pre-Raphaelite poets, particularly Rossetti, by the critic Robert Buchanan. He attacked their work for being overly sensual, erotic, and morally questionable, which he saw as a departure from the high moral tone of poets like Tennyson.

---

### **Key Figures and Writings**

The movement centered around a core group of founders and their associates.

*   **Dante Gabriel Rossetti (D.G. Rossetti):**
    *   **Role:** A painter and a poet, he was the charismatic leader and driving force of the Brotherhood. His work is intensely passionate, sensual, and often explores the connection between spiritual love and physical beauty.
    *   **Key Writings:**
        *   ***"The Blessed Damozel":*** His most famous poem, written when he was a teenager. It depicts a young woman in heaven yearning for her earthly lover, blending the spiritual and the sensual.
        *   ***Sonnets for Pictures:*** Poems written to accompany paintings, highlighting the close link between the two art forms for the PRB.
        *   ***The House of Life:*** A complex sonnet sequence exploring love and destiny.

*   **Christina Rossetti:**
    *   **Role:** The sister of D.G. Rossetti, she was not an official member but was deeply connected to the group. She is now considered one of the greatest poets of the Victorian era. Her poetry combines Pre-Raphaelite sensuousness and rich detail with deep, often conflicting, Anglican faith.
    *   **Key Writings:**
        *   ***Goblin Market and Other Poems:*** Her most famous collection. The title poem is a rich, symbolic allegory about temptation, sin, and sisterly redemption.
        *   *"Remember," "When I am Dead, My Dearest," "Up-hill."*

*   **William Morris:**
    *   **Role:** A poet, artist, designer, and socialist thinker associated with the second wave of Pre-Raphaelitism. He championed craftsmanship and sought to reform design and manufacturing in reaction against shoddy, mass-produced industrial goods.
    *   **Key Writings:**
        *   ***The Defence of Guenevere:*** His first volume of poetry, noted for its dramatic, psychological explorations of Arthurian characters.
        *   ***The Earthly Paradise:*** A long narrative poem.

*   **John Everett Millais & William Holman Hunt:**
    *   **Role:** They were the other two principal founders of the PRB, primarily painters. Their famous paintings, like Millais's *Ophelia* and Hunt's *The Light of the World*, perfectly exemplify the Pre-Raphaelite style and often depict literary or religious subjects.

*   **John Ruskin:**
    *   **Role:** Not a member, but the most powerful art critic of the Victorian era. He became the PRB's most important champion and defender, praising their principle of "truth to nature" in his influential writings.

The Pre-Raphaelite Movement's emphasis on detailed symbolism and sensuousness was a major influence on the later **Aesthetic and Symbolist movements** of the late 19th century.
