### Literary Movements: Realism

Realism was a major literary and artistic movement of the mid-to-late 19th century that began in France and spread across Europe and America. It developed as a reaction against the perceived excesses and exoticism of **Romanticism**. While the Romantics were interested in the strange, the heroic, and the sublime, the Realists turned their attention to the **ordinary, the everyday, and the contemporary**.

The core ambition of Realism is **verisimilitude**—the faithful and truthful representation of reality. Realist writers sought to portray life "as it really is," without idealization or sentimentality. The **novel** was the perfect vehicle for this movement, as its length and narrative scope allowed for a detailed and comprehensive depiction of society.

---

#### **Core Principles and Philosophy**

1.  **Truthful Representation of Reality:**
    *   **The Principle:** This is the foundational goal. Realism aims to create a believable illusion of contemporary life. It rejects the supernatural, the legendary, and the improbable plots of earlier literature.
    *   **Focus on the "Here and Now":** Realist writers focused on the social and material world of their own time. They wrote about recognizable people, places, and events.

2.  **Focus on the Commonplace and the Ordinary:**
    *   **The Principle:** The subjects of Realism are not kings, knights, or tragic heroes, but the ordinary people of the middle and lower classes. Their daily lives, struggles, and social interactions are considered worthy of serious literary treatment.
    *   **Detailed Observation:** Writers acted like social documentarians, carefully observing and recording the details of everyday existence—how people dressed, what they ate, how they spoke, and the social customs that governed their lives.

3.  **Objective and Unsentimental Narration:**
    *   **The Principle:** The Realist author often adopts the tone of an impartial, objective observer. The goal is to present the facts of a situation without overt authorial judgment or emotional manipulation. The writer attempts to "show, not tell."
    *   **Rejection of Sentimentality:** Realism avoids the high-flown emotion of Romanticism and the overly sentimental "happy endings" common in earlier fiction. It often presents a more complex and sometimes harsh view of life.

4.  **Emphasis on Character and Psychological Depth:**
    *   **The Principle:** Realism is more interested in character than in plot. The works explore the complex inner lives of individuals and how their personalities are shaped by their heredity, environment, and social circumstances. The characters are not simply "good" or "evil" but are presented as morally complex and contradictory beings.

5.  **Social Critique:**
    *   **The Principle:** By accurately depicting the world around them, Realist writers often exposed the social problems of their time: class inequality, poverty, political corruption, and the hypocrisy of social conventions. While the narration might be "objective," the choice of subject matter itself often serves as a form of social commentary.

### **Key Figures and Writings**

While Realism was a dominant mode in England, it is often seen as reaching its purest form in France and Russia.

#### **In France (The Originators)**

*   **Honoré de Balzac:**
    *   **Role:** Considered one of the fathers of Realism. His ambition was to document all of French society in a massive collection of interconnected novels.
    *   **Key Work:** ***La Comédie Humaine (The Human Comedy)***, a series of nearly 100 novels and stories depicting French life in the 19th century.

*   **Gustave Flaubert:**
    *   **Role:** Famous for his perfectionist style and his commitment to objective, impersonal narration.
    *   **Key Work:** ***Madame Bovary* (1856):** A landmark of Realism. It tells the story of a provincial doctor's wife who dreams of a more exciting, romantic life and is ultimately destroyed by her attempts to escape the boredom of her ordinary existence. Flaubert's meticulous detail and psychological insight make it a masterpiece of the movement.

#### **In England (Victorian Realism)**

English Realism was often blended with a stronger sense of moral purpose and authorial commentary than its French counterpart.

*   **George Eliot (Mary Ann Evans):**
    *   **Role:** The greatest psychological realist of the English novel. She believed the novelist's duty was to present a truthful and compassionate understanding of ordinary human lives.
    *   **Key Works:**
        *   ***Middlemarch: A Study of Provincial Life:*** Often cited as the pinnacle of English Realism. It weaves together the stories of multiple characters in a small town to create a comprehensive and deeply insightful portrait of an entire society.
        *   ***Adam Bede***, ***The Mill on the Floss***.

*   **Charles Dickens:**
    *   **Role:** His work is a mix of Realism and melodrama. While he was a master at depicting the harsh realities of urban poverty and social injustice in Victorian England, his work also features larger-than-life characters, sentimental scenes, and complex, coincidental plots that sometimes stray from strict realism.
    *   **Key Works:** ***Bleak House***, ***Great Expectations***.

*   **Thomas Hardy:**
    *   **Role:** A late Victorian Realist whose work often darkens into **Naturalism** (see below). He meticulously documented the rural life and customs of his fictional region of "Wessex."

#### **Naturalism: An Extreme Form of Realism**

*   **What it is:** Naturalism is a more pessimistic and deterministic offshoot of Realism. Influenced by **Charles Darwin's** theories of evolution and heredity, Naturalist writers believed that human beings are not free agents but are instead controlled by forces they cannot understand or control: their instincts, their social environment, and their inherited traits.
*   **Key Figure:** **Émile Zola** (in France). His novels often depict characters struggling futilely against their squalid, oppressive environments.
*   **English Example:** **Thomas Hardy's** novels, like ***Tess of the d'Urbervilles*** and ***Jude the Obscure***, show characters who are crushed by an indifferent universe and the rigid laws of society, embodying a Naturalistic worldview.
