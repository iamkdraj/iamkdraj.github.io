Modernism was a revolutionary international movement in the arts, dominating the first half of the 20th century. It was driven by a conscious and radical break with the traditions of the 19th century. Modernist writers felt that Victorian forms were inadequate to represent the reality of a world changed by war, industrialization, and new psychological theories. Their central call to action, famously articulated by **Ezra Pound**, was to **"Make It New."**

The period roughly begins with the death of Queen Victoria in 1901 and ends with the conclusion of World War II in 1945.

---

### Core Principles and Philosophy

*   **Experimentation with Form**: This is the hallmark of Modernism. Writers broke traditional narrative structures, chronology, and grammar. They used fragmentation, collage, and disjointed perspectives to reflect the chaotic nature of modern life.
*   **Focus on Individual Consciousness**: Influenced by **Sigmund Freud** and **William James**, Modernists shifted their focus from the external, social world to the inner world of the individual mind. Reality was seen as subjective. A key technique was **Stream of Consciousness**.
*   **Theme of Alienation and Fragmentation**: Modernist works are filled with a sense of loneliness, spiritual emptiness, and the breakdown of communication. This disillusionment was greatly intensified by the trauma of **World War I**.
*   **Use of Myth and the "Mythical Method"**: In a world that seemed chaotic, many Modernists (like **T.S. Eliot** and **James Joyce**) turned to ancient myths (often drawn from **Sir James Frazer's *The Golden Bough***) to find a sense of structure and universal significance for their fragmented modern narratives.
*   **Intellectualism and Difficulty**: Modernist literature is often deliberately complex, dense, and challenging, packed with allusions and symbols that demand an active and educated reader. It often included elements of **Symbolism** and **Imagism**.
    > [!note]- What is Imagism?
    > Imagism was an early 20th-century movement in poetry, championed by Ezra Pound. It favored precision of imagery and clear, sharp language. Its principles included: (1) Direct treatment of the "thing," whether subjective or objective. (2) To use absolutely no word that does not contribute to the presentation. (3) To compose in the sequence of the musical phrase, not in sequence of a metronome.

### Key Figures and Writings

*   **Poetry**:
    *   **T. S. Eliot**: The most influential poet and critic. *Key Works:* ***The Waste Land*** (the definitive Modernist poem), "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock."
    *   **W. B. Yeats**: An Irish poet whose later work is profoundly Modernist. *Key Works:* "The Second Coming," "Sailing to Byzantium."
    *   **Ezra Pound**: A central figure and leader of the Imagist movement.
*   **The Novel**:
    *   **James Joyce**: Master of the Stream of Consciousness technique. *Key Works:* ***Ulysses*** (1922), ***A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man***.
    *   **Virginia Woolf**: A key innovator of the psychological novel. *Key Works:* ***Mrs. Dalloway***, ***To the Lighthouse***.
    *   **D. H. Lawrence**: Explored the psychological impact of industrialization on human relationships. *Key Works:* ***Sons and Lovers***, *Women in Love*.
    *   **Joseph Conrad**: An early Modernist whose narrative techniques influenced later writers. *Key Work:* ***Heart of Darkness***.
