### The Commonwealth Period (or The Interregnum) (1649 – 1660)

This brief but significant period marks the time when England was without a monarch. It began with the execution of **King Charles I** in 1649 and ended with the restoration of the monarchy under **King Charles II** in 1660. For this reason, it is also known as the **Interregnum** (meaning "between reigns").

The dominant political and social force during this time was **Puritanism**, led by **Oliver Cromwell**, who ruled as Lord Protector.

---

#### **Context and Influences**

1.  **Puritan Rule:** The Puritans were a devout Protestant group who sought to "purify" the Church of England of its remaining Catholic traditions. They valued sobriety, morality, and strict religious observance. Their rise to power had a profound and restrictive impact on the arts and culture.

2.  **The Closing of the Theatres (1642):** This is the single most important event for literature during this era. The Puritans, viewing the theatres as dens of sin and moral corruption, had them officially closed in 1642. This act brought the golden age of English drama to an abrupt halt. With no public stage, dramatists had no outlet for their work.

3.  **Censorship and Religious Focus:** The Puritan government imposed strict censorship. As a result, the literary output of the period was dominated by religious and political prose. The vibrant, secular, and often bawdy literature of the Renaissance was suppressed.

---

#### **Key Characteristics of Commonwealth Literature**

*   **Dominance of Prose:** With drama gone, prose became the most important genre. Most of this prose was religious (sermons, pamphlets) or political in nature.
*   **Decline of Poetry:** While some great poetry was written, the overall poetic output declined. The light-hearted, celebratory "Carpe Diem" spirit of the Cavalier poets was out of step with the somber Puritan mood.
*   **Moral and Religious Seriousness:** The literature that was produced was overwhelmingly serious, moralistic, and preoccupied with religious themes.

---

### **Major Authors and Works**

Despite the restrictive atmosphere, this period produced some of England's greatest literary figures, who were carry-overs from the previous age. Their major works, however, were often published during or just after this period.

#### **1. John Milton (1608-1674)**

Milton is the towering literary figure of the 17th century, second only to Shakespeare in the English canon. He was a devout Puritan and an active supporter of the Commonwealth government, serving as its Latin Secretary.

*   **Epic Poetry:** Though he had planned it for years, Milton wrote his masterpiece, the great English epic poem ***Paradise Lost***, mostly during this period and the early years of the Restoration (published in 1667).
    *   ***Paradise Lost:*** An epic poem in **blank verse** that seeks to "justify the ways of God to men." It tells the biblical story of the Fall of Man: the temptation of Adam and Eve by the fallen angel Satan and their expulsion from the Garden of Eden. It is celebrated for its grand, sublime style and its complex portrayal of Satan, who is often seen as a tragic hero.
    *   ***Paradise Regained:*** A shorter epic sequel that deals with the temptation of Christ in the wilderness.
*   **Dramatic Poem:**
    *   ***Samson Agonistes:*** A powerful tragedy modeled on classical Greek drama, telling the story of the biblical hero Samson in his final days. It is a work of great personal and political resonance, reflecting Milton's own blindness and his disillusionment after the failure of the Puritan cause.
*   **Prose:** Milton was a formidable prose writer, producing numerous pamphlets on political and religious liberty.
    *   ***Areopagitica:*** A powerful and passionate argument against government censorship and in favor of the **freedom of the press**. It is one of the most famous and influential defenses of free speech ever written.

#### **2. The Late Metaphysical Poets**

The intellectual and spiritual style of the Metaphysical poets continued, often with a more intensely mystical and religious focus.
*   **Andrew Marvell:** While also associated with the Cavalier style for some of his work, Marvell's more serious poetry, like "The Garden," has a deep metaphysical quality. He was a complex figure who managed to navigate the turbulent political landscape, serving as an assistant to Milton.
*   **Henry Vaughan:** A Welsh poet whose work is known for its religious mysticism and its innocent, almost child-like view of nature and spirituality.
*   **Thomas Traherne:** A clergyman whose poetry and prose celebrate the glory of God's creation with intense, mystical joy. His work was almost entirely unknown until it was discovered in the early 20th century.

#### **3. Prose Writers**

*   **Thomas Hobbes:** A major political philosopher whose work stands in stark contrast to Milton's. His masterpiece, ***Leviathan***, argues for a strong, absolute government (a monarchy) as the only way to save people from their brutish, selfish nature. He famously described life in a state of nature as "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."
*   **Izaak Walton:** Famous for ***The Compleat Angler***, a gentle and charming book about the joys of fishing, which serves as a quiet escape from the political turmoil of the day.

---
*The Commonwealth period ends in 1660 when the monarchy is restored. This event ushers in a completely new era in English literature: **The Restoration and the 18th Century**, also known as the **Neo-Classical Period** or the **Age of Reason**.*
