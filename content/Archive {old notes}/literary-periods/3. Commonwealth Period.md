# The Commonwealth Period (or The Interregnum) (1649–1660)

This brief but significant 11-year period marks the time when England was a republic, without a monarch. It began with the execution of **King Charles I** in 1649 and ended with the **Restoration** of the monarchy under **King Charles II** in 1660. For this reason, it is also known as the **Interregnum** (meaning "between reigns"). The dominant political and social force during this time was **Puritanism**, led by **Oliver Cromwell**, who ruled as Lord Protector.

---

### Key Context and Influences

*   **Puritan Rule**: The Puritans were a devout Protestant group who sought to "purify" the Church of England of its remaining Catholic traditions. They valued sobriety, strict morality, and religious observance. Their rise to power had a profound and restrictive impact on the arts and culture.
*   **The Closing of the Theatres (1642)**: This is the single most important event for literature during this era. Viewing the theatres as dens of sin and moral corruption, the Puritan-led Parliament officially closed them in 142. This act brought the golden age of English drama to an abrupt halt and silenced a major outlet for secular literature.
*   **Censorship and Religious Focus**: The Puritan government imposed strict censorship. As a result, the literary output of the period was dominated by religious and political prose (sermons, pamphlets). The vibrant, secular, and often celebratory literature of the earlier Renaissance was suppressed.

---

### Characteristics of Commonwealth Literature

*   **Dominance of Prose**: With drama gone, prose became the most important genre, largely focused on religious and political arguments.
*   **Decline of Lyric Poetry**: While great poetry was written (especially by Milton), the overall poetic output declined. The light-hearted, *carpe diem* spirit of the Cavalier poets was out of step with the somber Puritan mood.
*   **Moral and Religious Seriousness**: The literature produced was overwhelmingly serious, moralistic, and preoccupied with religious and political themes.

---

## Major Authors and Works

Despite the restrictive atmosphere, this period produced some of England's greatest literary figures, who were carry-overs from the previous age. Their major works were often written or conceived during this time, even if published later.

### 1. John Milton (1608-1674)

Milton is the towering literary figure of the 17th century, a devout Puritan and an active supporter of the Commonwealth government, for which he served as Latin Secretary.

*   **Epic Poetry**: Milton composed his great epics during and immediately after this period, having gone blind in 1652.
    *   ***Paradise Lost*** (published 1667): The greatest epic poem in English, written in **blank verse**. Its stated purpose is to **"justify the ways of God to men."** It tells the biblical story of the Fall of Man: the temptation of Adam and Eve by the fallen angel Satan and their expulsion from the Garden of Eden. It is celebrated for its grand, sublime style and its complex, tragically heroic portrayal of Satan.
    *   ***Paradise Regained*** (1671): A shorter epic sequel that recounts the temptation of Christ in the wilderness.
*   **Dramatic Poem**:
    *   ***Samson Agonistes*** (1671): A powerful tragedy modeled on classical Greek drama (observing the three unities). It tells the story of the biblical hero Samson in his final days of blindness and captivity. The work has strong personal resonance, reflecting Milton's own blindness and his disillusionment after the failure of the Puritan cause.
*   **Prose**: Milton was a formidable prose writer, producing numerous pamphlets on political and religious liberty.
    *   ***Areopagitica*** (1644): A powerful and passionate prose argument against government censorship and in favor of the **freedom of the press**. It is one of the most famous and influential defenses of free speech ever written.

### 2. The Late Metaphysical Poets

The intellectual and spiritual style of the Metaphysical poets continued, often with a more intensely mystical and religious focus. Many of their works were published during this period.

> [!note]- Who were the Metaphysical Poets?
> The Metaphysical Poets were a group of 17th-century poets who, though not a formal "school," shared a common intellectual and witty style. The term "Metaphysical" was coined critically by **Dr. Samuel Johnson** in the 18th century. He famously said their poetry was marked by a style where **"the most heterogeneous [dissimilar] ideas are yoked by violence together."** This refers to their use of clever, unexpected comparisons.

*   **Key Characteristics**:
    *   **Intellectual & Witty**: The poetry is highly intellectual and often structured like a logical argument.
    *   **Colloquial Language**: It uses the rough rhythms of everyday speech, often beginning with an abrupt, dramatic opening (e.g., Donne's "For God's sake hold your tongue, and let me love").
    *   **Use of Conceits**: Their most famous device.
    *   **Themes**: Love, death, and religion were their primary subjects.

    > [!note]- What is a Metaphysical Conceit?
    > A conceit is a clever and unconventional extended metaphor that establishes a surprising parallel between two very dissimilar things. For example, in "A Valediction: Forbidding Mourning," **John Donne** compares the souls of two separated lovers to the two legs of a geometer's compass—a strange, un-poetic image that he develops through logic to show their spiritual connection. It is a hallmark of the intellectual style of the Metaphysicals.

*   **Key Metaphysical Poets Active/Published in this Era**:
    *   **Andrew Marvell** (1621-1678): A complex figure who navigated the political turmoil, serving as an assistant to Milton. His poetry combines Metaphysical depth with Cavalier elegance.
        *   *Key Works*: "The Garden," "An Horatian Ode upon Cromwell's Return from Ireland," and his most famous poem, "To His Coy Mistress."
    *   **Henry Vaughan** (1621-1695): A Welsh doctor and poet whose work is known for its religious mysticism and longing for a lost, childhood innocence. He was heavily influenced by George Herbert.
        *   *Key Work:* "The Retreat."
    *   **Thomas Traherne** (c. 1637-1674): A clergyman whose joyful, mystical poetry and prose celebrate the glory of God's creation. His work was almost entirely unknown until its rediscovery in the early 20th century.

### 3. Prose Writers

*   **Thomas Hobbes** (1588-1679): A major political philosopher whose work stands in stark contrast to Milton's ideals of liberty.
    *   ***Leviathan*** (1651): His masterpiece of political philosophy. It argues for a strong, absolute government (an absolute sovereign or monarch) as the only way to save people from their brutish, selfish nature. He famously described life in a "state of nature" as **"solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."**
*   **Izaak Walton** (1593-1683):
    *   ***The Compleat Angler*** (1653): A gentle and charming prose dialogue celebrating the art and spirit of fishing, which serves as a quiet pastoral escape from the political turmoil of the day.

---
*The Commonwealth period ends in 1660 when the monarchy is restored with Charles II. This event ushers in a completely new era in English literature: **The Restoration and the 18th Century**, also known as the **Neo-Classical Period** or the **Age of Reason**.*
