### The Metaphysical Poets (c. 1600 – 1660)

The term "Metaphysical" refers to a group of 17th-century English poets who, while not a formal school or movement, shared a common intellectual, witty, and philosophical style. They broke away from the smooth, musical, and conventional love poetry of the Elizabethan sonneteers.

The label itself is famous. It was coined over a century later by the critic **Dr. Samuel Johnson**. He used it somewhat negatively to describe a style he found intellectually showy and emotionally cold. His most famous critique was that in their poetry, **"the most heterogeneous ideas are yoked by violence together,"** meaning they combined very dissimilar ideas in startling and unexpected ways.

---

#### **Context and Influences**

The Metaphysical style emerged from the intellectual climate of the Jacobean and Caroline ages:

1.  **A Time of Transition and Doubt:** The certainty of the Elizabethan world was fading. New discoveries in science and astronomy (like the work of Copernicus and Galileo) were challenging old beliefs about the universe and man's place in it. This sense of intellectual and spiritual uncertainty is reflected in the poetry's questioning, argumentative nature.
2.  **Reaction Against Elizabethan Convention:** The poets were tired of the predictable, sugar-coated conventions of Elizabethan love poetry, particularly the Petrarchan sonnet, where the lover was always sighing and the beloved was an unattainable, idealized goddess. The Metaphysical poets aimed for a more realistic, psychologically complex, and intellectually honest portrayal of love and life.
3.  **Influence of Scholasticism:** Many of the poets were highly educated men familiar with medieval scholastic philosophy, which used logic and debate to explore complex theological questions. They applied this same argumentative and logical method to poetry.

#### **Key Characteristics of Metaphysical Poetry**

This is the most important area to master for your exam.

1.  **The Metaphysical Conceit:** This is their hallmark.
    *   **What it is:** A **conceit** is a highly clever, complex, and often startling extended metaphor that makes a comparison between two things that seem to have no connection at all. Unlike a simple simile, it is developed and argued throughout the poem.
    *   **Classic Example:** In John Donne's "A Valediction: Forbidding Mourning," he compares the souls of two lovers to the two legs of a geometer's compass. One leg stays fixed at the center (the lover at home) while the other roams, but they are always connected and lean towards each other, making the circle perfect. This is a strange, un-poetic comparison, but Donne makes it work through brilliant logic.

2.  **Intellectual and Argumentative Tone:** Metaphysical poetry is often described as "thinking in verse." The poems frequently read like a logical argument or a debate, using reason to analyze emotion. The goal is not just to feel, but to *understand* the feeling.

3.  **Use of Colloquial Language and Abrupt Openings:** The poems often begin with a dramatic, conversational, and sometimes shocking opening line. They use the rhythm of everyday speech rather than smooth, predictable meter.
    *   **Example:** John Donne's "The Canonization" starts with the aggressive command: "For God's sake hold your tongue, and let me love."

4.  **Fusion of Passion and Thought:** T.S. Eliot, a 20th-century poet who helped revive interest in the Metaphysicals, praised them for what he called the **"unification of sensibility."** He argued that they did not separate thinking from feeling; for them, a thought was an experience that modified their sensibility. They could "feel their thought as immediately as the odour of a rose."

5.  **Use of Wit:** "Wit" here doesn't just mean humor. It refers to intellectual cleverness, ingenuity, and the ability to see surprising connections between different things.

6.  **Themes:** The primary subjects were:
    *   **Love:** Explored in a realistic, cynical, and sometimes spiritual way.
    *   **Religion and Faith:** Many poems are intense explorations of doubt, faith, and the relationship with God.
    *   **Death and Mortality:** A constant awareness of the fleeting nature of life.

### **Major Metaphysical Poets and Works**

*   **John Donne (1572-1631):** The pioneer and greatest of the Metaphysical poets. His work is divided into his youthful love poetry (often cynical and passionate) and his later, deeply religious poetry and sermons, written after he became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral.
    *   *Key Works:* "The Flea," "The Sun Rising," "A Valediction: Forbidding Mourning," the "Holy Sonnets" (especially "Death, be not proud").

*   **George Herbert (1593-1633):** A poet of quiet, intense religious devotion. His poems often use simple language and everyday imagery to explore complex spiritual struggles. Many of his poems are **emblematic** or **pattern poems**, where the shape of the poem on the page reflects its subject (e.g., "The Altar" and "Easter Wings").
    *   *Key Work:* A single volume of poems titled *The Temple*.

*   **Andrew Marvell (1621-1678):** A versatile poet who combined Metaphysical wit with classical grace. His poetry is known for its complexity and rich imagery.
    *   *Key Works:* "To His Coy Mistress" (a masterpiece of the "Carpe Diem" theme argued with metaphysical logic), "The Garden," "An Horatian Ode upon Cromwell's Return from Ireland."

*   **Henry Vaughan (1621-1695):** A Welsh doctor and poet whose work is filled with a sense of religious mysticism and a longing for a lost, childhood innocence. He was heavily influenced by George Herbert.
    *   *Key Work:* "The Retreat."

*   **Richard Crashaw (1613-1649):** Known for his passionate, ecstatic, and ornate religious poetry, influenced by the continental Baroque style. He converted to Catholicism.

*   **Abraham Cowley (1618-1667):** Was extremely popular in his own time, though his reputation has since faded. It was in a discussion of Cowley that Dr. Johnson originally developed his theory of the Metaphysical poets.
