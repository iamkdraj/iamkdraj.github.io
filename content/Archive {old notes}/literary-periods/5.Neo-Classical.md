### The Neo-Classical Period (1660 – 1798)
**(Also known as The Restoration and the 18th Century, or The Age of Reason)**

The term "Neo-Classical" literally means "New Classical." This period is defined by a renewed admiration for the classical literature of ancient Greece and Rome, but with a different emphasis than the Renaissance. While the Renaissance writers were *inspired* by the classics, the Neo-Classical writers sought to *imitate* and *emulate* them, valuing the qualities of **order, clarity, logic, and restraint** that they saw in works by authors like Homer, Virgil, and Horace.

This era begins sharply in 1660 with the **Restoration** of the monarchy and ends in 1798 with the publication of Wordsworth and Coleridge's *Lyrical Ballads*, which officially ushered in the Romantic Age.

---

#### **Context and Influences**

1.  **The Restoration (1660):** After eleven years of sober Puritan rule under Cromwell, the monarchy was restored with **King Charles II**. Charles and his court had been exiled in France, and they brought back the sophisticated, witty, and often licentious tastes of the French court. This led to an immediate and dramatic reaction against Puritan severity. The **theatres were reopened**, and a new kind of drama emerged.

2.  **The Age of Reason / The Enlightenment:** This was the dominant intellectual movement across Europe. It championed **reason, logic, and scientific observation** as the primary tools for understanding the world. Thinkers like **John Locke** in England and Voltaire in France argued that the universe operated according to a set of rational, understandable laws, and that human society could and should be organized along similarly rational lines. Superstition, religious fanaticism, and emotional excess were viewed with suspicion.

3.  **Rise of the Middle Class:** England was becoming a more commercial and urban society. A wealthy and literate middle class emerged, centered in London. This new audience wanted literature that was realistic, relatable, and dealt with contemporary social issues. This directly led to the rise of two new literary forms: the **novel** and **journalism**.

---

#### **Key Characteristics of Neo-Classical Literature**

*   **Emphasis on Reason and Logic:** Literature was expected to be rational, logical, and clear. Emotional outbursts were considered a sign of poor taste.
*   **Didacticism (Literature with a Moral Purpose):** Writers believed that literature should not just entertain, but also **instruct** and improve the reader. The goal was to teach moral lessons and correct social vices. **Satire** was the preferred tool for this.
*   **Value for Order and Clarity:** The style was precise, polished, and elegant. The **heroic couplet** (two rhyming lines of iambic pentameter) became the dominant poetic form, perfected by Alexander Pope.
*   **Focus on Society, Not the Individual:** Unlike the later Romantics, Neo-Classical writers were more interested in man as a social being. Their subject was society, its manners, its institutions, and its follies, not the inner world of the individual soul.
*   **Imitation of Classical Models:** Writers consciously modeled their works on classical genres like the epic, ode, and satire.

---

### **Major Sub-Periods and Authors**

The Neo-Classical Period is divided into three distinct phases.

#### **1. The Restoration Period (1660 – 1700)**
This phase is known for its reaction against Puritanism and is most famous for its witty, cynical, and often immoral **Comedy of Manners**.

*   **Restoration Comedy (Comedy of Manners):** These plays satirized the artificial manners and amoral intrigues of the upper class. The plots revolve around illicit love affairs, witty repartee, and the conflict between the sophisticated "wits" and the foolish "fops."
    *   **William Congreve:** The master of the genre. *Key Work:* ***The Way of the World***.
    *   **William Wycherley:** *Key Work:* ***The Country Wife***.
    *   **George Etherege:** *Key Work:* ***The Man of Mode***.

*   **Poetry:**
    *   **John Dryden:** The dominant literary figure of the age. He was a master of prose, drama, and poetry, and excelled at political satire. He perfected the use of the **heroic couplet**.
        *   *Key Works:* ***Absalom and Achitophel*** (a brilliant political satire), ***Mac Flecknoe*** (a satirical mock-epic).

*   **Prose:**
    *   **John Bunyan:** A Puritan writer who wrote the famous religious allegory ***The Pilgrim's Progress*** while in prison.
    *   **Samuel Pepys:** Famous for his detailed and candid ***Diary***, which gives an unparalleled eyewitness account of life during the Restoration, including events like the Great Plague and the Great Fire of London.

#### **2. The Augustan Age / The Age of Pope (1700 – 1745)**
This is the high point of Neo-Classicism, named after the reign of the Roman Emperor Augustus, which was a time of great literary achievement. The English writers saw themselves as achieving a similar level of classical polish.

*   **Poetry:**
    *   **Alexander Pope:** The central poet of the age. He is the undisputed master of the **heroic couplet**. His poetry is witty, satirical, and perfectly polished.
        *   *Key Works:* ***The Rape of the Lock*** (the most famous **mock-epic** in English, satirizing a trivial social squabble in the grand style of a classical epic), ***An Essay on Man*** (a philosophical poem), ***An Essay on Criticism***.
    *   **The Mock-Epic:** A key genre of the period. It uses the grand, lofty style of a classical epic (invocations, supernatural machinery, epic battles) to satirize a trivial or foolish subject.

*   **Prose:** This era is often called the **"Age of Prose and Reason."**
    *   **Jonathan Swift:** One of the greatest satirists in the English language. His satire is darker and more biting than Pope's.
        *   *Key Works:* ***Gulliver's Travels*** (a sweeping satire of human nature and politics), ***A Modest Proposal*** (a shocking satirical pamphlet suggesting that the poor Irish sell their children as food).
    *   **Joseph Addison and Richard Steele:** Pioneering journalists who created influential periodicals like ***The Tatler*** and ***The Spectator***. They wrote essays on social manners and morals, aiming to educate and refine the growing middle class.
    *   **The Rise of the Novel:** This period saw the birth of the English novel. Early novelists include:
        *   **Daniel Defoe:** ***Robinson Crusoe***, ***Moll Flanders***.
        *   **Samuel Richardson:** ***Pamela***, ***Clarissa*** (known for the **epistolary novel**—a novel written as a series of letters).
        *   **Henry Fielding:** ***Tom Jones***, ***Joseph Andrews***.

#### **3. The Age of Sensibility / The Age of Johnson (1745 – 1798)**
This is a transitional period. While the Neo-Classical ideals of reason and order still dominated, there was a growing emphasis on emotion, sentiment, and the individual's experience, which paved the way for Romanticism.

*   **Dr. Samuel Johnson:** The dominant literary figure of this phase. He was a poet, critic, essayist, and a brilliant conversationalist.
    *   *Key Works:* His monumental ***A Dictionary of the English Language***, his critical work ***Lives of the Most Eminent English Poets*** (where he famously analyzed the Metaphysical poets), and his philosophical romance ***Rasselas***.

*   **The Rise of the Novel (Continued):** The novel continued to develop and flourish.
    *   **Laurence Sterne:** ***Tristram Shandy*** (an experimental and wildly unconventional novel).
    *   **Tobias Smollett:** ***The Adventures of Roderick Random***.
    *   **Oliver Goldsmith:** ***The Vicar of Wakefield***.

*   **Pre-Romantic Trends (The Seeds of Romanticism):**
    *   **The Graveyard School:** A group of poets who wrote sad, reflective poems about death, mortality, and the transience of life, often set in graveyards. Their melancholic tone was a departure from Neo-Classical wit.
        *   **Thomas Gray:** His ***Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard*** is the most famous poem of this school and a landmark of 18th-century poetry.
    *   **The Gothic Novel:** This new genre emerged, focusing on mystery, horror, and the supernatural, set in gloomy, decaying castles. This was a direct rebellion against the rationalism of the age.
        *   **Horace Walpole:** ***The Castle of Otranto*** (considered the first Gothic novel).
        *   **Ann Radcliffe:** ***The Mysteries of Udolpho***.

---
*The publication of **Lyrical Ballads** in 1798, with its emphasis on emotion, nature, and the common man, marks the definitive end of the Neo-Classical era and the beginning of the **Romantic Period**.*
