# The Metaphysical Poets (c. 1600 – 1660)

The term "Metaphysical Poets" refers to a group of 17th-century English poets who, while not a formal school, shared a common intellectual, witty, and philosophical style. They consciously broke away from the smooth, musical, and conventional love poetry of the Elizabethan sonneteers (like Sidney and Spenser).

The label itself is famous. It was first used by **John Dryden**, who said that John Donne "affects the metaphysics." However, the term was popularized over a century later by the critic **Dr. Samuel Johnson** in his work *Lives of the Most Eminent English Poets*. He used it somewhat critically to describe a style he found intellectually showy and emotionally cold. His most famous critique was that in their poetry, **"the most heterogeneous ideas are yoked by violence together,"** meaning they combined very dissimilar ideas in startling and unexpected ways.

---

### Context and Influences

The Metaphysical style emerged from the intellectual climate of the Jacobean and Caroline ages:

*   **A Time of Transition and Doubt**: The certainty of the Elizabethan world was fading. New discoveries in science and astronomy (the work of Copernicus and Galileo) were challenging old beliefs about the universe and humanity's place in it. This sense of intellectual and spiritual uncertainty is reflected in the poetry's questioning, argumentative nature.
*   **Reaction Against Elizabethan Convention**: These poets grew tired of the predictable, sugar-coated conventions of Elizabethan love poetry, particularly the Petrarchan sonnet tradition where the lover was always sighing and the beloved was an unattainable, idealized goddess. The Metaphysicals aimed for a more realistic, psychologically complex, and intellectually honest portrayal of love.
*   **Influence of Scholasticism**: Many of the poets were highly educated men familiar with medieval scholastic philosophy, which used logic and debate to explore complex theological questions. They applied this same argumentative method to poetry, using reason to dissect emotions.

---

### Key Characteristics of Metaphysical Poetry

*   **Intellectual and Argumentative Tone**: Metaphysical poetry is often described as "thinking in verse." The poems frequently read like a logical argument or a debate. The goal is not just to feel an emotion, but to analyze and understand it.
*   **Use of Colloquial Language**: They used plain, direct language and the rough rhythms of everyday speech rather than smooth, "poetic" diction. Their poems often have abrupt, dramatic openings.
    *   **Example**: John Donne's "The Canonization" starts with the aggressive command: "For God's sake hold your tongue, and let me love."
*   **Use of Wit**: In the 17th-century sense, "wit" meant more than just humor. It referred to intellectual cleverness, ingenuity, and the ability to see surprising connections and parallels between different things.
*   **Fusion of Passion and Thought**: The 20th-century poet **T.S. Eliot**, who was instrumental in reviving their reputation, praised the Metaphysicals for what he called the **"unification of sensibility."** He argued that they did not separate thinking from feeling; for them, a thought was an experience that modified their sensibility. They could "feel their thought as immediately as the odour of a rose."
*   **Use of Paradox and Puns**: The poets frequently used paradoxes (statements that seem contradictory but contain a truth) and serious puns to explore complex themes.
*   **The Metaphysical Conceit**: This is their most famous and defining characteristic.

    > [!note]- What is a Metaphysical Conceit?
    > A conceit is a highly clever, complex, and often startling extended metaphor that makes a comparison between two things that seem to have absolutely no connection. Unlike a simple simile, the comparison is developed and argued logically throughout the poem, often drawing from fields like science, law, theology, or geography.
    >
    > **Classic Example**: In John Donne's "A Valediction: Forbidding Mourning," he compares the souls of two separated lovers to the two legs of a geometer's compass. This is a strange, un-poetic comparison, but Donne makes it work through brilliant logic to illustrate how the souls remain connected despite physical distance.
    >
    > **Another Example**: In "The Flea," Donne uses a flea that has bitten both him and his beloved as a complex, witty, and outrageous argument for their physical union.

### Major Metaphysical Poets and Works

*   **John Donne (1572-1631)**: The pioneer and greatest of the Metaphysical poets. His work is divided into his youthful love poetry (often cynical and passionate) and his later, deeply religious poetry and sermons, written after he became Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral.
    *   *Key Works:* "The Flea," "The Sun Rising," "Go and Catch a Falling Star," "A Valediction: Forbidding Mourning," "The Canonization," the "Holy Sonnets" (especially "Death, be not proud").

*   **George Herbert (1593-1633)**: A poet of quiet, intense religious devotion. His poems often use simple language and everyday imagery to explore complex spiritual struggles.
    *   *Key Work:* A single volume of poems titled ***The Temple***.
    *   *Notable Poems:* "The Pulley," "The Collar," and his **pattern poems** like "The Altar" and "Easter Wings," where the shape of the poem on the page reflects its subject.

*   **Andrew Marvell (1621-1678)**: A versatile poet who combined Metaphysical wit with the classical grace of the Cavalier poets. His poetry is known for its complexity and rich imagery.
    *   *Key Works:* "To His Coy Mistress" (a masterpiece of the *carpe diem* theme argued with metaphysical logic), "The Garden," "The Definition of Love."

*   **Henry Vaughan (1621-1695)**: A Welsh doctor and poet whose work is filled with a sense of religious mysticism and a longing for a lost, childhood innocence. He was heavily influenced by George Herbert.
    *   *Key Work:* "The Retreat."

*   **Richard Crashaw (c. 1613-1649)**: Known for his passionate, ecstatic, and ornate religious poetry, influenced by the continental Baroque style. He converted to Catholicism.

*   **Abraham Cowley (1618-1667)**: Was extremely popular in his own time, though his reputation has since faded. It was in his biography of Cowley that Dr. Johnson originally developed his theory of the Metaphysical poets.
