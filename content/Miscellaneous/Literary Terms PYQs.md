## Category 2: An Acquaintance with Literary Forms & Figures of Speech

### I. Literary Forms

#### Q. 7 (Paper 2011): Which one of the following is not a characteristic feature of a pastoral elegy ?
> [!tip]- The soul of the dead Shepherd realizes at the end that death in this world is the entry to a higher life.
> ❌ Incorrect. This is a common characteristic. The elegy traditionally moves from grief to consolation, often with a Christian or pagan assurance of immortality.

> [!tip]- The mourner charges with negligence of the nymph or other guardians of the dead Shepherd.
> ❌ Incorrect. This is a conventional feature. The poet, in his grief, often asks "Where were you, nymphs?" when the shepherd died, a famous convention seen in Milton's *Lycidas*.

> [!tip]- There is a procession of appropriate mourners.
> ❌ Incorrect. This is a typical feature of the pastoral elegy, where various figures from nature or mythology come to mourn the deceased shepherd.

> [!tip]- The speaker begins by invoking the muses.
> ✅ Correct! This statement is not a defining characteristic. While some elegies might invoke a muse, the conventional opening of an elegy is an expression of grief, not necessarily a formal invocation. The invocation of the muse is the primary convention of an **epic poem**.

#### Q. 118 (Spl Edu 2015): identify the literary device used: a story with mystery and terror usually built around a gloomy supernatural surrounding is a
> [!tip]- buildings roman
> ❌ Incorrect. A *Bildungsroman* is a "coming-of-age" novel that follows the psychological and moral growth of the protagonist.

> [!tip]- gothic tale
> ✅ Correct! This is the precise definition of a Gothic tale (or Gothic novel). It is characterized by mystery, terror, a gloomy atmosphere, and supernatural elements.

> [!tip]- fable
> ❌ Incorrect. A fable is a short story, typically with animals as characters, that conveys a moral.

> [!tip]- pastoral elegy
> ❌ Incorrect. A pastoral elegy is a poem mourning a death, set in an idealized rural landscape.

#### Q. 130 (ST-22, 2011/12): ‘The strophe’, ‘The antistrophe’ and ‘The epode’ are parts of which classical literary device?
> [!tip]- Sonnet
> ❌ Incorrect. A sonnet is a 14-line poem with a specific rhyme scheme.

> [!tip]- Epic
> ❌ Incorrect. An epic is a long narrative poem about heroic deeds.

> [!tip]- Elegy
> ❌ Incorrect. An elegy is a poem of mourning.

> [!tip]- Ode
> ✅ Correct! The strophe, antistrophe, and epode form the three-part structure of a **Pindaric Ode**, a type of formal, classical ode modeled on the odes of the Greek poet Pindar. The structure corresponds to the movements of the Greek chorus in drama.

### II. Figures of Speech & Poetic Devices

#### Q. 24 (TSS-24): The use of a word which is opposite in meaning to another in the same sentence is called -
> [!tip]- Antithesis
> ✅ Correct! Antithesis is the juxtaposition of contrasting ideas in balanced phrases or clauses. For example, "Man proposes, God disposes" or "To err is human, to forgive divine."

> [!tip]- Metaphor
> ❌ Incorrect. A metaphor is a figure of speech that directly compares two unlike things without using "like" or "as."

> [!tip]- Personification
> ❌ Incorrect. Personification is giving human qualities to inanimate objects or abstract ideas.

> [!tip]- Paradox
> ❌ Incorrect. A paradox is a statement that appears self-contradictory but contains a latent truth. Antithesis is about balanced phrasing of opposites, while paradox is about a contradictory idea.

#### Q. 36 (TSS-24): The figure of speech in which a statement appears to be self-contradictory but contains a latent truth is -
> [!tip]- Paradox
> ✅ Correct! This is the exact definition of a paradox. A famous example is "The child is father of the man."

> [!tip]- Antithesis
> ❌ Incorrect. Antithesis is the balancing of opposite words or ideas in a sentence, like "Speech is silver, but silence is golden." It's about structure, not a contradictory idea itself.

> [!tip]- Oxymoron
> ❌ Incorrect. An oxymoron is a figure of speech that combines two contradictory terms in a brief phrase, like "living dead" or "deafening silence." A paradox is a full statement.

> [!tip]- Irony
> ❌ Incorrect. Irony involves a contrast between what is said and what is meant, or between appearance and reality.

#### Q. 52 (TSS-24): The line, ‘The ploughman homeward plods his weary way’ is an example of -
> [!tip]- Transferred Epithet
> ✅ Correct! A transferred epithet is when an adjective (or epithet) is transferred from the noun it should logically describe to another noun in the sentence. Here, the way is not "weary"; the ploughman is weary. The adjective "weary" has been transferred from the ploughman to the way.

> [!tip]- Metaphor
> ❌ Incorrect. There is no direct comparison between two unlike things.

> [!tip]- Synecdoche
> ❌ Incorrect. Synecdoche is when a part represents the whole (e.g., "all hands on deck").

> [!tip]- Metonymy
> ❌ Incorrect. Metonymy is when a closely associated object is used to represent something else (e.g., "the crown" for the monarchy).

#### Q. 86 (STS-32, 2011/13): Select the literary device in which a part of something is used to signify the whole.
> [!tip]- Synecdoche
> ✅ Correct! This is the definition of synecdoche. For example, saying "lend me your ears" means asking for a person's full attention, or "wheels" to refer to a whole car.

> [!tip]- Metonymy
> ❌ Incorrect. Metonymy is substituting the name of an attribute for the thing meant (e.g., "the pen is mightier than the sword," where 'pen' means literature and 'sword' means war). It's based on association, not a part-to-whole relationship.

> [!tip]- Transferred Epithet
> ❌ Incorrect. A transferred epithet involves moving an adjective to a different noun.

> [!tip]- Litotes
> ❌ Incorrect. Litotes is an ironic understatement in which an affirmative is expressed by the negative of its contrary (e.g., "you won't be sorry," meaning you'll be glad).

#### Q. 87 (STS-32, 2011/13): The line, ‘The more haste, the less speed,’ is an example of an –
> [!tip]- Epigram
> ✅ Correct! An epigram is a brief, witty, and often paradoxical statement. This famous saying fits the definition perfectly as it is concise, memorable, and presents a surprising truth.

> [!tip]- Antithesis
> ❌ Incorrect. While it contains contrasting ideas (haste/speed), its primary function is to be a witty, paradoxical saying, which is the definition of an epigram.

> [!tip]- Oxymoron
> ❌ Incorrect. An oxymoron is a two-word paradox (e.g., "bitter sweet").

> [!tip]- Pun
> ❌ Incorrect. A pun is a play on words.

#### Q. 88 (STS-32, 2011/13): The line, ‘Fair is foul, and foul is fair’, is an example of an –
> [!tip]- Alliteration
> ❌ Incorrect. While the line does use alliteration with the 'f' sound, the primary device is the juxtaposition of contradictory ideas.

> [!tip]- Assonance
> ❌ Incorrect. Assonance is the repetition of vowel sounds.

> [!tip]- Paradox
> ✅ Correct! This line from Shakespeare's *Macbeth* is a classic example of a paradox. It is a self-contradictory statement that reveals a key theme of the play: the blurring of the lines between good and evil.

> [!tip]- Simile
> ❌ Incorrect. A simile is a comparison using "like" or "as."

#### Q. 95 (ST-22, 2011/12): Which of the following is not an example of personification?
> [!tip]- Let not ambition mock their useful toil
> ❌ Incorrect. This is personification. The abstract quality 'ambition' is given the human ability to 'mock'.

> [!tip]- Death lays his icy hand on kings
> ❌ Incorrect. This is personification. 'Death' is given human-like hands to perform an action.

> [!tip]- The hills rejoice and clap their hands
> ❌ Incorrect. This is personification. The 'hills' are given the human abilities to 'rejoice' and 'clap hands'.

> [!tip]- And honour's voice provokes the silent dust
> ✅ Correct! This is an example of **Metonymy**, not personification. 'Honour's voice' stands for the call of honor or posthumous fame. 'Silent dust' stands for the dead person. While 'honour' is personified with a 'voice', the core relationship in the line is metonymic. *However, this is a very ambiguous question as personification is also present. In exam contexts, one must choose the *best* fit, but the question is poorly constructed.*

#### Q. 96 (ST-22, 2011/12): Which is the figure of speech used in the following line ? “The quality of mercy is not strained; It droppeth as the gentle rain from heaven”
> [!tip]- Metaphor
> ❌ Incorrect. It is not a direct comparison stating one thing *is* another.

> [!tip]- Simile
> ✅ Correct! A simile is a comparison between two unlike things using the words "like" or "as". Here, the quality of mercy is explicitly compared to gentle rain "as" it drops from heaven.

> [!tip]- Apostrophe
> ❌ Incorrect. An apostrophe is an address to an absent person or an inanimate object.

> [!tip]- Personification
> ❌ Incorrect. Mercy is not being given human qualities; it is being compared to rain.

#### Q. 112 (Exam 2011): The repetition of the same consonant sound is known as :
> [!tip]- Alliteration
> ✅ Correct! Alliteration is the repetition of the same consonant sounds at the beginning of words or in stressed syllables. For example, "Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers."

> [!tip]- Assonance
> ❌ Incorrect. Assonance is the repetition of vowel sounds within words (e.g., "the r**ai**n in Sp**ai**n").

> [!tip]- Rhyme
> ❌ Incorrect. Rhyme is the repetition of similar sounds in the final stressed syllables of words.

> [!tip]- Rhythm
> ❌ Incorrect. Rhythm is the pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables in a line of poetry.

#### Q. 113 (Exam 2011): The line ‘The child is the father of the man’ is an example of :
> [!tip]- Metaphor
> ❌ Incorrect. While metaphorical in nature, its primary quality is its apparent contradiction.

> [!tip]- Epigram
> ❌ Incorrect. While it is concise and witty enough to be considered an epigram, the core logical feature is paradox.

> [!tip]- Paradox
> ✅ Correct! This famous line from Wordsworth is a classic paradox. It seems logically impossible but reveals a profound truth: that our childhood experiences and character shape the adult we will become.

> [!tip]- Simile
> ❌ Incorrect. It does not use "like" or "as."

#### Q. 129 (ST-22, 2011/12): What is a ballad?
> [!tip]- A short story
> ❌ Incorrect. A ballad is a specific form of poetry or song, not just any short story.

> [!tip]- A very long poem
> ❌ Incorrect. Ballads are typically narrative but not as long as an epic poem.

> [!tip]- A short story in verse
> ✅ Correct! This is a good working definition. A ballad is a poem or song that tells a story, often about adventure, romance, or a dramatic event, in a simple, direct style.

> [!tip]- A short lyric
> ❌ Incorrect. A lyric poem expresses personal emotions or feelings, while a ballad is primarily narrative (it tells a story).
