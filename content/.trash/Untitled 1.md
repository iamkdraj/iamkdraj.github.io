# The Renaissance Period (c. 1500–1660)

## Overview
The word **"Renaissance"** (French for "Rebirth") refers to the period of European history that marked a gradual but profound shift away from the medieval worldview. Originating in Italy in the late 14th century with foundational figures like **[[Petrarch]]** and **[[Boccaccio]]**, its influence reached England around the early 16th century, flowering in the Elizabethan and Jacobean eras. The term was first used in its modern sense by French historian **Jules Michelet** in 1855.

This era represented a "rebirth" of interest in the classical art, literature, and learning of ancient Greece and Rome. The beginning of the English Renaissance is often dated to **1485** with the start of the Tudor Dynasty, ending the preceding "Barren Age" of literature. The period's decline can be marked by the death of King James I in 1625 or, more broadly, the start of the Restoration in 1660.

## Key Context and Influences

### Humanism
This was the single most important intellectual movement. It shifted focus from a God-centered to a **human-centered** worldview, celebrating human potential, reason, and achievement. The ideal was the multi-talented "Renaissance Man."

> [!note]- What is Humanism?
> Humanism was a philosophical and educational movement that emphasized the value and agency of human beings. It promoted the study of the "humanities" – classical literature, history, rhetoric, and philosophy – believing that this education could help people achieve their full potential. 
> 
> **Key English Humanists:**
> - **[[Sir Thomas More]]** (author of *Utopia*)
> - **[[Erasmus]]** (Dutch humanist influential in England)
> - **[[John Colet]]** (Dean of St. Paul's Cathedral)
> - **[[Sir Thomas Elyot]]** (author of *The Book Named the Governor*)
> 
> It did not reject religion but focused on making life on Earth more meaningful.

### The Protestant Reformation
King Henry VIII's break from the Roman Catholic Church placed immense emphasis on the individual's direct relationship with God. This led to the monumental **[[King James Bible]] (1611)**.

> [!note]- The King James Bible (1611)
> Also known as the Authorized Version, this translation of the Bible was a landmark achievement of English prose. Its powerful, simple, and rhythmic language had an enormous and lasting influence on all subsequent English literature, standardizing the language for generations.
> 
> **Key Features:**
> - Committee of 47 scholars
> - Based on earlier translations (Tyndale, Bishops' Bible)
> - Majestic, ceremonial language
> - Influenced writers from Milton to Hemingway

### The Age of Discovery
Voyages by explorers like **[[Sir Francis Drake]]** and **[[Sir Walter Raleigh]]** broadened intellectual horizons, fueled national pride, and brought new wealth and ideas to England.

**Major Expeditions:**
- Sir Francis Drake's circumnavigation (1577-1580)
- Sir Walter Raleigh's attempts to colonize Virginia
- The defeat of the Spanish Armada (1588)

### Invention of the Printing Press
Introduced to England by **[[William Caxton]]** in 1476, this technology made books more accessible, fueling literacy and the rapid spread of Humanist and Reformation ideas.

**Impact on Literature:**
- Standardization of spelling and grammar
- Wider distribution of texts
- Growth of reading public
- Emergence of professional writers

## Literary Characteristics of the Renaissance

### Focus on Humanism
Literature explored complex human characters, their ambitions, and internal conflicts. **[[Christopher Marlowe]]** is often called the **"true child of the Renaissance"** for his depiction of protagonists with limitless ambition.

### Nationalism and Patriotism
A surge of national pride, especially after the defeat of the **Spanish Armada in 1588**, is evident in works like Shakespeare's history plays.

> [!note]- The Spanish Armada (1588)
> The defeat of this massive Spanish fleet, sent to invade England, was a pivotal moment. It secured England's independence from Catholic Spain, boosted national morale, and established England as a major naval power, fueling the patriotic spirit seen in Elizabethan literature.

### Richness and Experimentation
A "golden age" for the English language, with writers coining new words and experimenting with literary forms, most notably the **[[sonnet]]** and **[[blank verse]]**.

### Classical Influence
Writers drew heavily on Greek and Roman myths, history, and literary forms like the epic and tragedy.

**Key Classical Sources:**
- [[Ovid]]'s *Metamorphoses*
- [[Plutarch]]'s *Lives*
- [[Seneca]]'s tragedies
- [[Virgil]]'s *Aeneid*

### Spirit of Optimism
The era was marked by a belief in unlimited human and national potential, sometimes captured in phrases like **"love and lust for knowledge, beauty and wealth."**

## Sub-Periods of the Renaissance

The English Renaissance is typically divided into four phases:

### 1. The Elizabethan Age (1558–1603)
**Ruler:** [[Queen Elizabeth I]]
**Character:** Often considered the "Golden Age" of English literature, called a **"nest of singing birds"** for its explosion of poetry. Drama was the dominant literary form.

#### Elizabethan Drama

##### Early Developments
- **First regular English comedy:** Nicholas Udall's ***[[Ralph Roister Doister]]*** (c. 1553)
- **First English tragedy:** ***[[Gorboduc]]*** (1561) by Thomas Norton and Thomas Sackville
  - Notable for being the first play written in **[[blank verse]]**

> [!note]- What is Blank Verse?
> Blank verse is unrhymed poetry written in **iambic pentameter**.
> - **Iambic Pentameter:** Five iambic feet per line (ten syllables total)
> - **Iamb:** Two-syllable foot with stress on second syllable (da-DUM)
> - **Examples:** Shakespeare's plays, Milton's *Paradise Lost*
> - **Why important:** Rhythm closely mimics natural English speech

##### The University Wits
A group of educated, innovative playwrights who paved the way for Shakespeare.

> [!note]- Who were the University Wits?
> Late 16th-century playwrights and pamphleteers educated at Oxford and Cambridge. They transformed English drama with sophisticated plots and powerful language.
> 
> **Key Members:**
> - **[[Christopher Marlowe]]** - The greatest of the group
> - **[[Thomas Kyd]]** - Master of revenge tragedy
> - **[[Robert Greene]]** - Pastoral romance specialist
> - **[[John Lyly]]** - Court comedy pioneer
> - **[[George Peele]]** - Pageant and history play writer
> - **[[Thomas Nashe]]** - Satirist and pamphleteer

##### Major Playwrights

###### Christopher Marlowe (1564-1593)
The greatest of the University Wits. He perfected blank verse into what Ben Jonson called the **"mighty line."** His plays feature larger-than-life protagonists consumed by ambition.

**Key Works:**
- ***[[Dr. Faustus]]*** - A scholar sells his soul for knowledge
- ***[[Tamburlaine the Great]]*** - A shepherd becomes a world-conqueror
- ***[[The Jew of Malta]]*** - Revenge and greed in the Mediterranean
- ***[[Edward II]]*** - Historical tragedy about a weak king

**Marlowe's Contributions:**
- Perfected blank verse for drama
- Created the "overreacher" character type
- Influenced Shakespeare's early works

###### Thomas Kyd (1558-1594)
Famous for ***[[The Spanish Tragedy]]***, which established the wildly popular genre of the **[[Revenge Tragedy]]**.

> [!note]- What is a Revenge Tragedy?
> A dramatic genre where the central motive is revenge for a real or imagined injury.
> 
> **Key Elements:**
> - Secret murder (often of a family member)
> - Vengeful ghost demanding justice
> - Plotting and intrigue
> - Madness (real and feigned)
> - Bloody, violent climax
> - Multiple deaths in final scene
> 
> **Influenced by:** Roman playwright Seneca
> **Famous Examples:** *The Spanish Tragedy*, *Hamlet*, *The Revenger's Tragedy*

###### William Shakespeare (1564-1616)
The supreme dramatist of the age. His works span multiple genres and represent the pinnacle of English Renaissance drama.

**Categories of Works:**
- **Tragedies:** *[[Hamlet]]*, *[[King Lear]]*, *[[Macbeth]]*, *[[Othello]]*, *[[Romeo and Juliet]]*
- **Comedies:** *[[As You Like It]]*, *[[A Midsummer Night's Dream]]*, *[[Much Ado About Nothing]]*
- **Histories:** *[[Henry V]]*, *[[Richard III]]*, *[[Henry IV Parts 1 & 2]]*
- **Romances/Tragicomedies:** *[[The Tempest]]*, *[[The Winter's Tale]]*, *[[Cymbeline]]*

**Shakespeare's Innovations:**
- Complex, psychologically realistic characters
- Masterful use of soliloquy
- Blending of genres (comic relief in tragedies)
- Exploration of universal themes

###### Ben Jonson (1572-1637)
Shakespeare's contemporary, known for his satirical **[[Comedy of Humours]]**.

> [!note]- What is a Comedy of Humours?
> Comedy based on the ancient medical theory of the "four humours" (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile).
> 
> **Characteristics:**
> - Each major character dominated by single trait/obsession
> - Represents imbalance of bodily humours
> - Satirizes human folly and excess
> - Realistic, contemporary settings
> 
> **Jonson's Major Works:**
> - ***[[Every Man in His Humour]]*** (1598)
> - ***[[Volpone]]*** (1605)
> - ***[[The Alchemist]]*** (1610)
> - ***[[Bartholomew Fair]]*** (1614)

#### Elizabethan Poetry

##### Sonnet Pioneers
**[[Sir Thomas Wyatt]]** & **[[Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey]]**: They **introduced the sonnet** to English literature from Italy. Their poems were published in ***[[Tottel's Miscellany]]*** (1557), the first printed anthology of English poetry.

- **Wyatt:** Used Petrarchan form, translated from Italian
- **Surrey:** Created English (Shakespearean) form, pioneered blank verse

> [!note]- What is a Sonnet?
> A lyric poem of **14 lines** written in iambic pentameter with a specific rhyme scheme.
> 
> **Types:**
> 
> **Italian (Petrarchan) Sonnet:**
> - **Octave** (8 lines): `abbaabba` - presents problem/question
> - **Sestet** (6 lines): `cdecde` or `cdcdcd` - offers resolution
> - **Volta:** Turn in thought between octave and sestet
> 
> **English (Shakespearean) Sonnet:**
> - **Three quatrains** (4 lines each): `abab cdcd efef`
> - **Final couplet** (2 lines): `gg`
> - Often provides witty twist or summary
> 
> **Spenserian Sonnet:**
> - **Linked rhyme scheme:** `abab bcbc cdcd ee`
> - Created by Edmund Spenser
> - Combines elements of both forms

##### Major Poets

###### Sir Philip Sidney (1554-1586)
The model Renaissance courtier: soldier, scholar, poet, and statesman.

**Major Works:**
- ***[[Astrophel and Stella]]*** (1591) - Sonnet sequence that sparked English sonnet craze
- ***[[The Defence of Poesie]]*** (1595) - Major work of literary criticism
- ***[[Arcadia]]*** (1590) - Prose romance

**Sidney's Contributions:**
- Established the sonnet sequence in English
- Defended poetry against Puritan attacks
- Model of Renaissance versatility

###### Edmund Spenser (1552-1599)
Called "the Poet's Poet" for his influence on later writers.

**Major Works:**
- ***[[The Faerie Queene]]*** (1590, 1596) - Vast, unfinished allegorical epic
- ***[[The Shepheardes Calender]]*** (1579) - Pastoral poem cycle
- ***[[Amoretti]]*** (1595) - Sonnet sequence of 88 sonnets
- ***[[Epithalamion]]*** (1595) - Wedding poem

> [!note]- What is an Allegory?
> A narrative where characters, events, and settings represent abstract ideas or moral qualities.
> 
> **Features:**
> - Literal meaning + deeper symbolic meaning
> - Characters often personify virtues/vices
> - Often teaches moral lessons
> 
> **In *The Faerie Queene*:**
> - Red Cross Knight = Holiness
> - Una = Truth/True Church
> - Duessa = Falsehood/Catholic Church
> - Gloriana = Elizabeth I/Glory

> [!note]- What is a Pastoral Poem?
> Poetry dealing with idealized rural life and shepherds.
> 
> **Characteristics:**
> - Shepherds as main characters
> - Idealized countryside setting
> - Contrast between rural innocence and urban corruption
> - Often contains social/political allegory
> 
> **Examples:** *The Shepheardes Calender*, Marlowe's "The Passionate Shepherd"

> [!note]- What is the Spenserian Stanza?
> Nine-line stanza invented by Spenser for *The Faerie Queene*.
> 
> **Structure:**
> - First 8 lines: iambic pentameter
> - Final line: Alexandrine (iambic hexameter - 12 syllables)
> - **Rhyme scheme:** `ababbcbcc`
> 
> **Later Use:** Byron's *Childe Harold's Pilgrimage*, Keats's *The Eve of St. Agnes*

###### William Shakespeare (Poet)
Wrote a famous sequence of **154 sonnets** exploring themes of love, time, beauty, and mortality.

**Structure of the Sequence:**
- **Sonnets 1-126:** Addressed to "Fair Youth"
- **Sonnets 127-152:** Addressed to "Dark Lady"
- **Sonnets 153-154:** "Greek" sonnets about Cupid

**Other Poetry:**
- ***[[Venus and Adonis]]*** (1593) - Narrative poem, very popular
- ***[[The Rape of Lucrece]]*** (1594) - Serious narrative poem
- ***[[The Phoenix and the Turtle]]*** (1601) - Allegorical poem

#### Elizabethan Prose

##### Sir Francis Bacon (1561-1626)
Known as the **"Father of the English Essay."** His essays are celebrated for their concise, memorable, and **aphoristic style**.

**Major Works:**
- ***[[Essays]]*** (1597, 1612, 1625) - 58 essays on various topics
- ***[[The Advancement of Learning]]*** (1605) - Classification of knowledge
- ***[[Novum Organum]]*** (1620) - Scientific method

> [!note]- What is Aphoristic Style?
> Characterized by **aphorisms**—short, pithy statements expressing general truths.
> 
> **Features:**
> - Concise, memorable phrasing
> - Packed with meaning
> - Often paradoxical or witty
> 
> **Bacon's Famous Aphorisms:**
> - "Some books are to be tasted, others to be swallowed, and some few to be chewed and digested."
> - "Reading maketh a full man; conference a ready man; and writing an exact man."
> - "Knowledge is power."

##### John Lyly (1553-1606)
Court playwright and prose stylist.

**Major Work:**
- ***[[Euphues: The Anatomy of Wit]]*** (1578) - Created the style known as **Euphuism**

> [!note]- What is Euphuism?
> Elaborate prose style characterized by:
> - **Balanced sentences** (antithesis)
> - **Excessive rhetorical questions**
> - **Allusions** to classical mythology and natural history
> - **Alliteration** and wordplay
> 
> **Example:** "Do we not commonly see that in painted pots is hidden the deadliest poison?"
> 
> **Influence:** Fashionable at court but later parodied (Shakespeare's *Love's Labour's Lost*)

### 2. The Jacobean Age (1603–1625)
**Ruler:** [[King James I]]
**Character:** Marked by increasing pessimism, religious controversy, and darker themes in literature.

#### Key Characteristics
- **Religious tensions:** Puritan vs. Anglican conflicts
- **Political intrigue:** Gunpowder Plot (1605)
- **Scientific advancement:** Francis Bacon's empirical method
- **Economic challenges:** Inflation, trade disputes

#### Major Writers

##### Drama
- **[[William Shakespeare]]** (later works): *King Lear*, *Macbeth*, *The Tempest*
- **[[Ben Jonson]]**: *Volpone*, *The Alchemist*
- **[[Thomas Middleton]]**: *The Changeling*, *Women Beware Women*
- **[[John Webster]]**: *The White Devil*, *The Duchess of Malfi*
- **[[Francis Beaumont]] and [[John Fletcher]]**: Collaborative tragicomedies

##### Poetry
- **[[John Donne]]**: Metaphysical poetry, *Songs and Sonnets*
- **[[George Herbert]]**: Religious poetry, *The Temple*
- **[[Andrew Marvell]]**: *To His Coy Mistress*, political poetry

##### Prose
- **[[King James Bible]]** (1611): Authorized Version
- **[[Robert Burton]]**: *The Anatomy of Melancholy*
- **[[Thomas Browne]]**: *Religio Medici*

#### Jacobean Drama Characteristics
- **Darker themes:** Corruption, revenge, moral ambiguity
- **Complex psychology:** Flawed, morally ambiguous characters
- **Social criticism:** Attacks on court corruption
- **Theatrical spectacle:** Masques, elaborate staging

### 3. The Caroline Age (1625–1649)
**Ruler:** [[King Charles I]]
**Character:** Period of increasing political tension leading to Civil War.

#### Key Characteristics
- **Political crisis:** Conflicts between King and Parliament
- **Religious divisions:** High Church vs. Puritan tensions
- **Cultural refinement:** Court culture, emphasis on wit and elegance
- **Growing Puritan influence:** Opposition to theater and "frivolous" arts

#### Major Writers

##### Drama
- **[[James Shirley]]**: Last great Caroline dramatist
- **[[Philip Massinger]]**: Social and political drama
- **[[John Ford]]**: Psychological drama, *'Tis Pity She's a Whore*

##### Poetry
- **[[Robert Herrick]]**: *Hesperides*, cavalier poet
- **[[Thomas Carew]]**: Court poetry, literary criticism
- **[[Sir John Suckling]]**: Cavalier lyrics
- **[[Richard Lovelace]]**: "To Lucasta, Going to the Wars"

##### Prose
- **[[Thomas Browne]]**: *Religio Medici*, prose style
- **[[Izaak Walton]]**: *The Compleat Angler*
- **[[Jeremy Taylor]]**: Devotional prose

#### Cavalier Poets
Group of courtly poets supporting King Charles I.

**Characteristics:**
- **Themes:** Love, honor, beauty, carpe diem
- **Style:** Elegant, polished, classical
- **Attitude:** Aristocratic, refined, often libertine
- **Influence:** Classical poets (Horace, Catullus)

### 4. The Commonwealth Period (1649–1660)
**Ruler:** [[Oliver Cromwell]] (Lord Protector)
**Character:** Puritan dominance, theaters closed, epic poetry flourishes.

#### Key Characteristics
- **Puritan rule:** Moral strictness, theater ban
- **Religious intensity:** Emphasis on spiritual writing
- **Political upheaval:** Republic, then Protectorate
- **Cultural suppression:** Many "frivolous" arts discouraged

#### Major Writers

##### Poetry
- **[[John Milton]]**: *Paradise Lost*, *Paradise Regained*, *Samson Agonistes*
- **[[Andrew Marvell]]**: Political poetry, *Upon Cromwell's Return*
- **[[Henry Vaughan]]**: Metaphysical religious poetry
- **[[Thomas Traherne]]**: Mystical poetry

##### Prose
- **[[John Milton]]**: Political tracts, *Areopagitica*
- **[[Thomas Hobbes]]**: *Leviathan*
- **[[Jeremy Taylor]]**: *Holy Living* and *Holy Dying*

## Major Literary Forms and Techniques

### Drama
- **[[Blank Verse]]**: Unrhymed iambic pentameter
- **[[Soliloquy]]**: Character's internal thoughts spoken aloud
- **[[Aside]]**: Direct address to audience
- **[[Subplot]]**: Secondary plot lines
- **[[Comic Relief]]**: Humor in serious plays

### Poetry
- **[[Sonnet]]**: 14-line lyric poem
- **[[Sonnet Sequence]]**: Series of related sonnets
- **[[Pastoral]]**: Idealized rural poetry
- **[[Metaphysical Conceit]]**: Extended, complex metaphor
- **[[Carpe Diem]]**: "Seize the day" theme

### Prose
- **[[Essay]]**: Short, personal prose piece
- **[[Character]]**: Brief personality sketch
- **[[Euphuism]]**: Elaborate, artificial prose style
- **[[Aphorism]]**: Concise, witty saying

## Key Themes and Motifs

### Renaissance Themes
- **[[Humanism]]**: Human dignity and potential
- **[[Individualism]]**: Personal achievement and identity
- **[[Nationalism]]**: English pride and identity
- **[[Love]]**: Courtly love, Petrarchan conventions
- **[[Time]]**: Mutability, mortality, carpe diem
- **[[Appearance vs. Reality]]**: Deception, disguise
- **[[Order vs. Chaos]]**: Social hierarchy, natural order

### Common Motifs
- **[[The Great Chain of Being]]**: Hierarchical universe
- **[[Fortune's Wheel]]**: Rise and fall of fortune
- **[[Memento Mori]]**: Remember death
- **[[Ubi Sunt]]**: "Where are they?" - transience
- **[[Carpe Diem]]**: Seize the day
- **[[Pastoral Ideal]]**: Simple rural life

## Language and Style

### Vocabulary Expansion
- **Neologisms:** New words coined (Shakespeare alone added ~1,700 words)
- **Classical borrowings:** Latin and Greek terms
- **Compound words:** Creative combinations
- **Functional shift:** Words changing grammatical roles

### Rhetorical Devices
- **[[Metaphor]]** and **[[Simile]]**: Figurative comparisons
- **[[Personification]]**: Human qualities to non-human things
- **[[Alliteration]]**: Repetition of initial consonant sounds
- **[[Assonance]]**: Repetition of vowel sounds
