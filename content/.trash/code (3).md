### 1. Fill in the blank with the most appropriate degree of adjective from the options given below - 
She can knit................than she can sew.
(1) best
(2) good
(3) better
(4) more better
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) best
> ❌ Incorrect. "Best" is the superlative degree, used for comparing three or more things.

> [!tip]- (2) good
> ❌ Incorrect. "Good" is the positive degree and is not used for comparisons.

> [!tip]- (3) better
> ✅ Correct. The word "than" indicates a comparison between two activities (knitting and sewing), which requires the comparative degree "better."

> [!tip]- (4) more better
> ❌ Incorrect. This is a double comparative. "Better" is already the comparative form of "good."

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The sentence makes a direct comparison between two skills using the word **"than"**. This requires the **comparative degree** of the adjective. The adjective "good" is irregular, and its degrees are:
> - **Positive:** good
> - **Comparative:** better
> - **Superlative:** best
> Therefore, "better" is the correct form for comparing two items. "More better" is always incorrect.

### 2. The term ‘Existentialism’ was coined by -
(1) Soren Kierkegaard
(2) Jean-Paul Sartre
(3) Martin Heidegger
(4) Friedrich Nietzsche
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) Soren Kierkegaard
> ❌ Incorrect. Kierkegaard is considered the "father of existentialism," but he did not use the term himself.

> [!tip]- (2) Jean-Paul Sartre
> ✅ Correct. While the French philosopher Gabriel Marcel first applied the term to Sartre in 1945, Jean-Paul Sartre was the one who accepted, defined, and popularized it, making him most associated with its coining in the public sphere.

> [!tip]- (3) Martin Heidegger
> ❌ Incorrect. Heidegger was a key figure in existentialism but rejected the label and did not coin the term.

> [!tip]- (4) Friedrich Nietzsche
> ❌ Incorrect. Nietzsche was a major influence on existentialism but wrote before the term was created.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The term **‘Existentialism’** has a complex origin. The philosopher **Søren Kierkegaard** is regarded as the first existentialist philosopher, but the term itself emerged much later. It was the French Catholic philosopher **Gabriel Marcel** who first used the term in the mid-1940s in a colloquy, applying it to **Jean-Paul Sartre**. Sartre then embraced the label in his lecture and essay "Existentialism Is a Humanism" (1946), defining it for a wide audience. Because Sartre defined and popularized the term, he is commonly credited with its "coining" in a functional sense.

### 3. What does the Phrasal Verb “got over” mean in the sentence?
He used to be afraid of heights but he has got over that now.
(1) catch
(2) escape
(3) to overcome or recover
(4) avoid
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) catch
> ❌ Incorrect. This is unrelated to the meaning.

> [!tip]- (2) escape
> ❌ Incorrect. While related, "overcome" is more precise for dealing with a fear or illness.

> [!tip]- (3) to overcome or recover
> ✅ Correct. The phrasal verb "get over" means to recover from an illness, setback, or emotional difficulty like a fear.

> [!tip]- (4) avoid
> ❌ Incorrect. "Getting over" something means facing and conquering it, not avoiding it.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The phrasal verb **"get over"** means to return to your usual state of health or happiness after an illness, a shock, or a disappointment. It also means to stop being controlled or bothered by something, such as a fear. In the sentence, the person is no longer controlled by his fear of heights, meaning he has **overcome** it.

### 4. Fill in the blanks with suitable Articles if any in the blanks below; put ‘x’ where ‘No-Article’ is needed -
.................... Neil Armstrong was .................... first man on .................... moon.
From the options given below, choose the correct one -
(1) The, the, the
(2) x, x, the
(3) x, the, the
(4) x, a, the
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) The, the, the
> ❌ Incorrect. A proper noun like "Neil Armstrong" does not usually take an article.

> [!tip]- (2) x, x, the
> ❌ Incorrect. Ordinal numbers like "first" require the definite article "the."

> [!tip]- (3) x, the, the
> ✅ Correct. No article for the proper name; "the" is used before an ordinal number ("first"); "the" is used for unique celestial bodies ("moon").

> [!tip]- (4) x, a, the
> ❌ Incorrect. "a first man" is incorrect; ordinals take "the."

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> This question tests three rules for using articles:
> 1.  **Proper Nouns:** Generally, no article (represented by 'x') is used before a person's name like **Neil Armstrong**.
> 2.  **Ordinal Numbers:** The definite article **"the"** is always used before ordinal numbers (first, second, third, etc.) when they function as adjectives: **the first man**.
> 3.  **Unique Nouns:** The definite article **"the"** is used for things that are unique, such as celestial bodies: **the sun, the moon, the earth**.
> Combining these rules gives the correct sequence: `x, the, the`.
