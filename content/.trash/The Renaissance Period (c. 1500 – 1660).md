The word **"Renaissance"** (French for "Rebirth") refers to the period of European history that marked a gradual but profound shift away from the medieval worldview. Originating in Italy in the late 14th century with foundational figures like **Petrarch** and **Boccaccio**, its influence reached England around the early 16th century, flowering in the Elizabethan and Jacobean eras. The term was first used in its modern sense by French historian **Jules Michelet** in 1855.

This era represented a "rebirth" of interest in the classical art, literature, and learning of ancient Greece and Rome. The beginning of the English Renaissance is often dated to **1485** with the start of the Tudor Dynasty, ending the preceding "Barren Age" of literature. The period's decline can be marked by the death of King James I in 1625 or, more broadly, the start of the Restoration in 1660.

---

### Key Context and Influences

*   **Humanism**: This was the single most important intellectual movement. It shifted focus from a God-centered to a **human-centered** worldview, celebrating human potential, reason, and achievement. The ideal was the multi-talented "Renaissance Man."
    > [!note]- What is Humanism?
    > Humanism was a philosophical and educational movement that emphasized the value and agency of human beings. It promoted the study of the "humanities" – classical literature, history, rhetoric, and philosophy – believing this education could help people achieve their full potential. Key English humanists included **Sir Thomas More** and **Erasmus**. It did not reject religion but focused on making life on Earth more meaningful.

*   **The Protestant Reformation**: King Henry VIII's break from the Roman Catholic Church placed immense emphasis on the individual's direct relationship with God. This led to the monumental **King James Bible (1611)**.
    > [!note]- The King James Bible (1611)
    > Also known as the Authorized Version, this translation of the Bible was a landmark achievement of English prose. Its powerful, simple, and rhythmic language had an enormous and lasting influence on all subsequent English literature, standardizing the language for generations.

*   **The Age of Discovery**: Voyages by explorers like Sir Francis Drake broadened intellectual horizons, fueled national pride, and brought new wealth and ideas to England.

*   **Invention of the Printing Press**: Introduced to England by **William Caxton** in 1476, this technology made books more accessible, fueling literacy and the rapid spread of Humanist and Reformation ideas.

---

### Literary Characteristics of the Renaissance

*   **Focus on Humanism**: Literature explored complex human characters, their ambitions, and internal conflicts. Christopher Marlowe is often called the **"true child of the Renaissance"** for his depiction of protagonists with limitless ambition.
*   **Nationalism and Patriotism**: A surge of national pride, especially after the defeat of the **Spanish Armada in 1588**, is evident in works like Shakespeare's history plays.
    > [!note]- The Spanish Armada (1588)
    > The defeat of this massive Spanish fleet, sent to invade England, was a pivotal moment. It secured England's independence from Catholic Spain, boosted national morale, and established England as a major naval power, fueling the patriotic spirit seen in Elizabethan literature.

*   **Richness and Experimentation**: A "golden age" for the English language, with writers coining new words and experimenting with literary forms, most notably the **sonnet** and **blank verse**.
*   **Classical Influence**: Writers drew heavily on Greek and Roman myths, history, and literary forms like the epic and tragedy.
*   **Spirit of Optimism**: The era was marked by a belief in unlimited human and national potential, sometimes captured in phrases like **"love and lust for knowledge, beauty and wealth."**

## Sub-Periods of the Renaissance

### 1. The Elizabethan Age (1558–1603)

This period, under Queen Elizabeth I, is often considered the "Golden Age" of English literature and was called a **"nest of singing birds"** for its explosion of poetry. Drama was the dominant literary form.

#### Elizabethan Drama

*   **Early Plays**: The **first regular English comedy** was Nicholas Udall's ***Ralph Roister Doister***. The **first English tragedy** was ***Gorboduc*** (1561) by Thomas Norton and Thomas Sackville, notable for being the first play written in **blank verse**.

    > [!note]- What is Blank Verse?
    > Blank verse is unrhymed poetry written in **iambic pentameter**.
    > *   **Iambic Pentameter:** This is the most common meter in English poetry. An "iamb" is a two-syllable foot with the stress on the second syllable (da-DUM). "Pentameter" means there are five such feet per line (ten syllables total).
    > Blank verse became the standard meter for English drama (Marlowe, Shakespeare) and epic poetry (Milton) because its rhythm closely mimics natural English speech.

*   **The University Wits**: A group of educated, innovative playwrights who paved the way for Shakespeare.

    > [!note]- Who were the University Wits?
    > This was a group of late 16th-century playwrights and pamphleteers who were educated at the universities of Oxford and Cambridge. They transformed English drama with their sophisticated plots and powerful language. Key members included:
    > *   **Christopher Marlowe**
    > *   **Thomas Kyd**
    > *   **Robert Greene**
    > *   **John Lyly**
    > *   **George Peele**
    > *   **Thomas Nashe**

*   **Christopher Marlowe**: The greatest of the Wits. He perfected blank verse into what Ben Jonson called the **"mighty line."** His plays feature larger-than-life protagonists consumed by ambition.
    *   *Key Works*: ***Dr. Faustus*** (a scholar sells his soul for knowledge), ***Tamburlaine the Great*** (a shepherd becomes a world-conqueror), *The Jew of Malta*.

*   **Thomas Kyd**: Famous for ***The Spanish Tragedy***, which established the wildly popular genre of the **Revenge Tragedy**.

    > [!note]- What is a Revenge Tragedy?
    > A Revenge Tragedy, also called a "tragedy of blood," is a dramatic genre where the central motive is revenge for a real or imagined injury. Key elements include a secret murder, a vengeful ghost, plotting and intrigue, madness (real and feigned), and a bloody, violent climax. It was heavily influenced by the Roman playwright Seneca, and Shakespeare's *Hamlet* is the most famous example.

*   **William Shakespeare (1564-1616)**: The supreme dramatist of the age. His works include tragedies (*Hamlet, King Lear*), comedies (*As You Like It*), histories (*Henry V*), and romances/tragicomedies (*The Tempest*).

*   **Ben Jonson**: Shakespeare's contemporary, known for his satirical **Comedy of Humours**.

    > [!note]- What is a Comedy of Humours?
    > This type of comedy is based on the ancient medical theory of the "four humours" (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile). In the plays, each major character is dominated by a single, overriding trait or obsession (e.g., jealousy, greed, melancholy) which represents an imbalance of these humours. This "humour" dictates their actions and makes them a target for satire. Jonson's plays, like ***Every Man in His Humour*** and ***The Alchemist***, are prime examples.

#### Elizabethan Poetry

*   **Sir Thomas Wyatt & Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey**: They **introduced the sonnet** to English literature from Italy. Their poems were published in ***Tottel's Miscellany*** (1557), the first printed anthology of English poetry. Wyatt used the Petrarchan form, while Surrey created the English (or Shakespearean) form and also pioneered **blank verse**.

    > [!note]- What is a Sonnet?
    > A sonnet is a lyric poem of **14 lines** written in iambic pentameter with a specific rhyme scheme.
    > *   **Italian (Petrarchan) Sonnet**: Divided into an **octave** (8 lines, rhyming `abbaabba`) which presents a problem, and a **sestet** (6 lines, with a variable rhyme like `cdecde` or `cdcdcd`) which offers a resolution. The turn in thought between the two is called the *volta*.
    > *   **English (Shakespearean) Sonnet**: Divided into **three quatrains** (4 lines each) and a concluding **couplet** (2 lines). The rhyme scheme is `abab cdcd efef gg`. The final couplet often provides a witty twist or summary.
    > *   **Spenserian Sonnet**: A variation created by Edmund Spenser with a linked rhyme scheme: `abab bcbc cdcd ee`.

*   **Sir Philip Sidney**: The model Renaissance courtier. He wrote ***Astrophel and Stella***, a sonnet sequence that sparked a craze for the form, and ***The Defence of Poesie***, a major work of literary criticism.

*   **Edmund Spenser**: Called "the Poet's Poet." His masterpiece is ***The Faerie Queene***, a vast, unfinished allegorical epic. He also wrote the pastoral poem ***The Shepheardes Calender*** and the sonnet sequence ***Amoretti***.

    > [!note]- What is an Allegory?
    > An allegory is a narrative in which characters, events, and settings represent abstract ideas or moral qualities. It has a literal meaning and a deeper, symbolic meaning. Spenser's *The Faerie Queene* is a famous example, where knights represent virtues like Holiness and Temperance on a quest.

    > [!note]- What is a Pastoral Poem?
    > Pastoral poetry deals with the lives of shepherds and the simplicity of rural life, often in an idealized way. It contrasts the supposed peace and innocence of the countryside with the corruption and complexity of city or court life. Spenser's *The Shepheardes Calender* is a key example.

    > [!note]- What is the Spenserian Stanza?
    > Invented by Spenser for *The Faerie Queene*, it is a nine-line stanza. The first eight lines are in iambic pentameter, and the final line is an **Alexandrine** (a twelve-syllable line in iambic hexameter). The rhyme scheme is **ababbcbcc**.

*   **William Shakespeare**: Wrote a famous sequence of **154 sonnets**, exploring themes of love, time, and beauty, addressed to a "Fair Youth" (Sonnets 1-126) and a "Dark Lady" (Sonnets 127-152), with two final "Greek" sonnets.

#### Elizabethan Prose

*   **Sir Francis Bacon**: Known as the **"Father of the English Essay."** His essays are celebrated for their concise, memorable, and **aphoristic style**. His major prose works include *The Advancement of Learning* and *Novum Organum*.

    > [!note]- What is Aphoristic Style?
    > An aphoristic style is characterized by the use of **aphorisms**—short, pithy statements that express a general truth or observation in a clever, memorable way. Bacon's essays are packed with them, making his work highly quotable. For example: "Some books are to be tasted, others to be swallowed, and some few to be chewed and digested."

*   **John Lyly**: His prose romance ***Euphues: The Anatomy of Wit*** created a highly artificial and elegant style known as **Euphuism**.

    > [!note]- What is Euphuism?
    > Euphuism is an elaborate prose style characterized by balanced sentence structures (antithesis), excessive use of rhetorical questions, and allusions to classical mythology and natural history. It was fashionable for a brief period at court but was later parodied for its artificiality.
