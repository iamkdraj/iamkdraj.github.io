### 1. Fill in the blank with the most appropriate degree of adjective from the options given below:  
She can knit ................ than she can sew.

> [!tip]- (1) best — ❌ Incorrect  
> "Best" is the **superlative** form, used to compare more than two things.

> [!tip]- (2) good — ❌ Incorrect  
> "Good" is the **positive** form and cannot show comparison.

> [!tip]- (3) better — ✅ Correct  
> "Better" is the **comparative** form of "good", used when comparing two actions.

> [!tip]- (4) more better — ❌ Incorrect  
> "More better" is **redundant** — "better" is already comparative.

> [!info]- 📘 Related Concepts (click to expand)
> **Degrees of Comparison**:  
> - **Positive**: base form (e.g., good, fast)  
> - **Comparative**: compares two (e.g., better, faster)  
> - **Superlative**: compares more than two (e.g., best, fastest)  
>  
> ✅ Only use **"better"** when comparing two things.  
> ❌ Never say "more better" — that's incorrect.

### 2. The term ‘Existentialism’ was coined by:

> [!tip]- (1) Soren Kierkegaard — ❌ Incorrect  
> He inspired existentialist thinking but didn't coin the term.

> [!tip]- (2) Jean-Paul Sartre — ❌ Incorrect  
> Sartre popularized the term but didn’t invent it.

> [!tip]- (3) Martin Heidegger — ✅ Correct  
> Heidegger used the term "existentialism" in his 1927 work **Being and Time**.

> [!tip]- (4) Friedrich Nietzsche — ❌ Incorrect  
> Nietzsche's ideas influenced existentialism, but he never used the term.

> [!info]- 📘 Related Concepts (click to expand)
> **Existentialism** is a philosophical movement focusing on:  
> - **Individual freedom and choice**  
> - **Meaning in an indifferent or absurd world**  
> - Key figures: Kierkegaard, Heidegger, Sartre, Camus  
> - Core ideas: alienation, authenticity, angst, free will

### 3. What does the phrasal verb “got over” mean in the sentence?  
*He used to be afraid of heights but he has got over that now.*

> [!tip]- (1) catch — ❌ Incorrect  
> "Catch" usually refers to acquiring something — not relevant here.

> [!tip]- (2) escape — ❌ Incorrect  
> “Escape” implies fleeing — different from overcoming a fear.

> [!tip]- (3) to overcome or recover — ✅ Correct  
> "Got over" means to recover from or stop being affected by something negative.

> [!tip]- (4) avoid — ❌ Incorrect  
> "Avoid" means to stay away, but not to overcome.

> [!info]- 📘 Related Concepts (click to expand)
> **Phrasal Verbs with "get":**  
> - **get over** – recover from (illness, emotion)  
> - **get through** – survive or manage  
> - **get along** – be friendly  
> - **get away** – escape  
>  
> These are **idiomatic expressions** where the meaning changes from the base verb.

### 4. Fill in the blanks with suitable articles:  
................ Neil Armstrong was ................ first man on ................ moon.

> [!tip]- (1) The, the, the — ✅ Correct  
> "The first man" and "the moon" are specific and require "the". "The Neil Armstrong" works in formal reference.

> [!tip]- (2) x, x, the — ❌ Incorrect  
> Missing required articles — grammatically incomplete.

> [!tip]- (3) x, the, the — ❌ Incorrect  
> "x" before "Neil Armstrong" sounds abrupt; formal contexts allow "the".

> [!tip]- (4) x, a, the — ❌ Incorrect  
> "A first man" is invalid — use "the" with **ordinal numbers** like "first".

> [!info]- 📘 Related Concepts (click to expand)
> **Articles Usage Rules:**  
> - **Definite ("the")**: specific or unique nouns (e.g., the sun, the moon)  
> - **Indefinite ("a/an")**: general, non-specific nouns  
> - Always use "the" with:  
>   - **Ordinal numbers**: the first, the second  
>   - **Superlatives**: the best, the tallest  
>   - **Famous people/titles** (sometimes): The Neil Armstrong (ceremonial usage)
