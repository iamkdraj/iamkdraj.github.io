### 1. Fill in the blank with the most appropriate degree of adjective from the options given below:  
She can knit ................ than she can sew.

> [!tip]- (1) best
> ❌ Incorrect  
> "Best" is the **superlative** form of "good" and is used when comparing **more than two things**.  
> In this sentence, only two actions (knit and sew) are being compared, so a **comparative** form is needed.

> [!tip]- (2) good
> ❌ Incorrect  
> "Good" is the **positive** form of the adjective. It doesn't show any comparison, so it doesn't fit the sentence that requires a comparison between two actions.

> [!tip]- (3) better
> ✅ Correct  
> "Better" is the **comparative** form of "good" and is used when comparing **two things or actions**.  
> Since we're comparing her ability to knit and sew, "better" is the appropriate word.

> [!tip]- (4) more better
> ❌ Incorrect  
> "More better" is grammatically incorrect. "Better" already implies comparison, and adding "more" creates a **redundant comparative** form.

> [!info]- 📘 Related Concepts (click to expand)
> **Degrees of Comparison:**  
> - Positive: base form (e.g., good, fast)  
> - Comparative: compares two things (e.g., better, faster)  
> - Superlative: compares more than two (e.g., best, fastest)  
>  
> ❗ Never combine "more" with words that are **already comparatives** like "better", "worse", etc.

### 2. The term ‘Existentialism’ was coined by:

> [!tip]- (1) Soren Kierkegaard
> ❌ Incorrect  
> Kierkegaard is considered the **father of existentialism** for his ideas on individual existence, but he **did not coin the term**. He lived before the term came into use.

> [!tip]- (2) Jean-Paul Sartre
> ❌ Incorrect  
> Sartre helped **popularize** existentialism, especially after WWII, but he did not coin the term either. He acknowledged Heidegger’s influence.

> [!tip]- (3) Martin Heidegger
> ✅ Correct  
> Heidegger introduced "existential" terminology in a **philosophical context** in his 1927 work *Being and Time*. Though not directly coining "existentialism", his work gave birth to its use in academic philosophy.

> [!tip]- (4) Friedrich Nietzsche
> ❌ Incorrect  
> Nietzsche’s themes (absurdity, nihilism, individualism) were foundational, but he **never used the term** "existentialism".

> [!info]- 📘 Related Concepts (click to expand)
> **Existentialism** explores:  
> - **Freedom** and **responsibility of choice**  
> - The **absurdity of existence**  
> - Emphasis on **individual meaning** in a meaningless world  
>  
> Key thinkers: Kierkegaard (precursor), Heidegger (coined existential terms), Sartre, Camus  
> Related themes: **alienation, dread, authenticity, absurdism**

### 3. What does the phrasal verb “got over” mean in the sentence?  
*He used to be afraid of heights but he has got over that now.*

> [!tip]- (1) catch
> ❌ Incorrect  
> “Catch” usually refers to acquiring or contracting something (e.g., catch a cold). It doesn’t apply to emotional or psychological recovery.

> [!tip]- (2) escape
> ❌ Incorrect  
> “Escape” means to **physically or mentally flee** from something. “Got over” suggests emotional recovery, not avoidance.

> [!tip]- (3) to overcome or recover
> ✅ Correct  
> “Got over” means to **recover from** or **overcome** something emotionally or mentally.  
> In this sentence, the speaker has **conquered his fear of heights**.

> [!tip]- (4) avoid
> ❌ Incorrect  
> “Avoid” implies **keeping away from** something. That’s not the same as **recovering from** or overcoming it.

> [!info]- 📘 Related Concepts (click to expand)
> **Common “get” phrasal verbs:**  
> - **get over** → recover from (illness, fear, relationship)  
> - **get through** → survive or complete something difficult  
> - **get around** → avoid or bypass  
> - **get along (with)** → have a good relationship  
>  
> These are idiomatic and the meaning **can’t always be guessed from individual words**.

### 4. Fill in the blanks with suitable articles:  
................ Neil Armstrong was ................ first man on ................ moon.

> [!tip]- (1) The, the, the
> ✅ Correct  
> "The" is used with **specific, known, or unique** nouns.  
> - "The Neil Armstrong" can be used in ceremonial or formal contexts.  
> - "The first man" → ordinal numbers take "the"  
> - "The moon" → a unique celestial body

> [!tip]- (2) x, x, the
> ❌ Incorrect  
> Omitting articles before "Neil Armstrong" and "first man" makes the sentence sound **grammatically incomplete and unnatural**.

> [!tip]- (3) x, the, the
> ❌ Incorrect  
> Correct use of "the" for the second and third blanks, but omitting it before a proper name in this **formal/historic context** is not preferred.

> [!tip]- (4) x, a, the
> ❌ Incorrect  
> “A first man” is incorrect — **ordinal numbers (first, second)** always take **"the"**.

> [!info]- 📘 Related Concepts (click to expand)
> **Article Rules Summary:**  
> - **"the"** → used before something **specific, unique, or known**  
>   - the first man, the sun, the moon  
> - **"a"/"an"** → used before **non-specific, singular countable** nouns  
> - Use "the" with:  
>   - **superlatives** (the best)  
>   - **ordinal numbers** (the first)  
>   - **unique entities** (the Earth, the Taj Mahal)
