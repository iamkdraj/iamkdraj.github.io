### 76. Change the degree of comparison without changing the meaning -
Mary is less clever than some other girls of the class.
(1) Mary is not as clever as all girls in the class.
(2) Mary is not as clever as some other girls of the class.
(3) No other girl in the class is as clever as Mary.
(4) Mary is not as clever as most of the girls in the class.
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) Mary is not as clever as all girls...
> ❌ Incorrect. This implies she is the least clever, which is not what the original sentence says.

> [!tip]- (2) Mary is not as clever as some other girls of the class.
> ✅ Correct. "Less clever than some" is equivalent to "not as clever as some." This transformation maintains the original meaning that there are some girls who are more clever than Mary.

> [!tip]- (3) No other girl... is as clever as Mary.
> ❌ Incorrect. This would mean Mary is the cleverest, which is the opposite of the original meaning.

> [!tip]- (4) Mary is not as clever as most of the girls...
> ❌ Incorrect. The original says "some," which is not necessarily "most." This changes the meaning.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The original sentence uses a comparative degree (`less...than`) to state that Mary's cleverness is exceeded by that of some other girls. To transform this into the positive degree without changing the meaning, we can use the `not as...as` structure.
> - `A is less than B` is logically equivalent to `A is not as [great] as B`.
> - Therefore, `Mary is less clever than some other girls` means the same as `Mary is not as clever as some other girls`. This accurately reflects that some girls are cleverer, while Mary may be as clever as or cleverer than others.

After lunch he .................... generally have a short nap. (Past habit)
(1) ought to
(2) will
(3) would
(4) should
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) ought to
> ❌ Incorrect. "Ought to" expresses moral obligation.

> [!tip]- (2) will
> ❌ Incorrect. "Will" can express a present habit, but "would" is used for the past.

> [!tip]- (3) would
> ✅ Correct. "Would" is used to describe a repeated, typical, or habitual action in the past.

> [!tip]- (4) should
> ❌ Incorrect. "Should" expresses advice or duty.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> To express a **past habit** or a typical action that someone repeatedly did in the past, we use the modal verb **"would"**. It is often used in nostalgic or descriptive narratives. Another option is "used to," but "would" is provided here.
> - Example: *Every summer, we **would** go to the beach.*
> The sentence describes a general habit he had in the past, making "would" the perfect choice.

### 84. Change the degree of comparison without changing the meaning -
Mumbai is richer than most other cities in India.
(1) Very few cities in India are as rich as Mumbai.
(2) All the cities in India are as rich as Mumbai.
(3) Very little cities in India are as rich as Mumbai.
(4) Most of the cities in India are richer than Mumbai.
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) Very few cities in India are as rich as Mumbai.
> ✅ Correct. This correctly transforms the comparative degree ("richer than most other") into the positive degree ("as rich as"), maintaining the meaning that Mumbai is one of the richest, but not necessarily the absolute richest.

> [!tip]- (2) All the cities in India are as rich as Mumbai.
> ❌ Incorrect. This changes the meaning entirely.

> [!tip]- (3) Very little cities...
> ❌ Incorrect. "Little" is used for uncountable nouns. "Cities" is countable, so "few" should be used.

> [!tip]- (4) Most of the cities in India are richer than Mumbai.
> ❌ Incorrect. This is the opposite of the original meaning.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> This question tests the transformation between degrees of comparison.
> - The structure `[Subject] is [Comparative Adj.]-er than most other [Noun]` (e.g., *richer than most other cities*) implies the subject is in the top tier, but not unique.
> - The equivalent structure in the positive degree is `Very few [Noun] are as [Positive Adj.] as [Subject]` (e.g., *Very few cities are as rich as Mumbai*).
> Both sentences convey the same idea: Mumbai is exceptionally rich, and only a small number of other cities can match its wealth.

### 89. Fill in the blank with appropriate main clause -
If they doubled the price of petrol, there ....................
(1) won’t be so much traffic on the road.
(2) can’t be so much traffic on the road.
(3) shan’t be so much traffic on the road.
(4) wouldn’t be so much traffic on the road.
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) won’t be...
> ❌ Incorrect. "won't" (will not) is used in the first conditional.

> [!tip]- (2) can’t be...
> ❌ Incorrect. This expresses inability and doesn't fit the conditional structure.

> [!tip]- (3) shan’t be...
> ❌ Incorrect. "shan't" (shall not) is used in the first conditional.

> [!tip]- (4) wouldn’t be so much traffic on the road.
> ✅ Correct. The if-clause uses the simple past ("doubled"), which indicates a Second Conditional sentence. The main clause must use `would + base verb`.

> - **If-clause (Condition):** `If + Simple Past` (If they doubled...)

### 90. Choose the correct active voice of the following sentence -
We were made to work.
(1) We had to work.
(2) Work made we.
(3) He made us work.
(4) To work he made us.
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) We had to work.
> ❌ Incorrect. This expresses obligation but is not a direct active transformation.

> [!tip]- (2) Work made we.
> ❌ Incorrect. "we" is a subject pronoun and cannot be an object. It should be "us."

> [!tip]- (3) He made us work.
> ✅ Correct. This is a valid active voice sentence. The agent is assumed to be "He" (or "They," "Someone"). The object is "us." The verb structure "made us work" is correct.

> [!tip]- (4) To work he made us.
> ❌ Incorrect. The word order is unnatural.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The original sentence `We were made to work` is in the **passive voice**. The agent (the one who did the making) is not stated. To change it to the **active voice**, we must supply a subject. A generic subject like "He," "She," or "They" is appropriate.
> - Passive: `We were made...` (Object `we` + passive verb `were made`)
> - Active: `[Subject] made us...` (Subject `He` + active verb `made` + object `us`)
> A special rule applies to the verb `make`: in the active voice, it is followed by an object and a **bare infinitive** (verb without 'to').
> - Active: He made us **work**.
> - Passive: We were made **to work**.
> Option (3) correctly demonstrates this active voice structure.

### 93. Fill in the blank with the Verb that agrees with the subject in the sentence below -
The hammer and sickle............. from a tall flagpost.
(1) was flew
(2) has flown
(3) was flying
(4) has flew
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) was flew
> ❌ Incorrect. This is grammatically incorrect. "was" would be followed by "flying."

> [!tip]- (2) has flown
> ❌ Incorrect. "has" is a singular verb, but the subject "The hammer and sickle" is usually treated as plural.

> [!tip]- (3) was flying
> ✅ Correct. Although the subject appears plural, "The hammer and sickle" can be treated as a single unit or symbol. In such cases, a singular verb like "was flying" is acceptable, describing an action in progress in the past.

> [!tip]- (4) has flew
> ❌ Incorrect. The past participle of "fly" is "flown," not "flew."

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> This question on subject-verb agreement is tricky. The subject is "The hammer and sickle."
> - **Rule 1:** When two nouns are joined by "and," they usually take a plural verb.
> - **Rule 2:** When two nouns joined by "and" refer to a single idea or unit, they take a singular verb (e.g., "Bread and butter **is** my favorite breakfast.").
> Here, "The hammer and sickle" refers to a single symbol on a flag. Therefore, it can be treated as a singular unit, making the singular verb **"was flying"** (Past Continuous) a correct and logical choice to describe the action.
