### Literary Movements: Existentialism

Existentialism is primarily a philosophical movement that flourished in Europe in the mid-20th century, but its ideas have had a massive influence on literature, drama, and art. It is not a single, coherent philosophy but rather a set of shared themes and questions centered on the nature of **human existence, freedom, and responsibility** in a meaningless or absurd universe.

The movement is most closely associated with French thinkers in the wake of World WarII, but its roots go back to 19th-century philosophers like **Søren Kierkegaard** and **Friedrich Nietzsche**. The term "Existentialism" itself was famously coined by the French philosopher **Gabriel Marcel** and later adopted by **Jean-Paul Sartre**.

---

#### **Core Principles and Philosophy**

1.  **Existence Precedes Essence:**
    *   **The Principle:** This is the most famous and fundamental tenet of Existentialism, articulated by **Jean-Paul Sartre**. It means that human beings are born into the world without a predefined purpose, nature, or "essence." We are born as a blank slate.
    *   **Implication:** Unlike a tool like a paperknife, which is designed with a purpose (its essence) before it is created (its existence), humans first exist, and only then do they define who they are—their essence—through their choices and actions. We are what we do.

2.  **Freedom and Responsibility:**
    *   **The Principle:** If there is no pre-ordained plan or divine purpose, then humans are radically **free** to choose their own path and create their own values.
    *   **"Condemned to be Free":** This is Sartre's famous paradox. We are forced to be free. We cannot escape the necessity of making choices, and even choosing not to choose is itself a choice.
    *   **Responsibility:** This freedom comes with an enormous burden of responsibility. We are solely responsible for our actions and for the person we become. There are no excuses—we cannot blame God, fate, or our upbringing.

3.  **The Absurd:**
    *   **The Principle:** The term is most associated with **Albert Camus**. The Absurd arises from the conflict between humanity's desire to find meaning and purpose in life and the cold, silent, and meaningless universe that offers none.
    *   **Living with the Absurd:** The existentialist response to this is not to despair, but to embrace the Absurd. One must recognize the meaninglessness of existence and yet continue to live passionately and defiantly. Camus uses the Greek myth of **Sisyphus**—condemned to eternally roll a boulder up a hill only for it to roll back down—as the ultimate absurd hero. We must imagine Sisyphus happy because he accepts his fate and finds meaning in the struggle itself.

4.  **Anxiety, Dread (Angst), and Alienation:**
    *   **The Principle:** The realization of our total freedom and responsibility in a meaningless world naturally produces a sense of **Angst** (a German term for dread or anxiety). This is not a fear of a specific object, but a general feeling of unease about the human condition itself.
    *   **Alienation:** Existentialist literature often portrays characters who feel a profound sense of alienation—from society, from nature, and even from their own selves.

### **Key Figures and Writings**

Existentialist ideas are explored in both philosophical treatises and creative works.

#### **The Philosophers**

*   **Søren Kierkegaard (19th Century Danish Philosopher):**
    *   **Role:** Considered the "father of existentialism." A Christian existentialist, he emphasized the importance of individual choice, subjective truth, and the "leap of faith" required to find meaning in a relationship with God.
*   **Friedrich Nietzsche (19th Century German Philosopher):**
    *   **Role:** Famous for his declaration that **"God is dead,"** he argued that humanity must overcome traditional morality and create its own values. His idea of the **"Übermensch" (Overman)** is a figure who embraces life's suffering and forges his own destiny.
*   **Jean-Paul Sartre (20th Century French Philosopher and Writer):**
    *   **Role:** The central figure and popularizer of atheistic existentialism.
    *   **Key Philosophical Work:** ***Being and Nothingness***.
    *   **Key Literary Works:**
        *   ***Nausea* (Novel):** A classic existentialist novel whose protagonist is overwhelmed by the sheer, contingent existence of things.
        *   ***No Exit* (Play):** A famous play in which three characters are trapped in a room in Hell, discovering that **"Hell is other people."**
*   **Albert Camus (20th Century French Writer):**
    *   **Role:** Though he rejected the label "existentialist," his work is a cornerstone of the movement, focusing on the theme of the Absurd.
    *   **Key Philosophical Work:** ***The Myth of Sisyphus*** (an essay exploring the Absurd).
    *   **Key Literary Works:**
        *   ***The Stranger* (or *The Outsider*) (Novel):** The story of a detached man who commits a senseless murder and refuses to "play the game" of societal expectations, even to save his own life.
        *   ***The Plague* (Novel):** An allegory about the human response to suffering and the importance of solidarity in an absurd world.

#### **The Theatre of the Absurd**

This post-WWII dramatic movement is the purest theatrical expression of existentialist philosophy. Playwrights like **Samuel Beckett** and **Harold Pinter** abandoned realistic plots and characters to create plays that dramatize the illogical, meaningless, and absurd nature of human existence.
*   **Samuel Beckett's *Waiting for Godot*** is the quintessential example, where two tramps wait endlessly for someone who never arrives, passing the time with circular conversations and pointless activities to distract themselves from the void.

Existentialism's focus on the individual's struggle for meaning in a chaotic world made it a defining voice of the 20th century, deeply influencing later literature, film, and culture.
