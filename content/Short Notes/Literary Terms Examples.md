### Ballad

A narrative song or poem, often focusing on a single dramatic episode.

---

#### **Traditional Ballad (also Folk or Popular Ballad)**
*A narrative song of anonymous authorship, transmitted orally. Characterized by its dramatic, impersonal, and condensed storytelling.*

**Examples Mentioned:**
*   `"Sir Patrick Spens"` - Anonymous
*   `"Edward"` - Anonymous
*   `"Lord Randall"` - Anonymous
*   `"Child Waters"` - Anonymous

**Key Published Collections:**
*   `Reliques of Ancient English Poetry` - Editor: Thomas Percy - 1765
*   `English and Scottish Popular Ballads` - Editor: Francis J. Child - 1882–98
*   `The Traditional Tunes of the Child Ballads` - Editor: Bertrand H. Bronson - 1959–72

---

#### **Broadside Ballad**
*A ballad printed on a single sheet ("broadside"), dealing with a current event, and sung to a well-known tune.*

**Example mentioned as a modern parallel:**
*   `"The Ballad of Bonnie and Clyde"` - (e.g., Woody Guthrie, Bob Dylan, Joan Baez)

---

#### **Literary Ballad**
*A narrative poem by a known author, written in deliberate imitation of the form and spirit of the traditional ballad.*

**Examples Mentioned:**
*   `"Lenore"` - G. A. Bürger - 1774
*   `"Erlkönig"` - Goethe - 1782
*   `"Rime of the Ancient Mariner"` - Samuel Taylor Coleridge - 1798
*   `"We Are Seven"` - William Wordsworth - 1798
*   `"Proud Maisie"` - Walter Scott
*   `"La Belle Dame sans Merci"` - John Keats

**Key Published Collection:**
*   `Lyrical Ballads` - Authors: William Wordsworth & S. T. Coleridge - 1798

### Ode

A long lyric poem that is serious in subject, elevated in style, and elaborate in its stanzaic structure. It is often a public poem of praise or meditation.

---

#### **Pindaric Ode (or Regular Ode)**
*An ode that imitates the form of the Greek poet Pindar, structured in a repeating three-part pattern of strophe, antistrophe, and epode.*

**Examples Mentioned:**
*   `"To the Immortal Memory and Friendship of That Noble Pair, Sir Lucius Cary and Sir H. Morison"` - Ben Jonson - 1629
*   `"The Progress of Poesy"` - Thomas Gray - 1757

---

#### **Horatian Ode**
*Modeled on the odes of the Roman poet Horace. It is typically calmer, more meditative, and more colloquial than the Pindaric ode. It usually uses a single, repeated stanza form (homostrophic).*

**Examples Mentioned:**
*   `"An Horatian Ode upon Cromwell's Return from Ireland"` - Andrew Marvell - 1650
*   `"To Autumn"` - John Keats - 1820

---

#### **Irregular Ode (or Cowleyan Ode)**
*An ode that imitates the Pindaric style and subject matter but disregards the strict repeating stanzaic pattern. Each stanza can establish its own unique structure. This has become the most common form for the English ode.*

**Examples Mentioned:**
*   `"Ode: Intimations of Immortality"` - William Wordsworth - 1807
*   `"Anne Killigrew"` - John Dryden
*   `"Alexander's Feast"` - John Dryden
*   `"Ode to Evening"` - William Collins
*   `"Hymn to Adversity"` - Thomas Gray
*   `"Ode to Duty"` - William Wordsworth
*   `"Dejection: An Ode"` - Samuel Taylor Coleridge
*   `"Ode to the West Wind"` - Percy Bysshe Shelley
*   `"Ode to the Confederate Dead"` - Allen Tate
*   `"The Idea of Order at Key West"` - Wallace Stevens

**Widely Recognized Example:**
*   `"Ode on a Grecian Urn"` - John Keats - 1820
____
### Elegy

In modern usage, a formal and sustained lament in verse for the death of a particular person, often ending in consolation. In its broader, historical sense, it can also be a poem of somber meditation on mortality and loss.

**Examples Mentioned:**
*   `"The Wanderer"` & `"The Seafarer"` - Anonymous (Old English)
*   Elegies (love poems on mutability) - John Donne
*   `Pearl` - Anonymous (Medieval)
*   `Book of the Duchess` - Geoffrey Chaucer
*   `"Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard"` - Thomas Gray - 1757
*   `In Memoriam` - Alfred, Lord Tennyson - 1850
*   `Duino Elegies` - Rainer Maria Rilke - 1912–22
*   `"In Memory of W. B. Yeats"` - W. H. Auden - 1940

**Widely Recognized Example:**
*   `"O Captain! My Captain!"` - Walt Whitman - 1865

---

#### **Pastoral Elegy**
*A major subtype of elegy where the poet and the person being mourned are depicted as shepherds. It follows a set of classical conventions.*

**Examples Mentioned:**
*   `"Astrophel"` (on Sir Philip Sidney) - Edmund Spenser - 1595
*   `"Lycidas"` - John Milton - 1638
*   `"Adonais"` (on John Keats) - Percy Bysshe Shelley - 1821
*   `"Thyrsis"` (on A. H. Clough) - Matthew Arnold - 1866

---

#### **Related Forms**

*   **Dirge:** *A short, less formal, and often song-like lament for the dead.*
    *   `"Full Fathom Five Thy Father Lies"` - William Shakespeare
    *   `"A Song from Shakespeare's Cymbeline"` - William Collins - 1749

*   **Threnody:** *Another term for a dirge or lament.*

*   **Monody:** *An elegy or dirge presented as the utterance of a single person.*
    *   The text notes that Milton subtitled `"Lycidas"` a monody, and Arnold subtitled `"Thyrsis"` a monody.

### Sonnet

A lyric poem consisting of a single stanza of fourteen iambic pentameter lines linked by an intricate rhyme scheme.

---

#### **Italian or Petrarchan Sonnet**
*Characterized by two main parts: an eight-line **octave** which states a problem or situation, followed by a six-line **sestet** which provides a resolution.*

*   **Structure:** Octave + Sestet
*   **Rhyme Scheme:** `abbaabba cdecde` (or a variant like `cdccdc` in the sestet)
*   **Key Practitioners Mentioned:**
    *   Petrarch (originator)
    *   Sir Thomas Wyatt (introduced to England)
    *   John Milton
    *   William Wordsworth
    *   Christina Rossetti
    *   Dante Gabriel Rossetti

**Widely Recognized Example:**
*   `"On His Blindness"` - John Milton

---

#### **English or Shakespearean Sonnet**
*Characterized by three four-line **quatrains** and a concluding two-line **couplet** that often provides an epigrammatic turn of thought.*

*   **Structure:** Three Quatrains + Couplet
*   **Rhyme Scheme:** `abab cdcd efef gg`
*   **Key Practitioners Mentioned:**
    *   Earl of Surrey (developer)
    *   William Shakespeare (greatest practitioner)

**Widely Recognized Example:**
*   `Sonnet 18 ("Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?")` - William Shakespeare

##### **Spenserian Sonnet (Variant)**
*A variation of the English sonnet where the quatrains are linked by a continuing rhyme.*

*   **Rhyme Scheme:** `abab bcbc cdcd ee`
*   **Key Practitioner Mentioned:**
    *   Edmund Spenser

---

#### **Other Key Practitioners and Works Mentioned**

*   **John Donne:** `Holy Sonnets` (shifted the subject to religious themes)
*   **19th Century:**
    *   John Keats
    *   Elizabeth Barrett Browning
*   **20th Century:**
    *   W. B. Yeats
    *   Robert Frost
    *   W. H. Auden
    *   Dylan Thomas
    *   Edwin Arlington Robinson
    *   Edna St. Vincent Millay

### Epic (or Heroic Poem)

A long verse narrative on a serious subject, told in a formal and elevated style, centered on a heroic or quasi-divine figure whose actions determine the fate of a tribe, nation, or the human race.

---

#### **Traditional Epic (also Folk or Primary Epic)**
*A written version of what was originally an oral poem about a tribal or national hero during a warlike age.*

**Examples Mentioned:**
*   `Iliad` and `Odyssey` - Homer (ascribed to)
*   `Beowulf` - Anonymous (Anglo-Saxon)
*   `Chanson de Roland` - Anonymous (French)
*   `Poema del Cid` - Anonymous (Spanish)
*   `Nibelungenlied` - Anonymous (German)

---

#### **Literary Epic**
*An epic composed by an individual author in deliberate imitation of the traditional epic form.*

**Examples Mentioned:**
*   `Aeneid` - Virgil
*   `Paradise Lost` - John Milton - 1667
*   `Hyperion` (fragmentary epic) - John Keats
*   `The Four Zoas`, `Milton`, `Jerusalem` (prophetic books) - William Blake

### Burlesque

An "incongruous imitation" of the manner or subject matter of a serious literary work. It creates humor through a disparity between a high style and a low subject (high burlesque), or a low style and a high subject (low burlesque). It is often a form of satire.

---

#### **I. High Burlesque**
*Applies a high, dignified style to a low or trivial subject.*

*   **Parody:**
    *   *Definition:* Imitates the serious style of a particular work, author, or genre and applies it to a comically inappropriate subject.
    *   **Examples Mentioned:**
        *   `"The Splendid Shilling"` (parody of Milton's `Paradise Lost`) - John Phillips - 1705
        *   `Joseph Andrews` (parody of Richardson's `Pamela`) - Henry Fielding - 1742
        *   `Northanger Abbey` (parody of the gothic novel genre) - Jane Austen - 1818
        *   `A Christmas Garland` - Max Beerbohm - 1912
        *   `Cold Comfort Farm` - Stella Gibbons - 1936
        *   `Possession` (contains parodies of Victorian writing) - A. S. Byatt - 1990

*   **Mock Epic (or Mock-Heroic Poem):**
    *   *Definition:* A sustained parody that imitates the elaborate form and ceremonious style of the epic genre to narrate a trivial subject.
    *   **Examples Mentioned:**
        *   `The Rape of the Lock` - Alexander Pope - 1714
        *   `"Ode on the Death of a Favorite Cat"` (mock-heroic ode) - Thomas Gray - 1748

---

#### **II. Low Burlesque**
*Applies a low, undignified style to a high or serious subject.*

*   **Hudibrastic Poem:**
    *   *Definition:* Named after Samuel Butler's `Hudibras`, it satirizes a high subject (like chivalry or Puritanism) using doggerel verse and a colloquial style to describe mundane and humiliating misadventures.
    *   **Example Mentioned:**
        *   `Hudibras` - Samuel Butler - 1663

*   **Travesty:**
    *   *Definition:* Mocks a particular work by treating its lofty subject in a grotesquely undignified manner and style.
    *   **Examples Mentioned:**
        *   `"Across the Street and Into the Bar"` (travesty of Hemingway's `Across the River and Into the Trees`)
        *   The film `Young Frankenstein` (travesty of Shelley's novel `Frankenstein`)

---

#### **Other Forms**

*   **Lampoon:**
    *   *Definition:* A short satirical work that describes a particular person's appearance and character in a ridiculous way, often using **caricature** (exaggeration or distortion for comic effect).
    *   **Example Mentioned:**
        *   The description of Zimri (Duke of Buckingham) in `Absalom and Achitophel` - John Dryden - 1681

*   **Theatrical Burlesque:**
    *   *Definition:* Plays that mock serious dramatic forms through incongruous imitation.
    *   **Examples Mentioned:**
        *   `The Beggar's Opera` (burlesque of Italian opera) - John Gay - 1728
        *   `The Threepenny Opera` (modeled on `The Beggar's Opera`) - Bertolt Brecht & Kurt Weill - 1928
        *   Comic musical plays by Gilbert and Sullivan (contain elements of burlesque).

### Dramatic Monologue

A type of lyric poem, perfected by Robert Browning, in which a single speaker—who is patently not the poet—utters the entire poem in a specific situation at a critical moment. The primary focus is to reveal the speaker's temperament and character to the reader.

**Key Practitioners & Examples Mentioned:**
*   `"My Last Duchess"` - Robert Browning
*   `"The Bishop Orders His Tomb"` - Robert Browning
*   `"Andrea del Sarto"` - Robert Browning
*   `"Soliloquy of the Spanish Cloister"` - Robert Browning
*   `"Caliban upon Setebos"` - Robert Browning

**Widely Recognized Example:**
*   `"The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock"` - T. S. Eliot - 1915

---

#### **Related and Contrasting Forms**

*   **Soliloquy:**
    *   *Definition:* A monologue within a play where a character expresses their private thoughts aloud. (A dramatic monologue is a standalone poem, not a part of a play).

*   **Dramatic Lyric:**
    *   *Definition:* A monologue uttered in a specific dramatic situation, but where the focus is on the speaker's argument or ideas rather than the inadvertent revelation of their character.
    *   **Examples Mentioned:**
        *   `"The Canonization"` - John Donne
        *   `"The Flea"` - John Donne

*   **Contrasting Example (Not a Dramatic Monologue):**
    *   `"Tintern Abbey"` - William Wordsworth - 1798
        *   *Reason:* The speaker is identified with the poet, and the focus is on the evolution of thought and emotion, not on objective character revelation.

### Satire

The literary art of diminishing a subject by making it ridiculous and evoking attitudes of amusement, contempt, scorn, or indignation. Unlike pure comedy, satire uses laughter as a weapon against a specific target.

**General Examples Mentioned:**
*   Malvolio in `Twelfth Night` (aspects of satire) - William Shakespeare
*   `Volpone` - Ben Jonson - 1607
*   `MacFlecknoe` (personal satire against Thomas Shadwell) - John Dryden - 1682
*   `"A Satyr against Mankind"` - Earl of Rochester - 1675
*   `Gulliver's Travels` - Jonathan Swift - 1726

---

#### **I. Formal (or Direct) Satire**
*The satiric persona speaks in the first person ("I") either to the reader or to a character within the work known as the **adversarius**.*

*   **Horatian Satire:**
    *   *Tone:* Urbane, witty, and tolerant. The speaker is moved to wry amusement rather than indignation at human folly.
    *   **Examples Mentioned:**
        *   `Moral Essays` - Alexander Pope - 1731–35
        *   `"Epistle to Dr. Arbuthnot"` - Alexander Pope - 1735

*   **Juvenalian Satire:**
    *   *Tone:* Serious, moralistic, and indignant. The speaker decries vice and error with dignified and public utterance.
    *   **Examples Mentioned:**
        *   `"London"` - Samuel Johnson - 1738
        *   `"The Vanity of Human Wishes"` - Samuel Johnson - 1749

---

#### **II. Indirect Satire**
*The satire is expressed through a fictional narrative, where characters make themselves ridiculous by their actions, thoughts, and words.*

*   **Menippean Satire (also Varronian Satire or Anatomy):**
    *   *Form:* A miscellaneous prose form (often with verse interpolations) featuring a series of dialogues and debates among loquacious eccentrics, pedants, and various representatives of philosophical or professional viewpoints.
    *   **Examples Mentioned:**
        *   `Anatomy of Melancholy` - Robert Burton - 1621
        *   `Gargantua and Pantagruel` - François Rabelais - 1564
        *   `Candide` - Voltaire - 1759
        *   `Nightmare Abbey` - Thomas Love Peacock - 1818
        *   `Point Counter Point` - Aldous Huxley - 1928
        *   The `Alice in Wonderland` books - Lewis Carroll

### Irony

The use of dissembling or hiding what is actually the case to achieve special rhetorical or artistic effects. The core meaning involves a discrepancy between what is said or understood and what is actually true.

---

#### **Verbal Irony**
*A statement in which the speaker's implied meaning differs sharply from the meaning that is ostensibly expressed. The speaker intends a different, often opposite, evaluation.*

**Examples Mentioned:**
*   The Baron's reply in `The Rape of the Lock` - Alexander Pope - 1714
*   The opening sentence of `Pride and Prejudice` - Jane Austen - 1813

*   **Sarcasm:** *A crude and taunting form of verbal irony, using apparent praise for dispraise.*

---

#### **Structural Irony**
*The author introduces a structural feature (like a naive hero or a fallible narrator) that sustains a duplex meaning throughout the work, allowing the reader to understand an implied meaning that the character does not.*

**Examples Mentioned (using a naive narrator/spokesman):**
*   `"A Modest Proposal"` - Jonathan Swift - 1729
*   `"Soliloquy of the Spanish Cloister"` - Robert Browning - 1842
*   `Pale Fire` - Vladimir Nabokov - 1962

---

#### **Stable vs. Unstable Irony**

*   **Stable Irony:** *The author provides a clear, firm ground for the reader to understand the ironic subversion of the surface meaning.*

*   **Unstable Irony:** *Offers no fixed standpoint, as every position is undercut by further ironies. Common in the literature of the absurd.*
    *   **Examples Mentioned:**
        *   `Waiting for Godot` - Samuel Beckett - 1955
        *   `The Unnamable` - Samuel Beckett - 1960

---

#### **Specific Types of Irony**

*   **Socratic Irony:**
    *   *Definition:* A pose of ignorance assumed to expose the weakness of an opponent's position through questioning.
    *   **Example Mentioned:**
        *   Socrates in Plato's dialogues.

*   **Dramatic Irony:**
    *   *Definition:* A situation where the audience knows something that a character is ignorant of. The character's actions or words are thus inappropriate to the actual circumstances.
    *   **Examples Mentioned:**
        *   `Oedipus the King` - Sophocles
        *   The Malvolio scene in `Twelfth Night` - William Shakespeare

*   **Cosmic Irony (or Irony of Fate):**
    *   *Definition:* A deity or fate is represented as deliberately manipulating events to mock a protagonist's false hopes.
    *   **Example Mentioned:**
        *   `Tess of the D'Urbervilles` - Thomas Hardy - 1891

*   **Romantic Irony:**
    *   *Definition:* The author shatters the illusion of reality by revealing their role as the creator and manipulator of the story.
    *   **Examples Mentioned:**
        *   `Tristram Shandy` - Laurence Sterne - 1759–67
        *   `Don Juan` - Lord Byron - 1819–24

---

#### **Irony in New Criticism**
*Used in an extended sense as a literary criterion, referring to a poem's internal equilibrium of opposing attitudes and its awareness of complementary or opposite possibilities. This "invulnerability" to external skepticism was seen as a mark of great poetry.*
