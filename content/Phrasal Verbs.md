# Phrasal Verb: Break Down

---

## 1. Break down — (a) Stop functioning (machinery/vehicle)

**Meaning:** When a machine or vehicle stops working.

**Examples:**
- My car **broke down** on the way to the airport.
- The elevator **broke down**, so we had to take the stairs.
- The old computer **breaks down** every time I open a large file.
- Our washing machine **broke down** after just six months.
- The truck **broke down** in the middle of nowhere.

---

## 2. Break down — (b) Lose control emotionally

**Meaning:** When a person starts crying or gets overwhelmed emotionally.

**Examples:**
- She **broke down** and cried when she heard the news.
- He **broke down** during the funeral speech.
- The child **broke down** after getting scolded.
- I **broke down** when I found my lost dog injured.
- He finally **broke down** and admitted he needed help.

---

## 3. Break down — (c) Explain in detail (break something down)

**Meaning:** To separate something into smaller parts to make it easier to understand.

**Examples:**
- Let me **break down** the process for you.
- The teacher **broke down** the equation step by step.
- Can you **break down** your expenses for the trip?
- He **broke down** the concept using simple diagrams.
- The instructions **break down** each stage of the setup.

---

## 4. Break down — (d) Collapse or fail (system, discussion, organization)

**Meaning:** When a system, relationship, or negotiation fails or stops working properly.

**Examples:**
- The peace talks **broke down** after a week.
- Communication **broke down** between the teams.
- The agreement **broke down** due to a lack of trust.
- The company **broke down** under financial pressure.
- His health **broke down** from years of stress.

# Phrasal Verb: Break In

---

## 1. Break in — (a) Enter by force (often illegally)

**Meaning:** To enter a building illegally or by force, usually to steal.

**Examples:**
- Thieves **broke in** through the back window last night.
- Someone tried to **break in** while we were asleep.
- The house was **broken into** twice last month.
- Burglars **broke in** and stole all the electronics.
- He installed a new alarm system after the last **break-in**.

---

## 2. Break in — (b) Train or accustom (especially shoes, animals, new equipment)

**Meaning:** To gradually make something (like shoes or machinery) or someone (like a horse) suitable for use.

**Examples:**
- It took me two weeks to **break in** my new boots.
- He’s trying to **break in** the new interns before the project starts.
- The horse was difficult to **break in**, but eventually calmed down.
- Don’t run a marathon in shoes you haven’t **broken in**.
- You need to **break in** the new engine gently.

---

## 3. Break in — (c) Interrupt a conversation

**Meaning:** To enter a conversation abruptly, especially to add input.

**Examples:**
- I was talking when she rudely **broke in**.
- He **broke in** to correct the mistake I made.
- “Sorry to **break in**, but I have something to add,” she said.
- Please don’t **break in** while others are speaking.
- The moderator had to **break in** to keep the debate on track.

- A chunk of ice **broke off** from the glacier.

# Phrasal Verb: Break Through

---

## 1. Break through — (a) Make a way through a barrier or obstacle

- The army **broke through** enemy lines at dawn.
- We **broke through** the fog and saw the valley below.
- She finally **broke through** the wall of silence around the issue.

---

## 2. Break through — (b) Achieve success after difficulty

**Meaning:** To succeed after persistent effort, especially in difficult situations.

**Examples:**
- The singer **broke through** with her third album.
- After years of rejection, he finally **broke through** as an author.
- They **broke through** in the market with their innovative product.
- Scientists **broke through** in their search for a vaccine.
- The underdog team **broke through** and won the tournament.

---

## 3. Break through — (c) Become visible or noticeable

**Meaning:** To appear or emerge, especially after being hidden.

**Examples:**
- The sun **broke through** the clouds in the afternoon.
- Her real personality **broke through** despite her shyness.
- A smile **broke through** his otherwise stern expression.
- Light finally **broke through** the thick forest canopy.
- His emotions **broke through** during the final speech.

# Phrasal Verbs with "Bring"

---

## 1. Bring about — Cause something to happen

**Meaning:** To cause a change, situation, or result.

**Examples:**
- The new policy **brought about** major improvements in education.
- His actions **brought about** the company’s collapse.
- The reforms **brought about** significant social changes.
- That speech **brought about** a shift in public opinion.
- The crisis **brought about** tighter regulations.

---

## 2. Bring along — Take someone/something with you

**Meaning:** To carry someone or something with you when you go somewhere.

**Examples:**
- Can I **bring along** my cousin to the party?
- Don’t forget to **bring along** your ID.
- She **brought along** her guitar for the trip.
- I’ll **bring along** the documents for review.
- We **brought along** a picnic basket for the hike.

---

## 3. Bring back — Return something; revive memories or practices

**Meanings:**
### (a) Return something

**Examples:**
- Please **bring back** my book tomorrow.
- She **brought back** the dress she borrowed.
- Can you **bring back** some snacks?
- I need to **bring back** the keys to the office.
- He **brought back** my phone after I left it.

### (b) Cause to remember or revive

**Examples:**
- That song **brings back** memories of college.
- The photo **brought back** his childhood days.
- Smells can easily **bring back** the past.
- The documentary **brought back** emotions I’d buried.
- Visiting that place **brought back** so many memories.

---

## 4. Bring down — Reduce; cause to fall

**Meanings:**
### (a) Cause to fall (physically, emotionally, or politically)

**Examples:**
- The scandal **brought down** the minister.
- Inflation is **bringing down** the economy.
- The protesters aimed to **bring down** the regime.
- That mistake **brought down** his reputation.
- Negative thoughts can **bring you down**.

### (b) Reduce (price, temperature, level)

- We need to **bring down** the costs.
- They **brought down** the prices during the sale.
- This medicine will **bring down** your fever.
- We’re working to **bring down** emissions.
