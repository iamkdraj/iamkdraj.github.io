### 1. Fill in the blank with the most appropriate degree of adjective from the options given below - 
She can knit................than she can sew.
(1) best
(2) good
(3) better
(4) more better
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) best
> ❌ Incorrect. "Best" is the superlative degree, used for comparing three or more things.

> [!tip]- (2) good
> ❌ Incorrect. "Good" is the positive degree and is not used for comparisons.

> [!tip]- (3) better
> ✅ Correct. The word "than" indicates a comparison between two activities (knitting and sewing), which requires the comparative degree "better."

> [!tip]- (4) more better
> ❌ Incorrect. This is a double comparative. "Better" is already the comparative form of "good."

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The sentence makes a direct comparison between two skills using the word **"than"**. This requires the **comparative degree** of the adjective. The adjective "good" is irregular, and its degrees are:
> - **Positive:** good
> - **Comparative:** better
> - **Superlative:** best
> Therefore, "better" is the correct form for comparing two items. "More better" is always incorrect.

### 2. The term ‘Existentialism’ was coined by -
(1) Soren Kierkegaard
(2) Jean-Paul Sartre
(3) Martin Heidegger
(4) Friedrich Nietzsche
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) Soren Kierkegaard
> ❌ Incorrect. Kierkegaard is considered the "father of existentialism," but he did not use the term himself.

> [!tip]- (2) Jean-Paul Sartre
> ✅ Correct. While the French philosopher Gabriel Marcel first applied the term to Sartre in 1945, Jean-Paul Sartre was the one who accepted, defined, and popularized it, making him most associated with its coining in the public sphere.

> [!tip]- (3) Martin Heidegger
> ❌ Incorrect. Heidegger was a key figure in existentialism but rejected the label and did not coin the term.

> [!tip]- (4) Friedrich Nietzsche
> ❌ Incorrect. Nietzsche was a major influence on existentialism but wrote before the term was created.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The term **‘Existentialism’** has a complex origin. The philosopher **Søren Kierkegaard** is regarded as the first existentialist philosopher, but the term itself emerged much later. It was the French Catholic philosopher **Gabriel Marcel** who first used the term in the mid-1940s in a colloquy, applying it to **Jean-Paul Sartre**. Sartre then embraced the label in his lecture and essay "Existentialism Is a Humanism" (1946), defining it for a wide audience. Because Sartre defined and popularized the term, he is commonly credited with its "coining" in a functional sense.

### 4. Fill in the blanks with suitable Articles if any in the blanks below; put ‘x’ where ‘No-Article’ is needed -
.................... Neil Armstrong was .................... first man on .................... moon.
From the options given below, choose the correct one -
(1) The, the, the
(2) x, x, the
(3) x, the, the
(4) x, a, the
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) The, the, the
> ❌ Incorrect. A proper noun like "Neil Armstrong" does not usually take an article.

> [!tip]- (2) x, x, the
> ❌ Incorrect. Ordinal numbers like "first" require the definite article "the."

> [!tip]- (3) x, the, the
> ✅ Correct. No article for the proper name; "the" is used before an ordinal number ("first"); "the" is used for unique celestial bodies ("moon").

> [!tip]- (4) x, a, the
> ❌ Incorrect. "a first man" is incorrect; ordinals take "the."

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> This question tests three rules for using articles:
> 1.  **Proper Nouns:** Generally, no article (represented by 'x') is used before a person's name like **Neil Armstrong**.
> 2.  **Ordinal Numbers:** The definite article **"the"** is always used before ordinal numbers (first, second, third, etc.) when they function as adjectives: **the first man**.
> 3.  **Unique Nouns:** The definite article **"the"** is used for things that are unique, such as celestial bodies: **the sun, the moon, the earth**.
> Combining these rules gives the correct sequence: `x, the, the`.

### 8. Express the meaning of the following sentence in a negative form. The meaning must remain the same.
Alfred was the best King that ever reigned in England.
(1) Never was seen as Alfred was as King.
(2) No other King is as good as Alfred ever reigned in England.
(3) No other King as good as Alfred ever reigned in England.
(4) No other King was as good as Alfred in England that never reigned in England.
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) Never was seen...
> ❌ Incorrect. This sentence is grammatically awkward and incomplete.

> [!tip]- (2) ...is as good as...
> ❌ Incorrect. The tense is wrong. The original sentence is in the past ("was"), so the new sentence must also be in the past.

> [!tip]- (3) No other King as good as Alfred ever reigned in England.
> ✅ Correct. This sentence correctly changes the superlative "best" to the positive "as good as" within the negative construction "No other...". It maintains the past tense and the original meaning. *Note: A verb "was" is implied or omitted after "King", i.e., "No other King (was) as good...". This is a common elliptical construction.*

> [!tip]- (4) ...that never reigned...
> ❌ Incorrect. This contains a logical contradiction ("reigned" and "never reigned").

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> To transform a sentence from the **superlative degree** to a negative sentence in the **positive degree** without changing the meaning, we use the structure `No other + [noun] + verb + as + [positive adjective] + as + [subject]`.
> - Superlative: Alfred was the **best** King.
> - Negative/Positive: **No other** King was **as good as** Alfred.
> Option (3) correctly uses this structure. The verb "was" is elliptically omitted after "King," which is acceptable in this construction, making it the best choice among the flawed options.

### 13. Which is not an essential feature of satire?
(1) It is intended to display tragedy
(2) It is inspired by a passion for reform
(3) It is inspired by a personal grievance
(4) It holds the mirror-up to nature
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) It is intended to display tragedy
> ✅ Correct. This is **not a feature** of satire. Satire uses humor, irony, and ridicule, while tragedy evokes pity and fear. They are distinct literary modes.

> [!tip]- (2) It is inspired by a passion for reform
> ❌ Incorrect. This is a key feature of Horatian and Juvenalian satire, which aim to correct vice and folly.

> [!tip]- (3) It is inspired by a personal grievance
> ❌ Incorrect. Personal attack is a common (though not universal) element of satire.

> [!tip]- (4) It holds the mirror-up to nature
> ❌ Incorrect. Satire does this by exposing the follies and vices of society, thus reflecting a version of reality.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> **Satire** is a literary genre that uses wit, irony, sarcasm, and ridicule to expose, criticize, or mock human folly, vice, or stupidity. Its ultimate aim is often to bring about reform. **Tragedy**, on the other hand, is a genre that deals with suffering, downfall, and serious themes, aiming to evoke catharsis (pity and fear) in the audience. The two genres have fundamentally different purposes and tones. A satire is comedic in its approach, whereas a tragedy is serious.

### 16. Fill in the blanks with a correct option -
We .................... go provided the weather .................... fine.
(1) shall, is
(2) ought to, has to be
(3) might, is being
(4) have to, was to being
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) shall, is
> ✅ Correct. This is a First Conditional sentence. The main clause uses the simple future ("shall go"), and the conditional clause introduced by "provided" uses the simple present ("is").

> [!tip]- (2) ought to, has to be
> ❌ Incorrect. This combination of modals doesn't fit the conditional structure.

> [!tip]- (3) might, is being
> ❌ Incorrect. "is being" (present continuous) is not the correct tense for the condition.

> [!tip]- (4) have to, was to being
> ❌ Incorrect. This is grammatically incorrect.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> This sentence is a **First Conditional**, which describes a real possibility in the future. The conjunction **"provided"** (or "providing") functions just like "if." The structure is:
> - **Main Clause (Result):** Simple Future (`will/shall + verb`)
> - **Conditional Clause:** Simple Present
> The sentence `We **shall go** provided the weather **is** fine` perfectly fits this pattern. It means if the weather is fine, we will go.

### 17. Change into Indirect form of Narration -
The Captain said, “It gives me great pleasure to be here this evening.”
(1) The Captain told that it had given me great pleasure to be there that evening.
(2) The Captain said that it has given him great pleasure to be here that evening.
(3) The Captain stated to me that it had given me great pleasure to be here the following evening.
(4) The Captain said that it gave him great pleasure to be there that evening.
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) ...had given me...
> ❌ Incorrect. "me" should change to "him," and the tense backshift from simple present is to simple past, not past perfect.

> [!tip]- (2) ...has given him... to be here...
> ❌ Incorrect. The tense has not been backshifted correctly, and "here" should change to "there."

> [!tip]- (3) ...stated to me... had given me...
> ❌ Incorrect. The pronouns and tense are incorrect.

> [!tip]- (4) The Captain said that it gave him great pleasure to be there that evening.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> When changing from **Direct to Indirect Speech**, several changes are necessary if the reporting verb is in the past ("said"):
> - **Tense Backshift:** Simple Present (`gives`) becomes Simple Past (`gave`).
> - **Pronoun Change:** First-person pronouns (`me`) change to reflect the speaker (`him`).
> - **Adverbial Change:** Adverbs of place and time change perspective (`here` becomes `there`, `this` becomes `that`).

### 150. The Principle of Gradation in teaching English language does not refer to -
(1) Organising the matter so that language learning becomes easy.
(2) Sequencing the material according to difficulty level.
(3) Grouping of material on various basis.
(4) Teaching complex sentence structures first and gradually introducing simpler ones.
(5) Question not attempted

> [!tip]- (1) Organising the matter...
> ❌ Incorrect. This is the overall purpose of gradation.

> [!tip]- (2) Sequencing the material according to difficulty level.
> ❌ Incorrect. This is the definition of sequencing, a key part of gradation.

> [!tip]- (3) Grouping of material on various basis.
> ❌ Incorrect. This is the other key part of gradation.

> [!tip]- (4) Teaching complex sentence structures first and gradually introducing simpler ones.
> ✅ Correct. This is **not** what gradation refers to; in fact, it is the opposite. The principle of gradation involves moving from simple to complex, not the other way around.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The **Principle of Gradation** in syllabus design means arranging the selected language material in a logical and learner-friendly order. A teacher would introduce simple sentence structures (like S+V+O) before moving on to more complex ones (like sentences with multiple subordinate clauses). Teaching complex structures first would violate this core principle and make learning much more difficult.
