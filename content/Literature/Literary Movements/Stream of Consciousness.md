Stream of Consciousness is not a movement in itself, but a highly influential **narrative technique** that became a hallmark of the **Modernist movement** in the early 20th century. It is a mode of writing that seeks to portray an individual's point of view by giving the written equivalent of their thought processes.

The term was coined by the American psychologist and philosopher **William James** in his *Principles of Psychology* (1890). He described the human mind not as a logical chain of thoughts but as a "river" or a "stream"—a continuous, flowing, and often chaotic jumble of sensations, memories, and associations. The term was first applied in a literary context by the novelist May Sinclair when discussing the work of Dorothy Richardson.

---

### Core Aims and Techniques

*   **Aim**: To give the reader direct, unmediated access to a character's mind, capturing their pre-speech level of consciousness—what one source calls "the actual experience of thinking... in all its chaos and distress."
*   **Subjective Reality**: The technique rejects an objective, all-knowing narrator. Instead, reality is filtered entirely through the mind of a character, reflecting how the world *feels* to that individual.
*   **Non-Linear Narrative**: The human mind jumps between past, present, and future. Stream of consciousness writing mimics this non-linear process, with the plot often advancing through psychological association rather than external events.
*   **Disrupted Syntax**: To mimic the speed and chaos of thought, writers often abandon conventional grammar and punctuation, using long, run-on sentences or fragmented phrases.
*   **Association with the Novel**: While poetry and drama use techniques like the dramatic monologue and soliloquy to reveal a character's mind, stream of consciousness is primarily associated with the novel.

> [!note]- Stream of Consciousness vs. Interior Monologue
> The two terms are often used interchangeably, but there is a useful distinction.
> *   **Stream of Consciousness** is the broad, inclusive term for all methods used to represent the full flow of a character's mind.
> *   **Interior Monologue** is a specific type of stream of consciousness. It presents a character's thoughts as if they were speaking to themselves. It can be more organized and logical than the most radical forms of stream of consciousness.

### Key Figures and Foundational Texts

*   **James Joyce (1882-1941)**: The undisputed master and most radical innovator of the technique.
    *   *Key Work:* ***Ulysses*** (1922). The final chapter, "Penelope," is a famous example—a single, unpunctuated, 40-page sentence representing Molly Bloom's thoughts as she lies in bed.
*   **Virginia Woolf (1882-1941)**: Developed a more lyrical and poetic version of the technique, focusing on the subtle shifts in perception and the inner lives of her characters.
    *   *Key Works:* ***Mrs. Dalloway*** (1925), ***To the Lighthouse*** (1927), ***The Waves*** (1931).
*   **William Faulkner (1897-1962)**: An American novelist who adapted the technique to explore the consciousness of his characters in the American South.
    *   *Key Work:* ***The Sound and the Fury*** (1929), which presents its story from the perspectives of three different brothers, including one with severe developmental disabilities, whose section is a chaotic flow of sensory impressions.
*   **Dorothy Richardson (1873-1957)**: A British writer considered a pioneer of the technique. Her multi-volume novel sequence, ***Pilgrimage***, was one of the first to be written almost entirely from within a single character's consciousness.
