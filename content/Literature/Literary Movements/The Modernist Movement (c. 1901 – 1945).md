Modernism was a revolutionary international movement in the arts, dominating the first half of the 20th century. It was driven by a conscious and radical break with the traditions of the 19th century. Modernist writers felt that Victorian forms and conventions were inadequate to represent the reality of a world changed by war, industrialization, and new psychological theories. Their central call to action, famously articulated by **Ezra Pound**, was to **"Make It New."**

The period roughly begins with the death of Queen Victoria in 1901 and ends with the conclusion of World War II in 1945.

---

### Context and Influences

*   **World War I (1914-1918)**: This was the most shattering event of the era. The unprecedented scale of death destroyed the old Victorian belief in progress and a stable world, leaving a generation with a deep sense of **disillusionment, trauma, and fragmentation**.
*   **New Psychological Theories**: The work of **Sigmund Freud** on the **unconscious mind** suggested that human identity was not stable or rational but driven by hidden desires and irrational impulses.
*   **New Philosophical and Social Ideas**: Thinkers like **Friedrich Nietzsche**, who famously declared that **"God is dead,"** captured the widespread loss of religious faith and the collapse of traditional moral certainties. The theories of **Karl Marx** highlighted the alienation of the individual in a capitalist society.
*   **The "Mythical Method"**: In a world that seemed chaotic, many Modernists (like **T.S. Eliot** and **James Joyce**) turned to ancient myths, often drawn from **Sir James Frazer's *The Golden Bough***, to find a sense of structure and universal significance for their fragmented modern narratives.

---

### Key Characteristics of Modernist Literature

*   **Experimentation with Form**: This is the hallmark of Modernism. Writers broke traditional narrative structures, chronology, and grammar, using fragmentation and collage. This includes a number of related movements, such as **Imagism, Symbolism, Futurism, Vorticism, and Expressionism**.
*   **Focus on Individual Consciousness**: Influenced by Freud and the philosopher **William James**, Modernists shifted their focus from the external, social world to the inner world of the individual mind. Reality was seen as subjective and was often represented through **Stream of Consciousness**.
    > [!note]- What is Stream of Consciousness?
    > This phrase, coined by psychologist **William James**, describes a narrative method that undertakes to reproduce the full spectrum and continuous flow of a character’s mental process. It presents a jumbled mix of conscious and half-conscious thoughts, memories, sensory perceptions, and random associations, often ignoring linear logic and conventional grammar.

*   **Themes of Alienation and Fragmentation**: Modernist works are filled with a sense of loneliness, spiritual emptiness, and the breakdown of communication. The modern city is frequently portrayed as a place of anonymity and decay.
*   **Intellectualism and Difficulty**: Modernist literature is often deliberately complex, dense, and challenging, packed with allusions and symbols that demand an active and educated reader. It often included elements of **Symbolism** and **Imagism**.
    > [!note]- What is Imagism?
    > Imagism was an early 20th-century movement in poetry, championed by Ezra Pound. It favored precision of imagery and clear, sharp language. Its principles included: (1) Direct treatment of the "thing." (2) To use absolutely no word that does not contribute to the presentation. (3) To compose in the sequence of the musical phrase, not in sequence of a metronome.

### Key Figures and Writings

#### Modernist Poetry
*   **T. S. Eliot**: The most influential poet and critic.
    *   *Key Works:* ***The Waste Land*** (the definitive Modernist poem), "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock."
*   **W. B. Yeats**: An Irish poet whose later work is profoundly Modernist.
    *   *Key Works:* "The Second Coming," "Sailing to Byzantium."
*   **Ezra Pound**: A central figure and leader of the Imagist movement.
    *   *Key Works:* *The Cantos*, "In a Station of the Metro."
*   **The War Poets**: A group who wrote about the brutal realities of WWI.
    *   *Key Figures*: **Wilfred Owen**, **Siegfried Sassoon**.

#### The Modernist Novel
*   **James Joyce**: Master of the Stream of Consciousness technique.
    *   *Key Works:* ***Ulysses*** (1922), ***A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man***.
*   **Virginia Woolf**: A key innovator of the psychological novel.
    *   *Key Works:* ***Mrs. Dalloway***, ***To the Lighthouse***.
*   **D. H. Lawrence**: Explored the psychological impact of industrialization on human relationships.
    *   *Key Works:* ***Sons and Lovers***, *Women in Love*.
*   **Joseph Conrad**: An early Modernist whose narrative techniques influenced later writers.
    *   *Key Work:* ***Heart of Darkness***.
*   **E. M. Forster**: Critiqued the social conventions of Edwardian England.
    *   *Key Work:* ***A Passage to India***.

#### Modernist Drama
*   **George Bernard Shaw**: Famous for his "plays of ideas" which debated social issues.
    *   *Key Works*: *Pygmalion*, *Man and Superman*.
*   **The Irish Literary Revival**: A movement to create a distinctly Irish literature.
    *   *Key Figures*: **W.B. Yeats**, **J.M. Synge** (*Riders to the Sea*).

**Summary for Quick Revision:**

Modernism (c. 1901-1945) was a revolutionary international art movement that broke with 19th-century traditions, driven by Ezra Pound's "Make It New." Influenced by WWI's disillusionment, new psychological theories (Freud), philosophical ideas (Nietzsche, Marx), and the "mythical method" (Frazer), Modernist literature features formal experimentation, focus on individual consciousness (Stream of Consciousness), and themes of alienation and fragmentation. It is often intellectual and challenging, incorporating Symbolism and Imagism. Key poets include T.S. Eliot (*The Waste Land*), W.B. Yeats, Ezra Pound, and the War Poets. Major novelists are James Joyce (*Ulysses*), Virginia Woolf, D.H. Lawrence, Joseph Conrad, and E.M. Forster. Modernist drama saw figures like George Bernard Shaw and the Irish Literary Revival.
