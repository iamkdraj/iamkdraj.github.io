## Definition
**Utilitarianism** is a moral and political philosophy that judges actions based on their consequences, specifically whether they produce "the greatest good for the greatest number." It emphasizes practical outcomes and measurable benefits over abstract principles or individual rights.

## Definition
**Utilitarianism** is a moral and political philosophy that judges actions based on their consequences, specifically whether they produce "the greatest good for the greatest number." It emphasizes practical outcomes and measurable benefits over abstract principles or individual rights.

## Core Principles

### Fundamental Concepts
- **Greatest Good**: Actions should maximize overall happiness or well-being
- **Greatest Number**: Priority given to majority benefit
- **Consequentialism**: Morality determined by outcomes, not intentions
- **Hedonistic Calculus**: Quantitative approach to measuring pleasure and pain
- **Practical Ethics**: Focus on real-world applications and results

### Key Elements
- **Utility**: Usefulness or benefit as the measure of value
- **Pleasure and Pain**: Happiness as presence of pleasure, absence of pain
- **Quantification**: Attempt to measure and compare different benefits
- **Impartiality**: Equal consideration of all individuals' interests
- **Efficiency**: Preference for solutions that maximize overall benefit

## Historical Development

### Founding Figures
- **Jeremy Bentham (1748-1832)**: Founder of classical utilitarianism
- **James Mill (1773-1836)**: Political economist and utilitarian theorist
- **John Stuart Mill (1806-1873)**: Refined and developed utilitarian theory
- **Henry Sidgwick (1838-1900)**: Systematic philosophical treatment

### Bentham's Contributions
- **Felicific Calculus**: Mathematical approach to measuring pleasure
- **Legal Reform**: Application to law and criminal justice
- **Prison Reform**: Design of the Panopticon prison
- **Social Policy**: Influence on government policy and administration

## Victorian Context

### Social and Political Influence
- **Government Policy**: Influenced Victorian social reforms
- **Economic Theory**: Connection to laissez-faire capitalism
- **Legal Reform**: Impact on criminal justice and civil law
- **Education**: Influence on educational theory and practice
- **Public Health**: Application to sanitation and medical policy

### Practical Applications
- **Poor Law Reform**: Workhouse system based on utilitarian principles
- **Factory Legislation**: Balancing worker welfare with economic efficiency
- **Educational Policy**: Emphasis on practical, useful knowledge
- **Urban Planning**: City design for maximum public benefit
- **Colonial Administration**: Application to imperial governance

## Literary Responses

### Critical Portrayals
- **Charles Dickens**: Satirical attacks on utilitarian coldness
  - *Hard Times*: Mr. Gradgrind as embodiment of utilitarian education
  - *A Christmas Carol*: Scrooge's initial utilitarian worldview
- **Thomas Carlyle**: Criticism of mechanical, calculating approach
- **John Ruskin**: Opposition to utilitarian aesthetics and values

### Dickens' Critique
- **Dehumanization**: Reduction of humans to statistical units
- **Emotional Poverty**: Neglect of imagination, feeling, and individuality
- **Social Mechanics**: Treatment of society as machine rather than organism
- **Educational Criticism**: Attack on fact-based, utilitarian education
- **Moral Inadequacy**: Failure to account for human complexity

## Philosophical Variations

### Classical Utilitarianism
- **Act Utilitarianism**: Judge each individual action by its consequences
- **Rule Utilitarianism**: Follow rules that generally produce best outcomes
- **Hedonistic Utilitarianism**: Focus on pleasure and pain
- **Preference Utilitarianism**: Emphasis on satisfying preferences
- **Negative Utilitarianism**: Priority on reducing suffering over increasing happiness

### Mill's Refinements
- **Qualitative Hedonism**: Some pleasures are higher than others
- **Individual Liberty**: Balance between individual freedom and social good
- **Competent Judges**: Those who have experienced different pleasures can judge their worth
- **Harm Principle**: Society can only restrict individual liberty to prevent harm to others

## Criticisms and Limitations

### Philosophical Objections
- **Measurement Problems**: Difficulty in quantifying and comparing different goods
- **Justice Issues**: Potential conflict between utility and individual rights
- **Demandingness**: Excessive moral requirements on individuals
- **Moral Intuitions**: Conflicts with common moral beliefs
- **Cultural Relativism**: Assumption of universal values and preferences

### Literary and Cultural Critique
- **Mechanistic View**: Reduction of human experience to calculation
- **Emotional Impoverishment**: Neglect of emotion, imagination, and beauty
- **Individual Dignity**: Potential sacrifice of individuals for collective benefit
- **Cultural Values**: Conflict with traditional moral and religious values
- **Artistic Criticism**: Opposition from aesthetic and romantic movements

## Legacy and Influence

### Political Impact
- **Liberal Democracy**: Influence on democratic theory and practice
- **Welfare State**: Contribution to social welfare policies
- **Cost-Benefit Analysis**: Modern policy evaluation methods
- **Public Administration**: Rational approach to government
- **International Relations**: Application to global governance

### Intellectual Influence
- **Economics**: Foundation for welfare economics and policy analysis
- **Ethics**: Continued debate in moral philosophy
- **Political Theory**: Influence on liberal and democratic thought
- **Social Science**: Quantitative approaches to social problems
- **Public Policy**: Evidence-based policy making
