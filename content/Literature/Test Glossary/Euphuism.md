# Euphuism

## Definition
**Euphuism** is a highly artificial and elaborate prose style that became fashionable in Elizabethan England. It is characterized by ornate language, balanced sentences, extensive use of alliteration, and frequent classical and mythological allusions.

## Origin
- **Creator**: John Lyly (1553-1606)
- **Source Work**: *Euphues: The Anatomy of Wit* (1578)
- **Name Origin**: From the protagonist "Euphues" (Greek for "well-endowed by nature")
- **Period**: Popular in the 1580s-1590s

## Key Characteristics

### Stylistic Features
- **Balanced Sentences**: Carefully structured parallel phrases
- **Antithesis**: Contrasting ideas placed side by side
- **Alliteration**: Repetitive use of initial consonant sounds
- **Rhetorical Questions**: Frequent use for dramatic effect
- **Elaborate Metaphors**: Complex and extended comparisons

### Content Elements
- **Classical Allusions**: References to Greek and Roman mythology
- **Natural History**: References to plants, animals, and natural phenomena
- **Moral Instruction**: Didactic purposes beneath ornate surface
- **Courtly Themes**: Love, friendship, and moral conduct

## Example Characteristics
```
"As the finest ruby staineth the colour of the rest that be in place, 
so the pretiest wench maketh the other seem to be odious and loathsome."
```

## Historical Context
- **Court Fashion**: Became the fashionable style at Elizabeth I's court
- **Educational Background**: Reflected humanist education and classical learning
- **Audience**: Aimed at educated, aristocratic readers
- **Influence**: Shaped prose style of the late 16th century

## Literary Significance
- **Prose Development**: Marked an important stage in English prose evolution
- **Artificial Elegance**: Represented the height of ornate writing
- **Reaction Against**: Later writers reacted against its artificiality
- **Influence on Drama**: Affected dialogue in early Elizabethan plays

## Impact and Reception
- **Popularity**: Briefly became the height of fashion
- **Decline**: Fell out of favor by the 1590s
- **Criticism**: Later criticized for excessive ornamentation
- **Literary History**: Seen as important stepping stone to mature English prose

## Later Reactions
- **Shakespeare**: Parodied euphuistic style in some characters
- **Bacon**: Developed a more direct, aphoristic style in reaction
- **Natural Style**: Led to preference for more natural prose
