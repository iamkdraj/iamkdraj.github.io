# Negative Capability

**Negative Capability** is a term coined by the Romantic poet **John Keats** in a letter to his brothers George and Tom on December 21, 1817. It describes the capacity of a great artist to exist in "uncertainties, Mysteries, doubts, without any irritable reaching after fact & reason."

## Core Meaning

*   **Embracing Ambiguity**: It is the ability to tolerate and even thrive in states of uncertainty, paradox, and unresolved questions, rather than seeking immediate answers or logical conclusions.
*   **Openness to Experience**: It implies a receptivity to the complexities of life and art, allowing for multiple perspectives and contradictory truths to coexist.
*   **Subordination of Ego**: For Keats, it meant that the poet should be able to empty himself of his own personality and biases to fully inhabit the subject of his poetry, allowing the truth of the subject to emerge without the imposition of the poet's own intellect or moralizing.
*   **Contrast with Coleridge**: Keats contrasted this with what he saw as Coleridge's tendency to seek definitive answers and systems, which Keats believed could stifle poetic imagination.

## Significance

Negative Capability is a central concept in Keats's aesthetic philosophy and has had a lasting impact on literary criticism. It champions a particular kind of artistic temperament that values intuition, imaginative empathy, and the acceptance of mystery over rigid rationality or dogmatic certainty. It is often seen as a key aspect of the [[Romanticism Movement]]'s emphasis on emotion and imagination.
