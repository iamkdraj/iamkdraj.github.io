# Metaphysical Conceit

A **metaphysical conceit** is a complex, elaborate, and often surprising comparison between two highly dissimilar things. It is a characteristic feature of the poetry of the [[The Metaphysical Poets (c. 1600–1660)]].

## Characteristics

*   **Intellectual and Analytical**: Unlike simpler similes or metaphors, a metaphysical conceit is often intellectual and analytical, exploring the implications of the comparison in detail.
*   **Unconventional Comparisons**: It draws comparisons between things that seem to have no obvious connection, often combining abstract ideas with concrete images.
*   **Extended and Elaborate**: The comparison is typically extended throughout a poem or a significant portion of it, with the poet exploring various facets of the analogy.
*   **Surprising and Witty**: The unexpected nature of the comparison often creates a sense of surprise and intellectual wit.

## Examples

*   **John Donne's "A Valediction: Forbidding Mourning"**: Donne compares two lovers to the two legs of a compass. As one leg moves away, the other leans after it, and when it comes home, the other stands firm. This elaborate comparison illustrates the spiritual and unbreakable bond between the lovers, even when physically separated.

    > If they be two, they are two so
    > As stiff twin compasses are two;
    > Thy soul, the fixed foot, makes no show
    > To move, but doth, if the other do.

    > And though it in the center sit,
    > Yet when the other far doth roam,
    > It leans and hearkens after it,
    > And grows erect, as that comes home.

*   **Andrew Marvell's "To His Coy Mistress"**: Marvell compares the lovers' slow courtship to the vastness of time and space, and then their passionate embrace to a cannonball.

## Significance

Metaphysical conceits allowed poets to explore complex ideas and emotions in a highly intellectual and original way. They challenged conventional poetic imagery and encouraged readers to think deeply about the connections between seemingly disparate concepts.
