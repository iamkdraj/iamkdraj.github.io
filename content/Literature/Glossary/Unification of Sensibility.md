# Unification of Sensibility

**Unification of Sensibility** is a critical term coined by the 20th-century poet and critic **T.S. Eliot** in his essay "The Metaphysical Poets" (1921). Eliot used this concept to praise the Metaphysical Poets for their ability to integrate thought and feeling, intellect and emotion, into a unified poetic experience.

## Core Meaning

*   **Integration of Thought and Feeling**: For Eliot, a poet with a "unified sensibility" could "feel his thought as immediately as the odour of a rose." This means that intellectual concepts were not abstract but were experienced sensuously and emotionally.
*   **Absence of a "Dissociation of Sensibility"**: Eliot argued that a "dissociation of sensibility" occurred in English poetry after the 17th century (roughly after the Metaphysical Poets and Andrew Marvell). This dissociation led to a split between thought and feeling, where poets either became too intellectual and abstract, or too emotional and sentimental, without integrating the two.
*   **Intellectual and Emotional Cohesion**: The term describes a state where the poet's intellect and emotions work in harmony, allowing for complex ideas to be conveyed with vivid sensory and emotional impact.

## Significance

Eliot's concept of the unification of sensibility was instrumental in the 20th-century re-evaluation and revival of interest in the [[Metaphysical Poets]]. It provided a framework for understanding their unique blend of intellectual rigor and emotional intensity. It also served as a critique of later poetic traditions that Eliot felt had lost this vital integration.
