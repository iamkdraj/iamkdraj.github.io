# Unification of Sensibility

## Definition
**Unification of Sensibility** is T.S. Eliot's concept describing the Metaphysical poets' ability to combine thought and feeling seamlessly. For them, intellectual ideas were experienced as immediately and intensely as physical sensations.

## Origin
- **Coined by**: T.S. Eliot (20th century)
- **Context**: His praise of 17th-century Metaphysical poets
- **Purpose**: Contrast with later poetry that separated thinking from feeling

## Key Concept
- **No Separation**: Thought and emotion are unified
- **Immediate Experience**: Ideas felt as directly as physical sensations
- **Eliot's Quote**: They could "feel their thought as immediately as the odour of a rose"
- **Intellectual Emotion**: Thinking was an emotional experience

## Examples
- **Donne's Love Poetry**: Passionate arguments about relationships
- **Herbert's Religious Verse**: Emotional theological reasoning
- **Marvell's Wit**: Feeling expressed through intellectual conceits

## Contrast with Later Poetry
- **18th-19th Century**: Thought and feeling often separated
- **Romantic Poetry**: Emphasized emotion over intellect
- **Metaphysical**: Seamlessly blended both elements

## Significance
- Eliot used this to revive interest in Metaphysical poets
- Influenced modern poetic theory
- Challenged Romantic poetry's dominance
