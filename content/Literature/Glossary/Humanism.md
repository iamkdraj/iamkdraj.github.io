# Humanism

**Humanism** is a philosophical and ethical stance that emphasizes the value and agency of human beings, individually and collectively, and generally prefers critical thinking and evidence (rationalism, empiricism) over established doctrine or faith (fideism). It is a broad term with various historical and contemporary manifestations.

## Key Principles

*   **Emphasis on Human Value**: Humanism places human beings at the center of its concerns, valuing human dignity, potential, and achievements.
*   **Reason and Evidence**: It promotes the use of reason, critical inquiry, and empirical evidence as the primary means of understanding the world and solving problems.
*   **Ethics and Morality**: Humanism often focuses on secular ethics and morality, deriving values from human experience, reason, and compassion rather than divine command.
*   **Individual Flourishing**: It seeks to promote the well-being, happiness, and self-realization of individuals.

## Historical Context

While humanist ideas can be traced back to ancient Greek philosophy, the term is most commonly associated with the **Renaissance Humanism** movement that emerged in Europe during the [[The Renaissance Period (c. 1500–1660)]]. Renaissance humanists rediscovered and promoted classical Greek and Roman literature, philosophy, and art, shifting intellectual focus from theological concerns to human affairs.

## Modern Humanism

In modern times, humanism often refers to **secular humanism**, which is a non-religious worldview that affirms the ability and responsibility of human beings to lead ethical lives of personal fulfillment that aspire to the greater good of humanity.
