# Intertextuality

**Intertextuality** is the shaping of a text's meaning by another text. It is the idea that all texts are interconnected and that no text exists in isolation. Every new text is a mosaic of references, allusions, and transformations of previous texts.

## Core Concepts

*   **Texts in Dialogue**: Intertextuality suggests that texts are constantly in dialogue with each other, consciously or unconsciously borrowing from, responding to, or transforming earlier works.
*   **Reader's Role**: The reader's understanding of a text is often enriched by their knowledge of other texts. Recognizing intertextual links can deepen appreciation and reveal new layers of meaning.
*   **Beyond Direct Quotation**: Intertextuality is more than just direct quotation or allusion. It encompasses a wide range of relationships, including parody, pastiche, translation, adaptation, and even unconscious influence.

## Significance in Literature

Intertextuality is a fundamental aspect of how literature functions, but it became a particularly prominent concept in literary theory, especially with the rise of [[Post-Modernism]].

*   **[[Post-Modernism]]**: Postmodern writers often deliberately employ intertextuality to highlight the constructed nature of reality and the idea that originality is an illusion. They play with existing narratives and forms to create new meanings.

## Examples

*   **James Joyce's *Ulysses***: This novel is a prime example, as it is structured as a modern retelling of Homer's *Odyssey*, with countless allusions to classical literature, history, and philosophy.
*   **T.S. Eliot's *The Waste Land***: This poem is famously intertextual, weaving together fragments from various myths, literary works, and cultural references to create a sense of fragmentation and disillusionment.
*   **Fairy Tale Retellings**: Many contemporary novels and films retell classic fairy tales, drawing on the original stories while offering new interpretations.
