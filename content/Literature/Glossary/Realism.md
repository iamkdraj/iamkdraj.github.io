## Definition
**Realism** is a literary movement and technique that aims to represent life as it actually is, without idealization or romantic subjectivity. It seeks to create a detailed, accurate, and panoramic portrait of society, focusing on ordinary people and everyday situations.

## Definition
**Realism** is a literary movement and technique that aims to represent life as it actually is, without idealization or romantic subjectivity. It seeks to create a detailed, accurate, and panoramic portrait of society, focusing on ordinary people and everyday situations.

## Historical Context
- **Period**: Mid-19th century (1850s-1900)
- **Peak**: Victorian era in England, especially 1850-1890
- **Origins**: Reaction against Romanticism's emphasis on emotion and imagination
- **Social Context**: Response to industrialization, urbanization, and social change

## Key Characteristics

### Literary Techniques
- **Detailed Description**: Precise, factual portrayal of settings and characters
- **Objective Narration**: Neutral, unbiased narrative voice
- **Contemporary Settings**: Stories set in the present day
- **Ordinary Characters**: Focus on middle-class and working-class protagonists
- **Social Issues**: Attention to current social problems and conditions

### Subject Matter
- **Everyday Life**: Common experiences and situations
- **Social Problems**: Poverty, industrialization, urban conditions
- **Middle-Class Life**: Domestic situations and social interactions
- **Work and Labor**: Industrial conditions and economic struggles
- **Family Relations**: Realistic portrayals of family dynamics

## Victorian Realism

### Dominance of the Novel
- **Primary Form**: The novel became the dominant literary form
- **Accessibility**: Serial publication made novels available to wide audiences
- **Social Function**: Literature as tool for social commentary and reform
- **Moral Purpose**: Combination of entertainment with instruction

### Major Practitioners
- **Charles Dickens**: Social realism with memorable characters
- **George Eliot**: Psychological realism and moral complexity
- **Elizabeth Gaskell**: Industrial novels and social conditions
- **Anthony Trollope**: Realistic portrayals of contemporary society
- **Thomas Hardy**: Rural realism and social determinism

## Techniques and Methods

### Narrative Strategies
- **Omniscient Narration**: All-knowing narrator providing comprehensive view
- **Multiple Perspectives**: Various viewpoints on social issues
- **Detailed Characterization**: Complex, believable characters
- **Causal Relationships**: Logical connections between events and consequences

### Descriptive Elements
- **Physical Environment**: Detailed settings reflecting social conditions
- **Dialect and Speech**: Authentic representation of different social classes
- **Historical Accuracy**: Careful attention to contemporary details
- **Documentary Quality**: Journalistic approach to social observation

## Social Function
- **Reform Literature**: Exposure of social ills to promote change
- **Middle-Class Values**: Reflection of bourgeois concerns and morality
- **Educational Purpose**: Instruction about social conditions and human nature
- **Democratic Literature**: Accessible to broad reading public

## Influence and Legacy
- **Modern Novel**: Foundation for later fictional techniques
- **Social Consciousness**: Established literature's role in social critique
- **Psychological Development**: Led to psychological realism
- **International Movement**: Spread to other national literatures
