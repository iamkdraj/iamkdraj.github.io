# Free Verse

**Free verse** is a form of poetry that does not use consistent meter patterns, rhyme, or any other musical pattern. It thus tends to follow the rhythm of natural speech.

## Characteristics

*   **No fixed meter**: Unlike traditional poetry, free verse does not adhere to a strict metrical pattern (e.g., iambic pentameter).
*   **No consistent rhyme scheme**: While individual lines or stanzas might rhyme, there is no overarching, predictable rhyme scheme.
*   **Emphasis on natural speech rhythms**: The lines are often broken to reflect the natural pauses and cadences of spoken language.
*   **Flexibility**: Poets have greater freedom in line length, stanza breaks, and the use of imagery and sound devices.

## Examples

*   **Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass***: Whitman is often credited with popularizing free verse in English poetry. His long, flowing lines and expansive themes are characteristic of the form.
*   **T.S. Eliot's "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock"**: While it contains some traditional elements, much of the poem is written in free verse, reflecting the fragmented consciousness of the speaker.
*   **William Carlos Williams's "The Red Wheelbarrow"**: A short, iconic example of free verse that focuses on concrete imagery.

## Significance

Free verse became particularly prominent during the [[The Modern Period (c. 1901 – 1945)]] as poets sought to break away from traditional forms and create a more direct and expressive language. It allowed for greater experimentation and a more realistic portrayal of modern life and thought.
