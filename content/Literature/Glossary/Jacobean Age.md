# Jacobean Age

## Definition
The **Jacobean Age** (1603-1625) is the period of English literature during the reign of King James I (*Jacobus* in Latin). It continues the Renaissance but shifts from Elizabethan optimism to a darker, more cynical and psychologically complex literary mood.

## Key Characteristics
- **Darker Tone**: More pessimistic than Elizabethan literature
- **Disillusionment**: Loss of faith in human nature and progress
- **Psychological Complexity**: Deeper exploration of character motivation
- **Political Intrigue**: Fascination with plots, betrayal, conspiracy
- **Satirical Edge**: Critical view of society and human folly

## Historical Context
- **Political Tensions**: Growing conflict between crown and Parliament
- **Religious Divisions**: Anglican, Puritan, and Catholic conflicts
- **Gunpowder Plot (1605)**: Created atmosphere of paranoia
- **Social Change**: Urban growth and changing class structures

## Major Literary Forms
- **Revenge Tragedy**: Dark, violent plays (Webster, Middleton)
- **City Comedy**: Satirical comedies about urban life (Jonson)
- **Metaphysical Poetry**: Intellectual, witty verse (Donne)
- **Tragicomedy**: Hybrid dramatic genre (Beaumont & Fletcher)

## Key Authors
- **William Shakespeare**: Greatest tragedies (*Hamlet*, *Othello*, *King Lear*, *Macbeth*)
- **John Webster**: *The White Devil*, *The Duchess of Malfi*
- **Ben Jonson**: *Volpone*, *The Alchemist*
- **John Donne**: Metaphysical poetry and sermons

## Important Works
- **King James Bible (1611)**: Monumental English prose achievement
- **Francis Bacon**: Essays and scientific works
- **Robert Burton**: *The Anatomy of Melancholy*
