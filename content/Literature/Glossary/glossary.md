# Literary Glossary

## Literary Periods

### The Commonwealth Period (or The Interregnum)
- **Definition**: A brief but significant 11-year period (1649-1660) in English history when England was a republic, without a monarch, following the execution of King Charles I. It ended with the Restoration of the monarchy under King Charles II.
- **Key Characteristics**: Dominated by Puritan rule, strict censorship, closing of theaters, and a focus on religious and political prose.

### The Jacobean Age
- **Definition**: Named after the reign of King James I (1603-1625), this period is a continuation of the Elizabethan Renaissance but with a darker, more cynical, and psychologically complex mood in literature.
- **Key Characteristics**: Growing political and religious instability, a mood of disillusionment, and the rise of revenge tragedy and city comedy.

### The Metaphysical Poets
- **Definition**: A group of 17th-century English poets who shared a common intellectual, witty, and philosophical style, consciously breaking from Elizabethan conventions. The term was popularized by Dr. Samuel Johnson.
- **Key Characteristics**: Intellectual and argumentative style, use of colloquial language, wit, paradox, puns, and especially the metaphysical conceit.

### The Modern Period (Modernism)
- **Definition**: A revolutionary international movement in the arts (roughly 1901-1945) characterized by a conscious and radical break with 19th-century traditions, driven by a sense of disillusionment and fragmentation after World War I.
- **Key Characteristics**: Experimentation with form, focus on individual consciousness, stream of consciousness, themes of alienation and fragmentation, and use of allusion, symbolism, and imagism.

### The Neo-Classical Period (Age of Reason, Augustan Age)
- **Definition**: A period in English literature (1660-1798) that admired and imitated the literature of classical Greece and Rome, valuing order, reason, restraint, logic, and correctness. It began with the Restoration and ended with the start of the Romantic Age.
- **Key Characteristics**: Emphasis on reason and logic, didacticism, satire, value for order and clarity, and the heroic couplet.

### The Post-Modern Period (Post-Modernism)
- **Definition**: A critical, skeptical, and often playful reaction against Modernism, generally beginning after World War II (c. 1945 – Present). It accepts chaos and fragmentation as reality and often plays with the idea of meaninglessness.
- **Key Characteristics**: Rejection of grand narratives, pastiche, irony, playfulness, black humor, intertextuality, metafiction, hyperreality, simulacra, and magic realism.

### The Renaissance Period
- **Definition**: Meaning "Rebirth," this period (c. 1500-1660 in England) marked a profound shift from the medieval worldview, characterized by a renewed interest in classical art, literature, and learning of ancient Greece and Rome.
- **Key Characteristics**: Humanism, Protestant Reformation, Age of Discovery, invention of the printing press, focus on human potential, nationalism, richness and experimentation in language, and classical influence.

### The Romantic Period (Romanticism, Romantic Revival)
- **Definition**: A profound artistic, literary, and intellectual movement (1798-1837) that originated as a revolt against the Neo-Classical emphasis on reason, order, and society, championing emotion, imagination, and the individual.
- **Key Characteristics**: Emphasis on emotion and imagination, glorification of nature, focus on the individual and subjectivity, interest in the supernatural and the exotic, and use of simple, "common" language.

### The Victorian Period
- **Definition**: Named after Queen Victoria's reign (1837-1901), an era of immense and rapid change, marked by the Industrial Revolution, imperial expansion, and a duality of confidence and doubt, faith and skepticism.
- **Key Characteristics**: Realism and the novel as dominant forms, moral purpose and didacticism, the Victorian Compromise (surface respectability vs. hidden realities), idealism vs. doubt, and exploration of social issues.

## Literary Movements

### Aestheticism
- **Definition**: A European arts movement that emphasized aesthetic values over moral or social themes in literature and art, advocating "Art for Art's Sake" as a reaction against utilitarian views of art and Victorian moral earnestness.
- **Key Characteristics**: Pursuit of pure aesthetic experience, stylistic innovation, rejection of moral didacticism, celebration of artifice, and interest in exotic and decadent themes.

### Existentialism
- **Definition**: A philosophical movement emphasizing individual existence, freedom, and responsibility. It posits that humans are free and responsible for their own choices and actions, and that there is no inherent meaning in life beyond what individuals create.
- **Key Characteristics**: Focus on individual freedom and responsibility, the absurdity of existence, anxiety, and the search for meaning in a meaningless world.

### Stream of Consciousness
- **Definition**: A narrative method that attempts to reproduce the full spectrum and continuous flow of a character's mental process, presenting a jumbled mix of conscious and half-conscious thoughts, memories, sensory perceptions, and random associations, often ignoring linear logic and conventional grammar.
- **Key Characteristics**: Mimics the natural flow of thought, often non-linear, uses free association, and provides deep psychological insight into characters.

### The Gothic Movement
- **Definition**: A literary genre that emerged in the mid-18th century, characterized by elements of fear, horror, death, and gloom, often set in dark, mysterious, and isolated locations like old castles or abbeys. It also incorporates romantic elements such as nature, individuality, and high emotion.
- **Key Characteristics**: Atmospheric settings (castles, ruins), supernatural elements (ghosts, curses), psychological horror (madness, obsession), mysterious characters, and dark themes (death, decay, forbidden knowledge).

### The Modernist Movement
- **Definition**: See "The Modern Period" above.

### The Post-Modernism Movement
- **Definition**: See "The Post-Modern Period" above.

### The Pre-Raphaelite Movement
- **Definition**: A group of English painters, poets, and critics founded in 1848 who sought to return to the detailed, intense, and complex composition of Quattrocento Italian art (before Raphael), rejecting the academic art of their time.
- **Key Characteristics**: Return to nature (direct observation), medieval revival, moral purpose in art, truth to nature, detailed naturalism, bright colors, and symbolic content.

### The Realism Movement
- **Definition**: A literary movement that attempts to represent life accurately and truthfully, focusing on ordinary people in everyday situations. It emphasizes detailed observation of social conditions and psychological motivation.
- **Key Characteristics**: Detailed social observation, psychological realism, contemporary settings, focus on middle and working-class subjects, and attention to social issues.

### The Romanticism Movement
- **Definition**: See "The Romantic Period" above.

### The Symbolist Movement
- **Definition**: An artistic and literary movement that originated in late 19th-century France and Belgium, emphasizing the expression of ideas, emotions, and states of mind indirectly, through symbols and suggestive imagery rather than direct description.
- **Key Characteristics**: Suggestion over description, use of symbols to evoke meaning, focus on inner experience, musicality of language, and often a sense of mystery or the ineffable.

## Key Literary Terms & Concepts

### Interregnum
- **Definition**: A period when normal government is suspended, especially between successive reigns or regimes. In English history, it specifically refers to the Commonwealth Period (1649-1660).

### Puritanism
- **Definition**: A religious reform movement in the 16th and 17th centuries that sought to "purify" the Church of England of its Roman Catholic practices, emphasizing strict moral and religious observance.
- **Literary Impact**: Led to censorship, closing of theaters, and a focus on religious and political prose during the Commonwealth Period.

### Puritan Rule
- **Definition**: The period during the Commonwealth (1649-1660) when England was governed by Puritan leaders, most notably Oliver Cromwell, who imposed strict moral codes and religious observance.

### The Closing of the Theatres
- **Definition**: The act by the Puritan-led Parliament in 1642 that officially closed all public theaters in England, viewing them as dens of sin and moral corruption. This significantly impacted English drama until the Restoration in 1660.

### Censorship and Religious Focus
- **Definition**: During the Commonwealth Period, the Puritan government imposed strict control over published materials, leading to a literary output dominated by religious and political arguments, suppressing secular literature.

### Dominance of Prose
- **Definition**: A characteristic of the Commonwealth Period where, due to the closing of theaters and censorship, prose (sermons, pamphlets, political arguments) became the most important literary genre.

### Decline of Lyric Poetry
- **Definition**: During the Commonwealth Period, the overall output of light-hearted, secular lyric poetry declined due to the somber Puritan mood and focus on serious religious and political themes.

### Moral and Religious Seriousness
- **Definition**: A pervasive tone in literature during the Commonwealth Period, reflecting the Puritan values of sobriety, strict morality, and preoccupation with religious and political themes.

### Blank Verse
- **Definition**: Unrhymed poetry written in **iambic pentameter**. An "iamb" is a two-syllable foot with the stress on the second syllable (da-DUM). "Pentameter" means there are five such feet per line (ten syllables total). It became standard for English drama and epic poetry.

### Literary Epic
- **Definition**: A long narrative poem on a serious subject, told in a formal and elevated style, centered on a heroic figure whose actions determine the fate of a nation or people. Unlike folk epics, they are deliberately crafted by a single author (e.g., Milton's *Paradise Lost*).

### Invocation of the Muse
- **Definition**: A convention in classical epic poetry where the poet formally calls upon a divine spirit or muse for inspiration and guidance in telling the story.

### In Medias Res
- **Definition**: A Latin phrase meaning "in the middle of things." It refers to the literary technique of beginning a narrative not at the beginning of the story, but at some crucial point in the middle, with earlier events revealed through flashbacks or exposition.

### Grand Scale
- **Definition**: A characteristic of epic poetry where the setting is vast and encompasses large geographical areas or even cosmic realms (Heaven, Hell, Earth).

### Supernatural Machinery
- **Definition**: The active involvement of gods, angels, demons, or other supernatural beings in the plot of an epic poem or other narrative, influencing events and characters.

### Monody
- **Definition**: A lament uttered by a single person, often in a poem or song. It expresses deep sorrow or mourning.

### Freedom of the press
- **Definition**: The principle that the communication and expression through various media, including printed materials, should be free from censorship or governmental interference. John Milton's *Areopagitica* is a famous argument for this.

### State of nature
- **Definition**: A concept in political philosophy, particularly associated with Thomas Hobbes, describing a hypothetical condition of humanity before the establishment of organized society or government. Hobbes famously described life in this state as "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."

### Mood of Disillusionment
- **Definition**: A pervasive feeling of disappointment resulting from the discovery that something is not as good as one believed it to be. This mood characterized the Jacobean Age, replacing the optimism of the Elizabethan era.

### Jacobean Drama
- **Definition**: The dramatic works produced during the reign of King James I (1603-1625), which continued Elizabethan traditions but often with a darker, more cynical, and sensational tone, including revenge tragedies and city comedies.

### Revenge Tragedy
- **Definition**: A dramatic genre popular in the Elizabethan and Jacobean eras, characterized by a protagonist who seeks revenge for a real or imagined wrong, often involving secret murders, ghosts, madness, and a bloody climax.

### City Comedy
- **Definition**: A satirical dramatic genre popular in the Jacobean era that focused on the lives, vices, and follies of the urban middle class in London, often exposing greed, hypocrisy, and social climbing.

### Comedy of Manners
- **Definition**: A satirical dramatic genre, particularly popular during the Restoration period, that satirizes the artificial manners, amoral intrigues, and witty dialogue (*repartee*) of the upper class.

### Comedy of Humours
- **Definition**: A type of comedy, perfected by Ben Jonson, based on the ancient medical theory of the "four humours." Characters are dominated by a single, overriding trait or obsession (a "humour") that dictates their actions and makes them a target for satire.

### Tragicomedy
- **Definition**: A dramatic genre that blends elements of both tragedy and comedy, often featuring serious or potentially tragic situations that ultimately resolve happily, avoiding a tragic outcome.

### Intellectual & Witty
- **Definition**: Key characteristics of Metaphysical poetry, referring to its complex, subtle thought, ingenious arguments, and clever use of language and ideas.

### Colloquial Language
- **Definition**: Informal, everyday language used in speech, as opposed to formal or literary language. Metaphysical poets often used this to create a sense of directness and realism.

### Conceits
- **Definition**: A clever and often extended metaphor that establishes a surprising parallel between two very dissimilar things. It is a hallmark of Metaphysical poetry.

### Paradox
- **Definition**: A statement that appears self-contradictory but contains a deeper truth or valid reasoning. Metaphysical poets frequently used paradox to explore complex ideas.

### Pun
- **Definition**: A play on words that exploits multiple meanings of a word or words that sound alike but have different meanings, often for humorous or rhetorical effect.

### Metaphysical Conceit
- **Definition**: A highly clever, complex, and often startling extended metaphor that makes a comparison between two things that seem to have absolutely no connection, developed and argued logically throughout the poem. (e.g., Donne comparing lovers' souls to a compass).

### Aphoristic Style
- **Definition**: A concise, memorable, and often witty style of writing that expresses a general truth or principle. Francis Bacon is known for his aphoristic essays.

### The Caroline Age
- **Definition**: Named for the reign of King Charles I (1625–1649), this era was dominated by escalating political and religious conflicts that led to the English Civil War. Literary trends of the Jacobean age continued.

### Cavalier Poets
- **Definition**: A group of 17th-century poets who supported King Charles I. Influenced by Ben Jonson, their work is characterized by elegance, clarity, and secular themes, often celebrating beauty, love, and the *carpe diem* philosophy.

### The Enlightenment
- **Definition**: An intellectual and philosophical movement in the 18th century that emphasized reason, logic, and scientific observation as the primary tools for understanding the world, challenging traditional authority and promoting individual liberty. Also known as the Age of Reason.

### Heroic Couplet
- **Definition**: A pair of rhyming lines in **iambic pentameter**, typically expressing a complete, self-contained thought. It was the dominant poetic form of the Neo-Classical period, perfected by Dryden and Pope.

### The Restoration Period
- **Definition**: The period in English history and literature (1660-1700) that began with the restoration of the monarchy under King Charles II, marking a reaction against Puritan severity and a return to more secular and witty forms of literature, especially drama.

### Heroic Tragedy
- **Definition**: A form of tragedy popular during the Restoration period, often written in heroic couplets, featuring larger-than-life heroes and themes of epic love and honor.

### Poet Laureate
- **Definition**: An official poet appointed by a monarch or government, expected to compose poems for special events or occasions. John Dryden was England's first Poet Laureate.

### Mock-epic
- **Definition**: A satirical literary form that treats a trivial subject in the elevated, formal style of an epic poem, often using epic conventions (e.g., invocation of the muse, grand battles) for humorous or satirical effect (e.g., Pope's *The Rape of the Lock*).

### The Rise of the Novel
- **Definition**: The emergence and development of the novel as a dominant literary genre in the 18th century, driven by a growing middle-class readership and a demand for realistic and relatable stories.

### Epistolary novel
- **Definition**: A novel written as a series of documents, typically letters, but sometimes diary entries, newspaper clippings, or other forms. Samuel Richardson was a pioneer of this form.

### Periodical Essays
- **Definition**: A new prose form that emerged in the 18th century, published in magazines and journals, offering commentary on social manners, morals, and contemporary issues (e.g., *The Tatler*, *The Spectator*).

### The Age of Sensibility (Age of Johnson, Age of Transition)
- **Definition**: A transitional period (1745-1798) between the Neo-Classical and Romantic eras. While reason still dominated, there was a growing emphasis on emotion, sentiment, and individual experience, paving the way for Romanticism.

### Pre-Romantic Trends
- **Definition**: Literary developments in the late 18th century that showed a shift away from strict Neo-Classical ideals and foreshadowed the Romantic movement, emphasizing emotion, nature, and the individual (e.g., The Graveyard School, Gothic Novel).

### The Graveyard School
- **Definition**: A group of 18th-century poets who wrote melancholic, reflective poems about death, mortality, and rural life, often set in graveyards. Their somber tone was a departure from Neo-Classical wit and a precursor to Romanticism.

### The Gothic Novel
- **Definition**: A genre of fiction that combines elements of horror and romance, often featuring mysterious castles, supernatural occurrences, psychological torment, and dark, suspenseful plots. It emerged in the mid-18th century and thrived during the Romantic period.

### Industrial Revolution
- **Definition**: The rapid development of industry in Britain (late 18th to mid-19th century) characterized by the introduction of machinery, steam power, and factory systems, leading to massive social, economic, and demographic changes.
- **Literary Impact**: Inspired themes of social injustice, urban poverty, and the dehumanizing effects of industrialization in Victorian literature.

### Disillusionment, trauma, and fragmentation
- **Definition**: Key themes in Modernist literature, reflecting the profound psychological impact of World War I and the collapse of traditional beliefs, leading to a sense of loss, brokenness, and spiritual emptiness.

### Unconscious mind
- **Definition**: A concept popularized by Sigmund Freud, referring to the part of the mind that contains thoughts, memories, and desires of which a person is not aware but which can influence behavior.
- **Literary Impact**: Led to a focus on exploring the inner, psychological lives of characters in Modernist literature.

### Mythical Method
- **Definition**: A literary technique, identified by T.S. Eliot and used by Modernist writers like James Joyce, that involves imposing a sense of order and universal significance onto the chaotic modern world by structuring contemporary narratives on ancient myths or archetypal patterns.

### Experimentation with Form
- **Definition**: A hallmark of Modernism, where writers deliberately broke traditional narrative structures, chronology, and grammar, using fragmentation, collage, and innovative techniques to reflect the chaotic nature of modern life.

### Fragmentation
- **Definition**: A literary technique, particularly in Modernism, where a narrative or text is broken into disconnected or incomplete parts, reflecting a sense of disorder, brokenness, or the collapse of traditional coherence.

### Collage
- **Definition**: A literary technique, often associated with Modernism, where disparate elements (e.g., different voices, texts, images) are juxtaposed or combined to create a new, often fragmented, whole.

### Imagism
- **Definition**: A poetic movement (early 20th century) led by Ezra Pound, advocating for poetry that uses precise, concrete images to convey meaning directly, without unnecessary words or abstract generalizations.

### Symbolism
- **Definition**: See "The Symbolist Movement" above.

### Futurism
- **Definition**: An artistic and social movement (early 20th century, originating in Italy) that emphasized speed, technology, youth, and violence, and objects such as the car, the airplane, and the industrial city. It rejected traditional forms and celebrated modernity.

### Vorticism
- **Definition**: A short-lived British literary and artistic movement (early 20th century), influenced by Cubism and Futurism, characterized by bold lines, sharp angles, and a dynamic, machine-like aesthetic, often expressing the energy of modern urban life.

### Focus on Individual Consciousness
- **Definition**: A key characteristic of Modernist literature, influenced by Freud and William James, where the narrative shifts from external events to the inner world of the individual mind, exploring subjective perceptions and psychological states.

### Interior Monologue
- **Definition**: A narrative technique that presents the thoughts, feelings, and associations of a character directly, as if they were speaking to themselves, often without the intervention of a narrator. It is closely related to stream of consciousness.

### Themes of Alienation and Fragmentation
- **Definition**: Recurring motifs in Modernist literature, reflecting a sense of loneliness, spiritual emptiness, the breakdown of communication, and the feeling of being isolated outsiders in a meaningless or chaotic world.

### Allusion
- **Definition**: An indirect or passing reference to a person, place, thing, or idea of historical, cultural, literary, or political significance. Modernist literature often uses complex allusions.

### Post-War World
- **Definition**: Refers to the period following World War II (after 1945), which profoundly influenced Post-Modern thought and literature, leading to skepticism about progress and grand narratives.

### Grand Narratives (Metanarratives)
- **Definition**: Overarching, comprehensive explanations or theories about history, society, or human experience (e.g., religion, capitalism, Marxism) that claim universal validity. Post-Modernism is characterized by a rejection of these.

### Incredulity toward metanarratives
- **Definition**: A core idea of Post-Modernism, famously articulated by Jean-François Lyotard, signifying a deep skepticism and distrust of any single, totalizing theory that claims to offer a universal explanation for history and human experience.

### Pastiche
- **Definition**: A literary or artistic work that imitates the style or character of the work of one or more other artists, often playfully or as a tribute, without satirical intent (unlike parody). Post-Modernism frequently uses pastiche.

### Irony, Playfulness, and Black Humor
- **Definition**: Common characteristics of Post-Modern literature, where serious or horrific subjects are often treated with a detached, darkly humorous, and playful tone, reflecting an acceptance of absurdity rather than despair.

### Intertextuality
- **Definition**: The idea that any text is a web of references to other texts. Post-Modern works are often self-consciously filled with allusions, quotations, and borrowings from other literature, films, and pop culture, creating a dense, self-referential world.

### Metafiction
- **Definition**: Fiction that is self-consciously about fiction, deliberately reminding the reader that they are reading a work of art. The author might directly address the reader, or characters might realize they are in a novel.

### Historiographic Metafiction
- **Definition**: A term coined by Linda Hutcheon, referring to novels that use metafictional techniques to challenge the idea of historical truth, suggesting that history itself is just another form of storytelling or interpretation.

### Hyperreality
- **Definition**: A concept, particularly from Jean Baudrillard, describing a condition in which what is real and what is simulation are indistinguishable, or where the simulation of reality is more real than reality itself.

### Simulacra
- **Definition**: Copies or representations that have no original or reality. In Baudrillard's theory, we live in a world of simulacra where the distinction between the real and the simulated has collapsed.

### Magic Realism
- **Definition**: A literary style, particularly popular in post-colonial literature, that blends fantastical or magical elements into a realistic setting, treating the impossible as perfectly normal and integrating it seamlessly into the everyday.

### Deconstruction
- **Definition**: A method of critical analysis, developed by Jacques Derrida, that aims to show that language is inherently unstable and that texts are full of internal contradictions, paradoxes, and hidden assumptions, thus "dismantling" the claim that a text can have a single, stable, authoritative meaning.

### The Theatre of the Absurd
- **Definition**: A dramatic movement (mid-20th century) that expresses the belief that human existence is meaningless and that communication is impossible. Absurdist plays reject realistic plots and characterization to portray a world that is illogical and nonsensical.

### Humanism
- **Definition**: A philosophical and educational movement during the Renaissance that shifted focus from a God-centered to a human-centered worldview, celebrating human potential, reason, and achievement, and promoting the study of classical literature, history, rhetoric, and philosophy.

### Protestant Reformation
- **Definition**: A major 16th-century European movement aimed at reforming the beliefs and practices of the Roman Catholic Church, leading to the establishment of Protestant churches.
- **Literary Impact**: Emphasized the individual's direct relationship with God, fueling religious and political controversy, and leading to works like the King James Bible.

### Age of Discovery
- **Definition**: A period from the early 15th to the early 17th century, during which European ships traveled around the world to search for new trading routes and partners.
- **Literary Impact**: Broadened intellectual horizons, fueled national pride, and brought new wealth and ideas to England during the Renaissance.

### Printing Press
- **Definition**: A device for applying pressure to an inked surface resting upon a print medium, thereby transferring the ink. Its invention by Gutenberg and introduction to England by William Caxton revolutionized book production.
- **Literary Impact**: Made books more accessible, fueled literacy, and rapidly spread Humanist and Reformation ideas during the Renaissance.

### Elizabethan Age
- **Definition**: The period of English history and literature during the reign of Queen Elizabeth I (1558–1603), often considered the "Golden Age" of English literature, especially for its drama and lyric poetry.

### Nest of singing birds
- **Definition**: A phrase used to describe the Elizabethan Age, referring to its explosion of lyric poetry and the flourishing of numerous talented poets.

### University Wits
- **Definition**: A group of late 16th-century playwrights and pamphleteers (e.g., Marlowe, Kyd) who were educated at Oxford or Cambridge and transformed English drama with their sophisticated plots and powerful language, paving the way for Shakespeare.

### Sonnet
- **Definition**: A lyric poem of **14 lines** written in iambic pentameter with a specific rhyme scheme. It originated in Italy and was introduced to England during the Renaissance.

### Italian (Petrarchan) Sonnet
- **Definition**: A sonnet form consisting of an **octave** (8 lines, `abbaabba`) that presents a problem or question, and a **sestet** (6 lines, `cdecde` or `cdcdcd`) that offers a resolution or answer. The shift in thought is called the *volta*.

### Octave
- **Definition**: The first eight lines of an Italian (Petrarchan) sonnet, typically rhyming `abbaabba`, which present a problem or question.

### Sestet
- **Definition**: The last six lines of an Italian (Petrarchan) sonnet, typically rhyming `cdecde` or `cdcdcd`, which offer a resolution or answer to the problem presented in the octave.

### Volta
- **Definition**: The "turn" or shift in thought or argument in a sonnet, typically occurring between the octave and the sestet in an Italian sonnet, or before the final couplet in an English sonnet.

### English (Shakespearean) Sonnet
- **Definition**: A sonnet form consisting of three **quatrains** (4 lines each) and a concluding **couplet** (2 lines). The rhyme scheme is `abab cdcd efef gg`. The final couplet often provides a witty twist or summary.

### Quatrains
- **Definition**: A stanza or poem of four lines, especially one having a rhyming scheme. In an English sonnet, there are three quatrains.

### Couplet
- **Definition**: Two lines of verse, usually in the same meter and joined by rhyme, that form a unit. In an English sonnet, the final two lines form a couplet.

### Spenserian Sonnet
- **Definition**: A variation of the sonnet form created by Edmund Spenser, with a linked rhyme scheme: `abab bcbc cdcd ee`.

### Spenserian Stanza
- **Definition**: A nine-line stanza form, with the first eight lines in iambic pentameter and the ninth line in iambic hexameter (an alexandrine), rhyming `ababbcbcc`. It was created by Edmund Spenser for *The Faerie Queene*.

### Euphuism
- **Definition**: A highly artificial, elegant, and ornate prose style popular in the late 16th century, characterized by elaborate rhetorical devices, antithesis, alliteration, and classical allusions. John Lyly's *Euphues* is the namesake.

### Allegory
- **Definition**: A narrative in which characters, events, and settings represent abstract ideas or moral qualities. It has a literal meaning and a deeper, symbolic meaning, often used to convey a moral or political message.

### Pastoral Poetry
- **Definition**: A genre of poetry that idealizes rural life, shepherds, and the simplicity of the countryside, often contrasting it with the perceived corruption of city or court life.

### Pantheism
- **Definition**: The belief that God is identical with the universe and that divinity is present in everything. For Romantics like Wordsworth, this meant God could be experienced directly through nature.

### Byronic Hero
- **Definition**: A character type, created by Lord Byron, who is intelligent, arrogant, cynical, brooding, and often an outcast. He is haunted by a secret sin or past trauma and rebels against societal norms.

### Negative Capability
- **Definition**: A term coined by John Keats, describing the ability of a great artist to exist in "uncertainties, Mysteries, doubts, without any irritable reaching after fact & reason." It means embracing complexity rather than seeking simple answers.

### Constitutional Monarchy
- **Definition**: A form of government in which a monarch acts as head of state within the parameters of a written (or unwritten) constitution, meaning they are not the sole source of political power.

### Reform Bills
- **Definition**: A series of acts passed by the British Parliament in the 19th century (e.g., 1832, 1867, 1884) that expanded voting rights to a wider segment of the male population, gradually democratizing the electoral system.

### Imperial Expansion
- **Definition**: The process by which a country increases its power and influence by gaining control over other territories and peoples, often through colonization, military force, or economic dominance. The Victorian era saw the British Empire reach its zenith.

### Pax Britannica
- **Definition**: A period of relative peace in Europe and the world (roughly 1815-1914) during which the British Empire's naval and economic supremacy allowed for global stability and facilitated international trade.

### Free Trade Movement
- **Definition**: A political movement in 19th-century Britain that advocated for the removal of tariffs and other barriers to international trade, believing it would lead to economic prosperity. The repeal of the Corn Laws (1846) was a key victory.

### Banking and Finance
- **Definition**: Refers to the financial institutions and activities related to managing money, credit, and investments. London became the world's financial center during the Victorian era.

### Working Conditions
- **Definition**: The environment and terms under which labor is performed, including hours, safety, wages, and treatment. The Industrial Revolution led to harsh working conditions, which became a focus of social reform and literature.

### Class Structure
- **Definition**: The hierarchical organization of society based on economic, social, and cultural factors. Victorian Britain had a rigid class structure (upper, middle, working classes) that influenced social mobility and daily life.

### Urban Growth
- **Definition**: The rapid increase in the size and population of cities, particularly during the Industrial Revolution, due to migration from rural areas seeking employment in factories. This led to overcrowding and social problems.

### Women's Rights
- **Definition**: The movement and advocacy for the social, economic, political, and legal equality of women. The Victorian era saw the gradual emergence of women's suffrage movements and legal reforms like the Married Women's Property Acts.

### Evangelical Revival
- **Definition**: A religious movement within Protestantism, particularly strong in 18th and 19th-century Britain, emphasizing personal conversion, the authority of the Bible, and a commitment to moral reform and social activism.

### Catholic Emancipation
- **Definition**: The process in the United Kingdom during the late 18th and early 19th centuries that removed many of the restrictions on Roman Catholics, allowing them to hold public office and sit in Parliament (achieved in 1829).

### Religious Doubt
- **Definition**: A state of uncertainty or skepticism regarding religious beliefs, often fueled by scientific discoveries (e.g., Darwin's theory of evolution) and biblical criticism during the Victorian era.

### Secular Education
- **Definition**: Education provided by public schools or institutions that are not affiliated with any religious denomination and do not promote religious instruction. Its gradual establishment occurred during the Victorian period.

### Scientific Challenges
- **Definition**: Refers to the new scientific discoveries and theories (e.g., Darwin's evolution, geological findings) that challenged traditional religious beliefs and understandings of the world during the Victorian era.

### Geological Discoveries
- **Definition**: Advances in the study of the Earth's physical structure and substance, its history, and the processes that act on it. Discoveries by geologists like Charles Lyell challenged biblical chronology regarding the age of the Earth.

### Biblical Criticism
- **Definition**: The academic study and interpretation of biblical texts, often using historical, literary, and textual analysis, which in the Victorian era questioned traditional authorship and historical accuracy, contributing to religious doubt.

### Archaeological Findings
- **Definition**: Discoveries made through the study of human history and prehistory through the excavation of sites and the analysis of artifacts and other physical remains. Ancient civilizations predating biblical chronology contributed to religious doubt.

### Religious Responses
- **Definition**: The various ways religious institutions and individuals reacted to the scientific and intellectual challenges of the Victorian era, ranging from fundamentalism to liberal Christianity and agnosticism.

### Fundamentalism
- **Definition**: A form of a religion, especially Islam or Protestant Christianity, that upholds belief in the strict, literal interpretation of scripture. In the Victorian era, it was a response to scientific and biblical criticism.

### Liberal Christianity
- **Definition**: A movement within Christianity that emphasizes reason, experience, and modern scholarship, seeking to reconcile Christian faith with scientific findings and modern thought.

### Agnosticism
- **Definition**: The view that the existence of God, of the divine, or the supernatural is unknown or unknowable. The term was coined by T.H. Huxley in the Victorian era to describe a suspended judgment on religious questions.

### Secularization
- **Definition**: The transformation of a society from close identification with religious values and institutions toward nonreligious values and secular institutions. This was a gradual process in Victorian intellectual discourse.

### Utilitarianism
- **Definition**: A philosophical theory, primarily associated with Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill, that judges the rightness or wrongness of actions based on their consequences, aiming to maximize happiness and minimize suffering for the greatest number of people ("the greatest good for the greatest number").

### Hedonistic Calculus
- **Definition**: A method proposed by Jeremy Bentham for calculating the amount of pleasure or pain that a specific action is likely to cause, based on factors like intensity, duration, certainty, and propinquity.

### Individual vs. Social Utility
- **Definition**: A tension within Utilitarianism concerning whether the focus should be on maximizing happiness for the individual or for the collective society.

### Rule vs. Act Utilitarianism
- **Definition**: Two branches of Utilitarianism: Act Utilitarianism judges the morality of individual actions based on their consequences, while Rule Utilitarianism judges actions based on whether they adhere to rules that, if generally followed, would maximize overall happiness.

### Legal Reform
- **Definition**: Changes made to laws and legal systems, often driven by social and philosophical movements. Utilitarianism influenced legal reforms in Victorian Britain, such as prison design.

### Education Policy
- **Definition**: The principles and government decisions that influence the provision of education. Utilitarianism emphasized practical, useful knowledge in Victorian education policy.

### Economic Theory
- **Definition**: A set of ideas and principles that explain how economies work. Utilitarianism supported free-market capitalism as a system that could maximize overall utility.

### Social Reform
- **Definition**: Organized efforts to improve social conditions and address societal problems, often driven by moral or philosophical principles. Utilitarianism provided a justification for government intervention in social issues.

### The Oxford Movement
- **Definition**: A movement within the Church of England (1833-1845) that sought to restore traditional Catholic practices and beliefs while remaining Anglican, emphasizing apostolic succession, sacramental theology, and liturgical tradition.

### Gothic Revival Architecture
- **Definition**: A 19th-century architectural movement that emulated medieval Gothic architecture, characterized by pointed arches, ribbed vaults, and elaborate ornamentation. It was influenced by the Oxford Movement's emphasis on traditional church forms.

### Liturgy
- **Definition**: The customary public worship performed by a religious group. The Oxford Movement sought to restore more elaborate ceremonies and vestments in Anglican liturgy.

### Religious Poetry
- **Definition**: Poetry that explores religious themes, beliefs, and experiences. The Oxford Movement inspired a significant body of devotional and theological verse.

### Positivism
- **Definition**: A philosophical system, founded by Auguste Comte, that holds that the only authentic knowledge is scientific knowledge, and that such knowledge can only come from positive affirmation of theories through strict scientific method and empirical evidence.

### Three Stages of Human Development
- **Definition**: Comte's theory that human societies and knowledge progress through three stages: Theological (supernatural explanations), Metaphysical (abstract principles), and Positive (scientific observation).

### Theological Stage
- **Definition**: The first stage in Comte's Positivism, where human understanding attributes phenomena to supernatural forces or deities.

### Metaphysical Stage
- **Definition**: The second stage in Comte's Positivism, where human understanding attributes phenomena to abstract forces or principles rather than supernatural beings.

### Positive Stage
- **Definition**: The final and most advanced stage in Comte's Positivism, where human understanding relies on scientific observation, empirical evidence, and the discovery of natural laws.

### Scientific Method
- **Definition**: A systematic approach to inquiry that involves observation, hypothesis formation, experimentation, data analysis, and conclusion, considered by Positivism as the only valid source of knowledge.

### Empiricism
- **Definition**: The theory that all knowledge is derived from sense-experience. Positivism is rooted in empiricism, emphasizing knowledge gained through observation.

### Determinism
- **Definition**: The philosophical idea that all events, including human actions, are ultimately determined by causes external to the will. Positivism and Naturalism often embrace a form of determinism, suggesting human behavior is governed by natural laws.

### Progress
- **Definition**: The idea of continuous improvement or advancement, particularly in social, scientific, and technological spheres. Positivism strongly believed in inevitable human progress through scientific understanding.

### Sociology
- **Definition**: The scientific study of human society, social behavior, patterns of social relationships, interaction, and culture. The term was coined by Auguste Comte.

### Social Darwinism
- **Definition**: A theory, popularized by Herbert Spencer, that applies Darwin's theory of natural selection to human society, arguing that social progress occurs through competition, with the "fittest" individuals and groups surviving and thriving.

### Survival of the fittest
- **Definition**: A phrase coined by Herbert Spencer, later adopted by Darwin, to describe the process of natural selection, where organisms best adapted to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce. In Social Darwinism, it applies to individuals and groups in society.

### Laissez-faire Economics
- **Definition**: An economic system in which transactions between private parties are free from government intervention, including regulation, privileges, tariffs, and subsidies. Social Darwinism often supported this, believing intervention interfered with natural selection.

### Racial Hierarchy
- **Definition**: A system of stratification that ranks individuals or groups based on perceived racial differences, often asserting the superiority of one race over others. Social Darwinism contributed to the justification of such hierarchies.

### Class Justification
- **Definition**: The use of theories (like Social Darwinism) to explain or legitimize existing social and economic inequalities, suggesting that wealth and poverty are natural outcomes of individual fitness or merit.

### Imperial Justification
- **Definition**: The use of theories (like Social Darwinism) to rationalize and legitimize colonial expansion and dominance, arguing that "civilized" nations have a right or duty to rule "primitive" peoples.

### Economic Policy
- **Definition**: Government actions and decisions concerning the economy, including taxation, spending, and regulation. Social Darwinism influenced policies that opposed welfare programs and social safety nets.

### Educational Theory
- **Definition**: Principles and ideas concerning the purpose, methods, and content of education. Social Darwinism influenced educational theories that emphasized competition-based learning and meritocracy.

### Socialism and Marxism
- **Definition**: Political and economic theories that advocate for social ownership or control of the means of production and distribution of goods, in contrast to private ownership. Marxism, developed by Karl Marx, emphasizes class struggle and revolution.

### Christian Socialism
- **Definition**: A movement that seeks to apply Christian principles to social reform, advocating for social justice, worker education, and cooperative societies.

### Class struggle
- **Definition**: A central concept in Marxist theory, referring to the conflict of interests between different social classes (e.g., the bourgeoisie and the proletariat) in a capitalist society.

### Dialectical materialism
- **Definition**: The philosophical basis of Marxism, which interprets history as a series of contradictions and conflicts (dialectic) arising from the material conditions of society (materialism), leading to social change and revolution.

### Fabian Society
- **Definition**: A British socialist organization founded in 1884, advocating for gradual, peaceful transition to socialism through education and political reform rather than revolution.

### Realism and the Novel
- **Definition**: The dominant literary mode of the Victorian era, where the novel became the preeminent genre, characterized by detailed social observation, complex plotting, and a panoramic scope to portray contemporary society accurately.

### Moral Purpose and Didacticism
- **Definition**: A key characteristic of Victorian literature, where writers believed literature should instruct as well as delight, serving as a tool for moral improvement and social reform, often with explicit moral commentary.

### Problem Novels
- **Definition**: A type of Victorian novel that directly addresses and explores contemporary social issues, such as poverty, industrial conditions, education, or women's rights, often with a reformist agenda.

### Didactic Elements
- **Definition**: Aspects of a literary work that are intended to teach a moral lesson or provide instruction. Victorian literature often contained explicit didactic elements.

### The Victorian Compromise
- **Definition**: A term describing the duality of Victorian society, characterized by a public emphasis on moral propriety, duty, and social order (surface respectability) that often masked hidden realities of hypocrisy, sexual repression, and social inequality.

### Idealism vs. Doubt
- **Definition**: A central tension in Victorian literature, reflecting the conflict between a belief in progress and traditional values (idealism) and anxieties arising from scientific discoveries, industrialization, and social problems (doubt).

### Nostalgia
- **Definition**: A sentimental longing or wistful affection for the past, typically for a period or place with happy personal associations. In Victorian literature, it often manifested as an idealization of the medieval or pre-industrial past.

### Serial Publication
- **Definition**: The practice of publishing novels or other literary works in installments over time, typically in magazines or as separate pamphlets. This dominated Victorian novel publishing and influenced narrative structure.

### Dramatic Monologue
- **Definition**: A type of lyric poem in which a single speaker (who is clearly not the poet) addresses a silent but identifiable listener in a specific, dramatic situation, inadvertently revealing their own character, motivations, and moral nature. Robert Browning perfected this form.

### Sprung rhythm
- **Definition**: A poetic meter invented by Gerard Manley Hopkins, designed to mimic the rhythm of natural speech. It counts only the stressed syllables in a line, allowing for a variable number of unstressed syllables.

### Return to Nature
- **Definition**: A key principle of the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood, advocating for direct observation from life and a rejection of academic conventions in art and literature.

### Medieval Revival
- **Definition**: A renewed interest in and emulation of the art, literature, and values of the Middle Ages, particularly evident in the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood as a reaction against industrial modernity.

### Moral Purpose
- **Definition**: The belief that art and literature should serve a didactic function, conveying serious moral or religious messages and contributing to the improvement of society. This was a key aim of the Pre-Raphaelites and much Victorian literature.

### Truth to Nature
- **Definition**: A fundamental principle of the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood, emphasizing meticulous detail and accurate representation of the natural world in their art and literature.

### Art for Art's Sake
- **Definition**: The central tenet of Aestheticism, asserting that art needs no justification beyond its own beauty and should be independent of moral, social, or utilitarian concerns. (French: *L'art pour l'art*).

### Decadent Movement
- **Definition**: A late 19th-century literary and artistic movement, closely associated with Aestheticism, characterized by a fascination with artificiality, exoticism, and often a sense of moral decay or perversity.

### Literary Realism
- **Definition**: See "The Realism Movement" above.

### Naturalism
- **Definition**: An extreme form of realism that applies scientific principles to literature, viewing human behavior as determined by heredity, environment, and social conditions, often with a pessimistic outlook.

### Literary Naturalism
- **Definition**: See "Naturalism" above.

### Sensation Fiction
- **Definition**: A literary genre popular in the 1860s that combined elements of Gothic horror with domestic realism, featuring shocking events, secrets, and crimes within respectable middle-class settings.

### Domestic Gothic
- **Definition**: A subgenre of Gothic literature, particularly seen in Sensation Fiction, where elements of horror, mystery, and psychological suspense are brought into familiar, often middle-class, domestic settings.

### Melodrama
- **Definition**: A dramatic genre characterized by exaggerated characters and exciting events intended to appeal to the emotions. It was a popular theatrical form in early Victorian drama, often with clear moral distinctions.

### Farce
- **Definition**: A comic dramatic work using buffoonery and horseplay and typically including crude characterization and ludicrously improbable situations. It was a form of light comedy in Victorian drama.

### Historical Drama
- **Definition**: A dramatic genre that portrays events or figures from history, often with romanticized or idealized treatments.

### Social Justice and Reform
- **Definition**: Major themes in Victorian literature, reflecting concerns about industrial conditions, urban poverty, women's rights, and the need for societal improvements.

### Faith and Doubt
- **Definition**: A central theme in Victorian literature, exploring the conflict between traditional religious beliefs and the challenges posed by scientific discoveries, leading to intellectual and spiritual anxiety.

### Empire and National Identity
- **Definition**: Major themes in Victorian literature, reflecting the pride in the British Empire's global dominance alongside questions about exploitation, racial attitudes, and the definition of Englishness.

### Progress and Tradition
- **Definition**: A key tension in Victorian literature, exploring the conflict between a belief in technological and social advancement (progress) and a longing for or adherence to established customs and values (tradition).

### Individual vs. Society
- **Definition**: A recurring theme in Victorian literature, examining the tension between personal desires, fulfillment, and autonomy, and the pressures of social conformity, class expectations, and gender roles.

### Bildungsroman
- **Definition**: A literary genre that focuses on the psychological and moral growth of the protagonist from youth to adulthood, often involving a journey of self-discovery and integration into society. (German for "novel of formation" or "novel of education").

### Gothic Elements
- **Definition**: Characteristics of Gothic literature, such as atmospheric settings, supernatural occurrences, psychological horror, mysterious characters, and dark themes, often used in Victorian literature to explore psychological states and social anxieties.

### Realism vs. Romanticism
- **Definition**: A comparison of two major literary modes. Realism focuses on objective observation and accurate representation of everyday life, while Romanticism emphasizes emotion, imagination, and individual experience. The Victorian period saw a shift towards realism, though romantic elements persisted.

### Free Indirect Discourse
- **Definition**: A narrative technique that blends third-person narration with a character's thoughts and speech patterns, creating an intimate yet objective perspective without using quotation marks or explicit tags like "he thought."

### The New Woman
- **Definition**: A feminist ideal that emerged in late Victorian literature, representing educated, independent women who challenged traditional gender roles and sought equality in marriage, education, and careers.

### Omniscient Narration
- **Definition**: A narrative technique where the narrator knows everything about all characters and events, including their thoughts, feelings, and motivations. This "all-knowing" narrator can move freely through time and space and often provides social commentary.

### The Three-Volume Novel ("Triple-Decker")
- **Definition**: The standard format for publishing serious fiction in Victorian Britain, consisting of three separate volumes. These expensive books were primarily sold to circulating libraries and influenced the length and structure of novels.
