# Impressionism

**Impressionism** was a 19th-century art movement characterized by relatively small, thin, yet visible brush strokes, open composition, emphasis on accurate depiction of light in its changing qualities (often accentuating the effects of the passage of time), ordinary subject matter, inclusion of movement as a crucial element of human perception and experience, and unusual visual angles.

## Characteristics

*   **Visible Brushstrokes**: Artists used loose, distinct brushstrokes to capture the fleeting impression of a scene.
*   **Light and Color**: A strong focus on capturing the changing effects of light and atmosphere, often using pure, unmixed colors.
*   **Everyday Subjects**: Impressionists often depicted ordinary scenes of contemporary life, landscapes, and cityscapes, rather than historical or mythological subjects.
*   **Open Composition**: Compositions often appeared spontaneous and informal, sometimes cropping subjects in a way that suggested a snapshot.

## Historical Context

Originating in France in the 1860s, Impressionism was a radical departure from the academic art of the time. It was initially met with harsh criticism but eventually gained widespread acceptance and influenced subsequent art movements.

## Impressionism in Literature

While primarily an art movement, Impressionism had an influence on literature, particularly in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Literary Impressionism often features:

*   **Focus on Subjective Experience**: Narratives that emphasize the character's perceptions, feelings, and sensory experiences rather than objective reality.
*   **Fragmented Narratives**: Stories that may not follow a strict chronological order, reflecting the way memories and perceptions are often fragmented.
*   **Emphasis on Atmosphere and Mood**: Writers use descriptive language to evoke a particular atmosphere or mood, similar to how Impressionist painters used light and color.
*   **Stream of Consciousness**: Techniques like [[Stream of Consciousness]] can be seen as literary parallels to Impressionist painting, capturing the flow of thoughts and sensations.
