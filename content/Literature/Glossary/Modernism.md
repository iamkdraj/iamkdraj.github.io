## Definition
**Modernism** was a revolutionary international movement in the arts that emerged in the early 20th century, characterized by a conscious and radical break with 19th-century traditions. Modernist writers believed that Victorian forms and conventions were inadequate to represent the reality of the modern world.

## Definition
**Modernism** was a revolutionary international movement in the arts that emerged in the early 20th century, characterized by a conscious and radical break with 19th-century traditions. Modernist writers believed that Victorian forms and conventions were inadequate to represent the reality of the modern world.

## Historical Context
- **Period**: c. 1901-1945 (death of Queen Victoria to end of WWII)
- **Geographic Scope**: International movement across Europe and America
- **Cultural Crisis**: Response to unprecedented social, political, and technological changes
- **Call to Action**: Ezra Pound's famous slogan "Make It New"

## Key Influences

### Historical Events
- **World War I (1914-1918)**: Shattered Victorian belief in progress and stability
- **Technological Revolution**: Rapid industrialization and urbanization
- **Social Upheaval**: Breakdown of traditional class structures and values
- **Cultural Displacement**: Mass migration and cultural fragmentation

### Intellectual Influences
- **Sigmund Freud**: Theories of the unconscious mind and irrationality
- **Friedrich Nietzsche**: Proclamation that "God is dead," loss of religious certainty
- **Karl Marx**: Critique of capitalism and social alienation
- **William James**: Psychology of consciousness and stream of consciousness
- **Sir James Frazer**: Anthropological studies of myth and ritual

## Core Characteristics

### Formal Innovation
- **Experimentation with Form**: Deliberate breaking of traditional structures
- **Fragmentation**: Disrupted chronology and narrative coherence
- **Collage Technique**: Juxtaposition of disparate elements
- **Typography**: Innovative use of visual elements in text
- **Genre Blending**: Mixing of literary forms and techniques

### Thematic Concerns
- **Alienation**: Individual isolation in modern society
- **Spiritual Emptiness**: Loss of religious and moral certainties
- **Urban Decay**: City as place of anonymity and meaninglessness
- **Psychological Complexity**: Focus on inner consciousness
- **Cultural Fragmentation**: Breakdown of unified cultural narratives

## Major Movements Within Modernism

### Imagism
- **Leaders**: Ezra Pound, Amy Lowell
- **Principles**: Direct treatment of subject, precise imagery, free verse
- **Influence**: Emphasis on concrete, visual presentation

### Symbolism
- **Origins**: French symbolist poets
- **Technique**: Use of symbols to suggest rather than state meaning
- **Practitioners**: T.S. Eliot, W.B. Yeats

### Futurism
- **Origin**: Italian movement led by Marinetti
- **Focus**: Celebration of technology, speed, and modern life
- **Influence**: Experimental typography and form

### Vorticism
- **Leaders**: Ezra Pound, Wyndham Lewis
- **Characteristics**: Angular, dynamic artistic style
- **Literary Application**: Emphasis on energy and movement

## Literary Techniques

### Narrative Innovation
- **Stream of Consciousness**: Direct presentation of thought processes
- **Interior Monologue**: Character's internal dialogue
- **Multiple Perspectives**: Fragmented narrative viewpoints
- **Mythical Method**: Use of myth to structure modern experience
- **Allusion**: Dense references to classical and contemporary works

### Stylistic Features
- **Free Verse**: Liberation from traditional poetic forms
- **Linguistic Experimentation**: Innovative use of language and syntax
- **Juxtaposition**: Unexpected combinations of images and ideas
- **Repetition**: Rhythmic and thematic repetition
- **Compression**: Condensed, economical expression

## Major Figures

### Poetry
- **T.S. Eliot**: "The Waste Land," "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock"
- **Ezra Pound**: "The Cantos," leader of Imagism
- **W.B. Yeats**: Later symbolist poetry
- **Wallace Stevens**: American modernist poetry
- **William Carlos Williams**: Imagist and objectivist poetry

### Fiction
- **James Joyce**: "Ulysses," master of stream of consciousness
- **Virginia Woolf**: "Mrs. Dalloway," "To the Lighthouse"
- **D.H. Lawrence**: "Sons and Lovers," psychological realism
- **Joseph Conrad**: "Heart of Darkness," early modernist techniques
- **Franz Kafka**: European modernist fiction

### Drama
- **George Bernard Shaw**: "Plays of ideas"
- **Eugene O'Neill**: American experimental drama
- **Samuel Beckett**: Minimalist, absurdist drama
- **Luigi Pirandello**: Italian experimental theater

## Global Impact

### National Movements
- **Irish Literary Revival**: Yeats, Synge, Joyce
- **Harlem Renaissance**: African American modernism
- **Russian Futurism**: Mayakovsky, experimental poetry
- **German Expressionism**: Kafka, experimental fiction
- **American Modernism**: Pound, Eliot, Williams

### Cultural Influence
- **Art**: Cubism, abstract expressionism
- **Music**: Atonality, experimental composition
- **Architecture**: Bauhaus, functionalism
- **Philosophy**: Existentialism, logical positivism
- **Psychology**: Freudian analysis, stream of consciousness

## Legacy and Influence

### Literary Impact
- **Narrative Technique**: Revolutionized storytelling methods
- **Poetic Form**: Established free verse as legitimate form
- **Critical Theory**: Influenced New Criticism and formalism
- **Academic Study**: Became central to literary curricula
- **Continuing Influence**: Shaped postmodern literature

### Cultural Significance
- **Artistic Freedom**: Established experimental art as legitimate
- **Cultural Critique**: Provided framework for social criticism
- **Intellectual Sophistication**: Raised artistic and intellectual standards
- **International Exchange**: Facilitated cross-cultural artistic dialogue
- **Modern Consciousness**: Shaped 20th-century cultural identity
