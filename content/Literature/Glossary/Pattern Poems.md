# Pattern Poems

## Definition
**Pattern Poems** (also called concrete or visual poems) are poems where the shape and visual arrangement of text on the page reflects or reinforces the poem's subject matter. The physical form becomes part of the meaning.

## Characteristics
- **Visual Form**: Shape matches the poem's content
- **Typographical Art**: Uses spacing, line breaks, font as artistic elements
- **Dual Meaning**: Both verbal and visual significance
- **Reader Experience**: Seen as well as read

## George Herbert's Examples
- **"The Altar"**: Shaped like a church altar
- **"Easter Wings"**: Lines arranged to look like bird wings
- **Integration**: Form and content work together

## Technique
- **Line Length**: Varies to create visual shape
- **Spacing**: Strategic use of white space
- **Word Placement**: Positioning for visual effect
- **Symbolic Shape**: Form represents poem's theme

## Purpose
- **Enhanced Meaning**: Visual reinforces verbal message
- **Reader Engagement**: Appeals to multiple senses
- **Artistic Innovation**: Combines poetry with visual art
- **Devotional Impact**: In Herbert's case, aids religious meditation

## Historical Context
- **17th Century**: Herbert pioneered English examples
- **Modern Revival**: Concrete poetry movement (20th century)
- **Digital Age**: New possibilities with computer typography
