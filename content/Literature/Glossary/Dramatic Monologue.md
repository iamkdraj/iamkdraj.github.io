# Dramatic Monologue

A **dramatic monologue** is a type of poem in which a single speaker addresses a silent listener, revealing their character and the situation in which they find themselves. The speaker is not the poet, but rather a fictional character created by the poet.

## Characteristics

*   **A single speaker**: The poem is spoken by one person.
*   **A silent listener**: The speaker is addressing another person who does not speak.
*   **Revelation of character**: The speaker reveals their personality, motivations, and biases through their words.
*   **A specific situation**: The poem is set in a particular time and place, and the speaker is responding to a specific event or situation.

## Examples

*   **Robert Browning's "My Last Duchess"**: The Duke of Ferrara is speaking to an emissary of the Count whose daughter he wishes to marry. He reveals his arrogance, jealousy, and cruelty as he describes the portrait of his late wife.
*   **T.S. Eliot's "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock"**: Prufrock is a timid and indecisive man who is struggling to find his place in the modern world. He reveals his anxieties and frustrations as he walks through the city streets.

## Significance

The dramatic monologue was a popular form in the [[The Victorian Period (c. 1837 – 1901)]]. It allowed poets to explore the psychology of their characters in a new and innovative way. It also allowed them to comment on social and political issues without directly expressing their own opinions.
