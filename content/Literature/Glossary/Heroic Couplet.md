# Heroic Couplet

A **heroic couplet** is a pair of rhyming lines of iambic pentameter. It was a dominant form of English poetry in the 17th and 18th centuries, particularly during the [[The Neo-Classical Period (1660–1798)]].

## Characteristics

*   **Iambic Pentameter**: Each line has ten syllables, with a pattern of unstressed and stressed syllables (da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM).
*   **Rhyme**: The two lines rhyme with each other (aa, bb, cc, etc.).
*   **End-stopped**: Each couplet is a self-contained grammatical unit, meaning that the thought is completed within the two lines.

## Examples

From Alexander Pope's "An Essay on Criticism":

> A little learning is a dang'rous thing;
> Drink deep, or taste not the Pierian spring:
> There shallow draughts intoxicate the brain,
> And drinking largely sobers us again.

## Significance

The heroic couplet was well-suited to the rational and witty spirit of the Neo-Classical period. Its balanced and orderly structure was seen as a reflection of the order and harmony of the universe. It was used in a wide variety of genres, including satire, epic poetry, and philosophical poems.
