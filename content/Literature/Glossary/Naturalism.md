# Naturalism

**Naturalism** in literature is a literary movement that emerged in the late 19th century, largely as an extension and more extreme form of [[The Realism Movement (c. 1850s – 1900)]]. It emphasizes the role of environmental, social, and hereditary forces in shaping human character and destiny. Naturalist writers often depict life as a grim, deterministic struggle, where characters have little control over their fates.

## Characteristics

*   **Determinism**: A central tenet of naturalism is the belief that human beings are largely products of their environment and heredity. Free will is often seen as an illusion.
*   **Pessimism**: Naturalist works often present a bleak and pessimistic view of life, highlighting the harsh realities of poverty, disease, and social injustice.
*   **Objectivity**: Writers strive for scientific objectivity, presenting characters and events with a detached, almost clinical perspective.
*   **Detailed Realism**: Like realism, naturalism employs meticulous detail to create a sense of verisimilitude, but often focuses on the darker, more sordid aspects of life.
*   **Lower Classes and Marginalized Characters**: Naturalist novels frequently feature characters from the working class or those on the fringes of society, whose struggles are often attributed to external forces.
*   **Survival of the Fittest**: Influenced by Darwinian theory, naturalism often explores themes of survival, adaptation, and the struggle for existence.

## Key Figures and Works

*   **Émile Zola (France)**: Considered the father of literary naturalism. His *Rougon-Macquart* series, including *Germinal* and *Nana*, meticulously explores the impact of heredity and environment on a family.
*   **Stephen Crane (America)**: *Maggie: A Girl of the Streets* depicts the tragic downfall of a young woman in a slum, driven by her circumstances.
*   **Theodore Dreiser (America)**: *Sister Carrie* portrays a young woman's rise to success, but also the moral compromises she makes along the way, influenced by urban life.

## Relationship to Realism

While naturalism shares realism's commitment to depicting life accurately, it goes further by suggesting that human behavior is largely determined by forces beyond individual control. Realism often focuses on the choices individuals make within their social context, whereas naturalism emphasizes the overwhelming power of external forces.
