## Definition
In 17th-century literature, **Wit** meant intellectual cleverness, ingenuity, and the ability to see surprising connections between different, seemingly unrelated things. It was valued as a sophisticated mental quality rather than simple humor.

## Characteristics
- **Intellectual Sharpness**: Quick, clever thinking
- **Unexpected Connections**: Linking dissimilar ideas
- **Logical Argumentation**: Reasoning through complex ideas  
- **Verbal Dexterity**: Skillful use of language
- **Surprise Element**: Startling but illuminating comparisons

## 17th Century vs. Modern Meaning
- **17th Century**: Intellectual brilliance and ingenuity
- **Modern**: Humor, comedy, amusing remarks
- **Metaphysical Use**: Serious exploration of ideas through clever comparisons

## Examples in Metaphysical Poetry
- **Donne's Conceits**: Comparing lovers to compass legs
- **Herbert's Imagery**: Simple objects revealing spiritual truths
- **Marvell's Arguments**: Logical seduction in "To His Coy Mistress"

## Function
- Demonstrates intellectual sophistication
- Creates memorable, striking images
- Argues complex philosophical points
- Engages readers' minds actively
