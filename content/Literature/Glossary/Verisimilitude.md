# Verisimilitude

**Verisimilitude** (from Latin *verisimilitudo*, meaning "likeness to truth") refers to the quality of appearing true or real. In literature, it is the semblance of reality in a work of fiction; the extent to which a fictional work is believable or plausible.

## Characteristics

*   **Believability**: The events, characters, and settings, even if fictional, must feel authentic and consistent within the world of the story.
*   **Plausibility**: The narrative should adhere to the laws of probability and the expectations of the reader, avoiding elements that strain credulity.
*   **Internal Consistency**: The rules and logic established within the fictional world must be maintained throughout the narrative.

## Significance in Literature

Verisimilitude is a crucial concept, particularly for literary movements that prioritize realistic representation:

*   **[[The Realism Movement (c. 1850s – 1900)]]**: Realist writers aimed for a high degree of verisimilitude, seeking to portray life "as it really is" without idealization or exaggeration. Their goal was to create a believable illusion of contemporary life.
*   **Naturalism**: As an extreme form of realism, naturalism also highly values verisimilitude, often through meticulous detail and scientific objectivity.

## Examples

*   A historical novel that accurately depicts the customs, language, and social conditions of a specific historical period exhibits verisimilitude.
*   A science fiction story that, despite its fantastical elements, presents a logically consistent and believable future world.
