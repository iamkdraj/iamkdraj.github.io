# Sublime

The **Sublime** is a concept, particularly prominent in 18th-century aesthetics, that refers to a quality of greatness, whether physical, moral, intellectual, metaphysical, or artistic. It is something that is awe-inspiring, grand, and even terrifying, evoking a sense of overwhelming power and magnitude that transcends human comprehension or measurement.

## Characteristics

*   **Awe and Terror**: Unlike beauty, which is often associated with pleasure and harmony, the sublime can evoke feelings of fear, dread, and a sense of insignificance in the face of immense power.
*   **Vastness and Infinity**: It is often associated with natural phenomena of immense scale, such as towering mountains, raging storms, vast oceans, or the infinite expanse of the cosmos.
*   **Overwhelming Power**: The sublime suggests a power that is beyond human control or understanding, leading to a sense of humility and wonder.
*   **Transcendence**: It can elevate the mind beyond the ordinary, mundane world, offering a glimpse of something beyond human experience.

## Key Theorists

*   **Edmund Burke**: In his *A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful* (1757), Burke distinguished between the beautiful and the sublime. He argued that beauty is associated with pleasure, smallness, and smoothness, while the sublime is associated with pain, vastness, and roughness, and that the latter evokes a more profound emotional response.
*   **Immanuel Kant**: In his *Critique of Judgment* (1790), Kant further developed the concept, distinguishing between the "mathematical sublime" (related to immense size) and the "dynamical sublime" (related to overwhelming power).

## The Sublime in Literature

The concept of the sublime was particularly influential in the [[The Gothic Movement (c. 1764 – 1820s)]] and the [[The Romanticism Movement (c. 1798 – 1837)]].

*   **Gothic Literature**: Gothic novels often used sublime landscapes (e.g., desolate castles, stormy seas) and supernatural elements to evoke fear, awe, and a sense of the unknown.
*   **Romantic Literature**: Romantic poets and writers often sought to capture the sublime in nature, emphasizing its wildness, grandeur, and its capacity to inspire profound emotional and spiritual experiences.
