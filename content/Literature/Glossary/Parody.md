# Parody

**Parody** is a literary or artistic work that imitates the style of another work, artist, or genre in an exaggerated way, usually for comic effect or ridicule. Unlike [[Pastiche]], which is a neutral imitation or homage, parody aims to mock, criticize, or comment on the original through humorous distortion.

## Characteristics

*   **Imitation and Exaggeration**: Parody closely mimics the distinctive features (e.g., language, plot conventions, character types, themes, stylistic quirks) of the original, but exaggerates them to highlight their absurdities or flaws.
*   **Comic or Critical Intent**: The primary purpose of parody is to amuse the audience by making fun of the original, or to offer a critical commentary on its conventions, ideas, or perceived weaknesses.
*   **Requires Familiarity**: For a parody to be effective, the audience must be familiar with the original work or genre being parodied.
*   **Often Satirical**: Parody is frequently used as a tool within [[Satire]] to critique social, political, or cultural phenomena by ridiculing their artistic expressions.

## Examples

*   ***Don Quixote* by Miguel de Cervantes**: Often considered one of the first great parodies, it satirizes the chivalric romances popular in Cervantes's time by depicting a delusional knight who tries to live by their outdated codes.
*   ***Scary Movie* film series**: These films parody popular horror movies by exaggerating their clichés and tropes for comedic effect.
*   ***The Daily Show* or *The Colbert Report***: These television programs often use parody to satirize news broadcasts and political commentary.

## Parody vs. Pastiche

The key distinction between parody and pastiche lies in their intent:

*   **Parody**: Aims to mock, critique, or satirize the original.
*   **Pastiche**: Aims to pay homage, imitate neutrally, or combine styles without critical intent.
