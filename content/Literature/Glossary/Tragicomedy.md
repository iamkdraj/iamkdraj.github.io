## Definition
**Tragicomedy** is a hybrid dramatic genre that combines serious tragic elements with comedic ones, typically featuring noble characters in dangerous situations that are resolved happily. It became popular in the Jacobean period as an alternative to pure tragedy or comedy.

## Key Characteristics
- **Mixed Elements**: Combines tragic and comic scenes
- **Happy Ending**: Always resolves positively despite serious conflicts
- **Noble Characters**: Features aristocratic or royal protagonists
- **Serious Themes**: Deals with love, honor, and political intrigue
- **Emotional Range**: Shifts between tension and relief

## Structure
- **Tragic Beginning**: Opens with serious conflict or danger
- **Comic Relief**: Interspersed with humorous scenes
- **Dramatic Reversal**: Sudden change leading to resolution
- **Reconciliation**: Conflicts resolved through forgiveness or discovery

## Major Practitioners
- **Francis Beaumont and John Fletcher**: Masters of the form
- **William Shakespeare**: Late romances (*The Tempest*, *The Winter's Tale*)

## Appeal
- Satisfied audience desire for both excitement and happy endings
- Reflected complex social and political realities
- Allowed exploration of serious themes without tragic consequences
