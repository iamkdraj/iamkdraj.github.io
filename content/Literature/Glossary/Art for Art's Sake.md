# Art for Art's Sake

**"Art for Art's Sake"** (from the French *l'art pour l'art*) is a slogan that expresses the philosophy that the intrinsic value of art, and the only true purpose of art, is to be art. It suggests that art does not need to serve a moral, political, didactic, or utilitarian purpose.

## Core Tenets

*   **Autonomy of Art**: Art is self-sufficient and should not be judged by external standards or its usefulness.
*   **Aesthetic Pleasure**: The primary goal of art is to provide aesthetic pleasure and beauty.
*   **Rejection of Didacticism**: Art should not be used to teach moral lessons or promote social agendas.
*   **Focus on Form**: Emphasis is often placed on the formal qualities of art (composition, color, line, sound) rather than its content or message.

## Historical Context

The phrase emerged in early 19th-century France, notably associated with Théophile Gautier, and gained prominence in England during the [[Aestheticism]] movement in the late 19th century. It was a reaction against the prevailing Victorian emphasis on art's moral and social utility.

## Significance

"Art for Art's Sake" was a foundational principle for many artistic movements, including Aestheticism, Symbolism, and parts of Modernism. It championed artistic freedom and the idea that art could exist purely for its own sake, without needing to justify its existence through external benefits.
