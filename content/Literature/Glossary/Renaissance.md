# Renaissance

## Definition
The **Renaissance** (French for "Rebirth") refers to the period of European history that marked a gradual but profound shift away from the medieval worldview. It represents a "rebirth" of interest in the classical art, literature, and learning of ancient Greece and Rome.

## Historical Context
- **Origins**: Began in Italy in the late 14th century with foundational figures like **Petrarch** and **Boccaccio**
- **English Renaissance**: Reached England around the early 16th century, flowering in the Elizabethan and Jacobean eras
- **Dating**: The English Renaissance is often dated from **1485** (start of Tudor Dynasty) to **1660** (Restoration)
- **Term Usage**: First used in its modern sense by French historian **Jules Michelet** in 1855

## Key Characteristics
- **Classical Revival**: Renewed interest in Greek and Roman culture, mythology, and literary forms
- **Humanism**: Shift from God-centered to human-centered worldview
- **Individualism**: Emphasis on human potential, reason, and achievement
- **Artistic Innovation**: Explosion of creativity in literature, art, and learning
- **The "Renaissance Man"**: Ideal of the multi-talented individual

## Literary Significance
- Marked the end of the "Barren Age" in English literature (after Chaucer's death in 1400)
- Introduction of new literary forms: sonnet, blank verse, revenge tragedy
- Development of English drama as a major art form
- Flourishing of poetry and prose
- Influence of classical models on English writers

## Sub-Periods
1. **Elizabethan Age** (1558–1603)
2. **Jacobean Age** (1603–1625)
3. **Caroline Age** (1625–1649)
4. **Commonwealth Period** (1649–1660)
