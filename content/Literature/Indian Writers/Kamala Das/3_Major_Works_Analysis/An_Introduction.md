# Analysis of "An Introduction"

## Introduction

"An Introduction," published in Kamala Das's 1965 collection *Summer in Calcutta*, is one of her most iconic and powerful poems. It serves as a bold declaration of her identity, her defiance against societal norms, and her right to choose her own language and voice. The poem is a quintessential example of her confessional style, characterized by its raw honesty, directness, and unapologetic assertion of female individuality and sexuality. It is a foundational text for understanding Kamala Das's poetic philosophy and her place as a feminist voice in Indian English literature.

## Plot Summary (or Poetic Narrative)

"An Introduction" is a dramatic monologue where the speaker directly addresses the reader, introducing herself and her life experiences. She begins by stating her age (sixteen) and the conventional expectations placed upon her as a woman in Indian society—to be a wife, to bear children, to conform. She recounts her early marriage and the disillusionment that followed, particularly the lack of emotional and sexual fulfillment.

She then asserts her right to her own body and her own desires, challenging the patriarchal control over female sexuality. The poem moves to a broader critique of societal hypocrisy and the suppression of women's voices. She speaks of her decision to write in English, despite being told it is not her language, and her determination to express her authentic self, regardless of criticism.

Throughout the poem, there's a powerful sense of rebellion against imposed identities and a fierce assertion of her individuality. She embraces her complexities, her desires, and her unique voice, concluding with a defiant acceptance of who she is, a woman who is "every woman who has ever loved."

## Major Characters (or Poetic Elements)

*   **The Speaker (Kamala Das):** The central figure, who is the poet herself. She is bold, defiant, and unapologetically honest, representing the voice of a woman asserting her autonomy and identity.

*   **Society/Patriarchy:** The unseen antagonist, representing the conventional norms, expectations, and restrictive forces that the speaker rebels against.

## Major Themes

*   **Search for Identity and Self-Assertion:** This is the poem's most prominent theme. The speaker struggles to define herself beyond the roles imposed by society and fiercely asserts her right to her own identity, voice, and desires.

*   **Female Sexuality and Agency:** The poem is a groundbreaking exploration of female sexual desire and the assertion of a woman's agency over her own body. It challenges the traditional silence and repression surrounding this topic.

*   **Rebellion Against Societal Norms:** The speaker openly defies conventional expectations regarding marriage, women's roles, and linguistic choices. Her life and her writing are acts of rebellion against patriarchal control.

*   **Language and Voice:** The poem addresses the politics of language, particularly the choice to write in English as an Indian. It is a declaration of her right to use any language to express her authentic self.

*   **Disillusionment with Marriage:** The poem touches upon the disillusionment with traditional marriage when it fails to provide emotional or sexual fulfillment, leading to a search for genuine connection.

## Literary Style

*   **Dramatic Monologue:** The poem is a direct address from the speaker to the reader, creating an intimate and confrontational tone.
*   **Confessional:** The poem is deeply personal and autobiographical, revealing intimate details of the poet's life and inner thoughts without inhibition.
*   **Direct and Unflinching Language:** Das uses simple, stark, and often provocative language, avoiding euphemisms, which contributes to the raw honesty and impact of the poem.
*   **Repetition:** The use of repetition, such as "I am I," emphasizes the speaker's assertion of individuality.
*   **Rhetorical Questions:** The poem employs rhetorical questions to challenge societal norms and engage the reader in a dialogue about identity and freedom.

## Critical Reception and Legacy

"An Introduction" is one of Kamala Das's most celebrated and controversial poems. It was praised for its courage, its honesty, and its powerful feminist message, but also drew criticism for its frankness regarding sexuality. Nevertheless, it remains a seminal work in Indian English literature, solidifying Kamala Das's reputation as a fearless and groundbreaking poet. It continues to inspire readers for its unapologetic assertion of female identity and its powerful critique of societal constraints.
