The word **"Renaissance"** (French for "Rebirth") refers to the period of European history that marked a gradual but profound shift away from the medieval worldview. Originating in Italy in the late 14th century with foundational figures like **Petrarch** and **Boccaccio**, its influence reached England around the early 16th century, flowering in the Elizabethan and Jacobean eras. The term was first used in its modern sense by French historian **Jules Michelet** in 1855.

This era represented a "rebirth" of interest in the classical art, literature, and learning of ancient Greece and Rome. After the death of Chaucer in 1400, English literature entered a period sometimes called the **"Barren Age."** The beginning of the English Renaissance is often dated to **1485** with the start of the Tudor Dynasty, and its decline can be marked by the death of King James I in 1625 or, more broadly, the start of the Restoration in 1660.

---

### Key Context and Influences

*   **Humanism**: This was the single most important intellectual movement. It shifted focus from a God-centered to a **human-centered** worldview, celebrating human potential, reason, and achievement. The ideal was the multi-talented "Renaissance Man."

*   **The Protestant Reformation**: King Henry VIII's break from the Roman Catholic Church placed immense emphasis on the individual's direct relationship with God, fueling religious and political controversy. This led to the monumental **King James Bible (1611)**.

*   **The Age of Discovery**: Voyages by explorers like Sir Francis Drake broadened intellectual horizons, fueled national pride, and brought new wealth and ideas to England.

*   **Invention of the Printing Press**: Introduced to England by **William Caxton** in 1476, this technology made books more accessible, fueling literacy and the rapid spread of Humanist and Reformation ideas.

> [!note]- The King James Bible (1611)
> Also known as the Authorized Version, this translation of the Bible was a landmark achievement of English prose. Commissioned during the Jacobean era, its powerful, simple, and rhythmic language had an enormous and lasting influence on all subsequent English literature, standardizing the language for generations.


> [!note]- What is Humanism?
> Humanism was a philosophical and educational movement that emphasized the value and agency of human beings. It promoted the study of the "humanities" – classical literature, history, rhetoric, and philosophy – believing that this education could help people achieve their full potential. Key humanists included **Sir Thomas More** (author of *Utopia*) and the Dutch scholar **Erasmus**. It did not reject religion but focused on making life on Earth more meaningful.


### Literary Characteristics of the Renaissance

*   **Focus on Humanism**: Literature explored complex human characters, their ambitions, and internal conflicts. Christopher Marlowe is often called the **"true child of the Renaissance"** for his depiction of protagonists with limitless ambition.
*   **Nationalism and Patriotism**: A surge of national pride, especially after the defeat of the **Spanish Armada in 1588**, is evident in works like Shakespeare's history plays.

*   **Richness and Experimentation**: A "golden age" for the English language, with writers coining new words and experimenting with literary forms, most notably the **sonnet**, **blank verse**, and new stanzaic forms.
*   **Classical Influence**: Writers drew heavily on Greek and Roman myths, history, and literary forms like the epic and tragedy.
*   **Spirit of Optimism and Exploration**: The era was marked by a belief in unlimited human and national potential, sometimes captured in phrases like **"love and lust for knowledge, beauty and wealth."**

> [!note]- The Spanish Armada (1588)
> The defeat of this massive Spanish fleet, sent to invade England, was a pivotal moment. It secured England's independence from Catholic Spain, boosted national morale, and established England as a major naval power, fueling the patriotic spirit seen in Elizabethan literature.

## Sub-Periods of the Renaissance

The English Renaissance is typically divided into four phases:

1.  **The Elizabethan Age (1558–1603)**
2.  **The Jacobean Age (1603–1625)**
3.  **The Caroline Age (1625–1649)**
4.  **The Commonwealth Period (1649–1660)**

### 1. The Elizabethan Age (1558–1603)

This period, under Queen Elizabeth I, is often considered the "Golden Age" of English literature. It was famously called a **"nest of singing birds"** for its explosion of lyric poetry, while drama was the dominant and most innovative literary form.

#### Elizabethan Drama

*   **Early Plays**: The **first regular English comedy** was Nicholas Udall's ***Ralph Roister Doister***. The **first English tragedy** was ***Gorboduc*** (1561) by Thomas Norton and Thomas Sackville, notable for being the first play written in **blank verse**.
*   **The University Wits**: A group of educated, innovative playwrights who paved the way for Shakespeare.

*   **Key Playwrights**:
    *   **Christopher Marlowe**: The greatest of the Wits. He perfected blank verse into what Ben Jonson called the **"mighty line."** His plays feature larger-than-life protagonists consumed by ambition (*Dr. Faustus*, *Tamburlaine the Great*).
    *   **Thomas Kyd**: Famous for ***The Spanish Tragedy***, which established the wildly popular genre of the **Revenge Tragedy**.
    *   **William Shakespeare (1564-1616)**: The supreme dramatist of the age. His Elizabethan works include tragedies (*Romeo and Juliet*), comedies (*As You Like It*), and histories (*Henry V*).
    *   **Ben Jonson**: Shakespeare's contemporary, known for his satirical **Comedy of Humours**.

> [!note]- Who were the University Wits?
> This was a group of late 16th-century playwrights and pamphleteers who were educated at the universities of Oxford or Cambridge. They transformed English drama with their sophisticated plots and powerful language. Key members included **Christopher Marlowe**, **Thomas Kyd**, **Robert Greene**, **John Lyly**, **George Peele**, and **Thomas Nashe**.

#### Elizabethan Poetry

*   **Introduction of the Sonnet**: **Sir Thomas Wyatt** & **Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey** introduced the sonnet to English literature from Italy. Their poems were published posthumously in ***Tottel's Miscellany*** (1557), the first printed anthology of English poetry. Wyatt used the Petrarchan form, while Surrey created the English (or Shakespearean) form and also pioneered the use of **blank verse** in English translation.
*   **Major Poets**:
    *   **Sir Philip Sidney**: The model Renaissance courtier. He wrote ***Astrophel and Stella***, a sonnet sequence that sparked a craze for the form, and ***The Defence of Poesie***, a major work of literary criticism.
    *   **Edmund Spenser**: Called "the Poet's Poet." His masterpiece is ***The Faerie Queene***, a vast, unfinished allegorical epic using the nine-line **Spenserian Stanza**. He also wrote the pastoral poem ***The Shepheardes Calender*** and the sonnet sequence ***Amoretti***.
    *   **William Shakespeare**: Wrote a famous sequence of **154 sonnets**, exploring themes of love, time, and beauty, addressed to a "Fair Youth" (1-126) and a "Dark Lady" (127-152). He also wrote the long narrative poems *Venus and Adonis* and *The Rape of Lucrece*.

#### Elizabethan Prose

*   **Sir Francis Bacon**: Known as the **"Father of the English Essay."** His essays are celebrated for their concise, memorable, and **aphoristic style**. His major prose works include *The Advancement of Learning*.
*   **John Lyly**: His prose romance ***Euphues: The Anatomy of Wit*** created a highly artificial and elegant style known as **Euphuism**.
*   **Sir Philip Sidney**: His prose romance ***Arcadia*** was a major work of prose fiction from this period.

### 2. The Jacobean Age (1603–1625)

Named after the reign of King James I (from the Latin *Jacobus*), this period saw a continuation of Elizabethan literary trends but often with a darker, more cynical, and questioning tone. The literature reflects a growing sense of unease and satire.

#### Jacobean Drama

*   **William Shakespeare**: Wrote his greatest tragedies (*Hamlet, Othello, King Lear, Macbeth*) and his final tragicomedies or "romances" (*The Tempest*) during this period.
*   **Ben Jonson**: Continued to dominate the stage with his satirical comedies like ***Volpone*** and ***The Alchemist***.
*   **Jacobean Tragedy**: This drama became darker and more sensational.
    *   **John Webster**: His violent and psychologically disturbing tragedies, like ***The Duchess of Malfi*** and ***The White Devil***, are prime examples of the era's taste for horror and corruption.
    *   **Thomas Middleton**: Another key playwright, known for his cynical city comedies and tragedies.

#### Jacobean Poetry: A Parting of the Ways

Two distinct poetic traditions emerged, originating with Ben Jonson and John Donne.

> [!note]- The Metaphysical Poets
> This name was first applied by Samuel Johnson to a group of 17th-century poets who followed the style of **John Donne**. Their work is characterized by:
> *   **Intellectual and Argumentative Style**: Poems are often presented as arguments or debates.
> *   **Metaphysical Conceits**: Ingenious, surprising, and often shocking comparisons between two very dissimilar things (e.g., lovers' souls to the legs of a compass in Donne's "A Valediction: Forbidding Mourning").
> *   **Colloquial Language and Rough Meter**: A deliberate break from the smooth, musical verse of Elizabethan poetry.
> *   **Themes**: Explored complex themes of love, death, and religious faith with psychological realism.
> *   **Key Figures**: **John Donne** (the founder), **George Herbert**, **Henry Vaughan**, and **Andrew Marvell**.

> [!note]- The Cavalier Poets
> This group comprised supporters of King Charles I (the "Cavaliers"). They were influenced by **Ben Jonson** and classical poets like Horace. Their work is characterized by:
> *   **Elegance and Clarity**: Polished, light, and musical verse.
> *   **Secular Themes**: Often celebrated beauty, love, loyalty, and the *carpe diem* ("seize the day") philosophy.
> *   **Classical and Conservative**: They avoided the intellectual complexity of the Metaphysicals in favor of grace and wit.
> *   **Key Figures**: **Robert Herrick**, **Richard Lovelace**, and **Sir John Suckling**.

#### Jacobean Prose

*   **The King James Bible (1611)**: The single most important prose achievement of the era.
*   **Sir Francis Bacon**: Published the final, expanded edition of his *Essays* in 1625. His *Novum Organum* (1620) was a key work of scientific philosophy.
*   **Robert Burton**: His encyclopedic and eccentric work, ***The Anatomy of Melancholy*** (1621), explored the human condition through the lens of melancholy.

### 3. The Caroline Age (1625–1649)

Named for the reign of King Charles I, this era was dominated by escalating political and religious conflicts between the King and Parliament, culminating in the English Civil War (1642–1649).

*   **Poetry**: The Metaphysical and Cavalier traditions continued to flourish, with many of their most famous works being written and published during this time.
*   **The Rise of Puritan Writers**: The Puritan voice grew stronger, most notably in the early work of **John Milton**, who wrote his great pastoral elegy ***Lycidas*** (1638) during this period.
*   **Drama**: The theater, associated with the court and seen as morally suspect by the Puritans, entered a period of decline.

### 4. The Commonwealth Period (1649–1660)

Also known as the Interregnum ("between reigns"), this was the period after the execution of Charles I when England was ruled as a republic by Parliament and Oliver Cromwell.

*   **The End of Renaissance Drama**: The Puritans officially **closed all public theaters in 1642**, and they remained shut until the Restoration in 1660. This act effectively ended the great tradition of English Renaissance drama.
*   **Prose Dominance**: Literary output shifted heavily towards religious and political prose. **John Milton** served as a propagandist for the Commonwealth government, writing powerful political pamphlets. Other notable prose writers include **Thomas Hobbes** (*Leviathan*, 1651) and **Izaak Walton** (*The Compleat Angler*, 1653).
*   **Poetry**: The major poetic work of the era was Milton's preparation for his great epic, ***Paradise Lost***, which would be published after the Restoration.

The Renaissance period concludes in 1660 with the restoration of the monarchy under Charles II, ushering in the Neoclassical Period.

### Appendix: Key Literary Forms and Concepts of the Renaissance

> [!note]- Allegory
> A narrative in which characters, events, and settings represent abstract ideas or moral qualities. It has a literal meaning and a deeper, symbolic meaning. Spenser's *The Faerie Queene* is a famous example.

> [!note]- Blank Verse
> Unrhymed poetry written in **iambic pentameter**. An "iamb" is a two-syllable foot with the stress on the second syllable (da-DUM). "Pentameter" means there are five such feet per line (ten syllables total). Blank verse became the standard for English drama (Marlowe, Shakespeare) and epic poetry (Milton) because its rhythm closely mimics natural English speech.

> [!note]- Comedy of Humours
> Based on the ancient medical theory of the "four humours." In these plays, each major character is dominated by a single, overriding trait or obsession (e.g., jealousy, greed) which represents an imbalance of these humours. This "humour" dictates their actions and makes them a target for satire. Ben Jonson's *Every Man in His Humour* is a prime example.

> [!note]- Pastoral Poetry
> Poetry that deals with the lives of shepherds and the simplicity of rural life, often in an idealized way. It contrasts the supposed peace of the countryside with the corruption of city or court life. Spenser's *The Shepheardes Calender* is a key English example.

> [!note]- Revenge Tragedy
> A dramatic genre where the central motive is revenge for a real or imagined injury. Key elements include a secret murder, a vengeful ghost, plotting, madness (real and feigned), and a bloody, violent climax. It was heavily influenced by the Roman playwright Seneca, and Shakespeare's *Hamlet* is the most famous example.

> [!note]- Sonnet
> A lyric poem of **14 lines** in iambic pentameter with a specific rhyme scheme.
> *   **Italian (Petrarchan) Sonnet**: An **octave** (8 lines, `abbaabba`) presenting a problem, and a **sestet** (6 lines, `cdecde` or `cdcdcd`) offering a resolution. The turn in thought is the *volta*.
> *   **English (Shakespearean) Sonnet**: Three **quatrains** (4 lines each) and a concluding **couplet** (2 lines). The rhyme scheme is `abab cdcd efef gg`. The final couplet often provides a witty twist.
> *   **Spenserian Sonnet**: A variation with a linked rhyme scheme: `abab bcbc cdcd ee`.
