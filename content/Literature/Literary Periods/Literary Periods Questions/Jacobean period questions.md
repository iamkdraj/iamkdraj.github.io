### 2. Which event in 1605 created a widespread sense of paranoia and conspiracy reflected in Jacobean drama?

> [!tip]- (1) The defeat of the Spanish Armada
> ❌ Incorrect  
> This occurred in 1588 during the Elizabethan era and created a mood of patriotic fervor, not paranoia.

> [!tip]- (2) The execution of King Charles I
> ❌ Incorrect  
> This event in 1649 ended the Caroline Age and began the Commonwealth period.

> [!tip]- (3) The Gunpowder Plot
> ✅ Correct  
> This failed Catholic plot to assassinate King James I and blow up Parliament fostered a climate of paranoia, intrigue, and betrayal that is a hallmark of Jacobean tragedy.

> [!tip]- (4) The start of the English Civil War
> ❌ Incorrect  
> The Civil War began later, in 1642, during the Caroline Age.

> [!info]- 📘 Related Concepts (click to expand)
> The fascination with plots, betrayal, and hidden motives in plays by Shakespeare, Webster, and Middleton reflects this anxious political atmosphere.

### 3. How did the general mood of the Jacobean Age differ from the Elizabethan Age?

> [!tip]- (1) It was more optimistic and patriotic.
> ❌ Incorrect  
> This describes the Elizabethan Age.

> [!tip]- (2) It was more cynical, satirical, and psychologically complex.
> ✅ Correct  
> The boundless optimism of the Elizabethan era gave way to a darker, more critical, and disillusioned view of humanity, marked by a fascination with moral decay and corruption.

> [!tip]- (3) It was more focused on religious harmony and peace.
> ❌ Incorrect  
> The period saw rising religious tensions between Anglicans, Puritans, and Catholics, not harmony.

> [!tip]- (4) It was simpler and less intellectual.
> ❌ Incorrect  
> The literature, especially Metaphysical poetry and the tragedies of Shakespeare and Webster, was highly intellectual and complex.

> [!info]- 📘 Related Concepts (click to expand)
> This shift in tone is one of the key distinctions between the two phases of the English Renaissance.

### 6. What is a "Comedy of Humours"?

> [!tip]- (1) A romantic comedy with a happy ending.
> ❌ Incorrect  
> This is a broader genre, not the specific satirical form based on humours.

> [!tip]- (2) A play where each character is dominated by a single, overriding trait.
> ✅ Correct  
> Perfected by Ben Jonson, this type of comedy is based on the theory of the "four humours," where a character's imbalance makes them a target for satire (e.g., a character dominated by greed).

> [!tip]- (3) A tragicomedy that mixes serious and funny elements.
> ❌ Incorrect  
> This is a different hybrid genre.

> [!tip]- (4) A play that uses puns and wordplay for comic effect.
> ❌ Incorrect  
> While it may use puns, the defining feature is the characterization based on humours.

> [!info]- 📘 Related Concepts (click to expand)
> This satirical genre focused on the vices and follies of the urban middle class in London, and Ben Jonson's *Volpone* and *The Alchemist* are its greatest examples.

### 10. What is the most famous characteristic device of Metaphysical poetry?

> [!tip]- (1) Smooth and musical language
> ❌ Incorrect  
> This is characteristic of Elizabethan poetry; Metaphysical poetry often used rough, colloquial language.

> [!tip]- (2) The use of conceits
> ✅ Correct  
> The metaphysical conceit—a clever, witty, and often shocking extended metaphor between two very dissimilar things—is the hallmark of the style.

> [!tip]- (3) Simple, emotional expression
> ❌ Incorrect  
> The poetry is highly intellectual and argumentative, not simple.

> [!tip]- (4) Patriotic themes
> ❌ Incorrect  
> The primary themes were love, death, and religion, explored psychologically.

> [!info]- 📘 Related Concepts (click to expand)
> John Donne's comparison of two lovers' souls to the legs of a compass is the classic example of a metaphysical conceit.

### 17. The comparison of two lovers' souls to the legs of a geometer's compass is a famous example of what?

> [!tip]- (1) A heroic simile
> ❌ Incorrect  
> A heroic (or epic) simile is a long, elaborate comparison, but this example is specifically famous for its intellectual and un-poetic nature.

> [!tip]- (2) A metaphysical conceit
> ✅ Correct  
> This image, from John Donne's "A Valediction: Forbidding Mourning," is the quintessential example of a metaphysical conceit—a startling, intellectual comparison between two very dissimilar things.

> [!tip]- (3) A paradox
> ❌ Incorrect  
> While Metaphysicals used paradox, this specific device is a conceit. A paradox is a self-contradictory statement.

> [!tip]- (4) An allegory
> ❌ Incorrect  
> An allegory is a whole narrative with a symbolic meaning, not a single figure of speech.

> [!info]- 📘 Related Concepts (click to expand)
> The conceit works by logically developing the comparison: one lover stays fixed at the center while the other roams, but they remain connected and eventually come back together.

### 19. A play that mixes serious, potentially tragic events with a happy, comedic resolution is known as a:

> [!tip]- (1) Revenge Tragedy
> ❌ Incorrect  
> A revenge tragedy always ends in a bloody catastrophe.

> [!tip]- (2) City Comedy
> ❌ Incorrect  
> A city comedy is satirical and focuses on urban life; it does not typically involve serious, life-threatening tragic elements.

> [!tip]- (3) Tragicomedy
> ✅ Correct  
> This hybrid genre, perfected by Beaumont and Fletcher, intentionally mixes the tones of tragedy and comedy, leading the audience to believe a disaster might occur before a happy resolution is achieved.

> [!tip]- (4) History Play
> ❌ Incorrect  
> A history play dramatizes historical events, which may be tragic or triumphant, but it is a distinct genre.

> [!info]- 📘 Related Concepts (click to expand)
> Shakespeare's late "romances" like *The Winter's Tale*, where a tragic first half is resolved by a miraculous and happy ending, are also examples of this genre.
