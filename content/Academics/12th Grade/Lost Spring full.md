## Part 1: 'Sometimes I find a Rupee in the garbage'

### Paragraph-by-Paragraph Translation

**English Paragraph (About the author):**
Anees Jung (1944) was born in Rourkela and spent her childhood and adolescence in Hyderabad. She received her education in Hyderabad and in the United States of America. Her parents were both writers. Anees Jung began her career as a writer in India. She has been an editor and columnist for major newspapers in India and abroad, and has authored several books. The following is an excerpt from her book titled *Lost Spring, Stories of Stolen Childhood*. Here she analyses the grinding poverty and traditions which condemn these children to a life of exploitation.

**English Paragraph:**

He would stop briefly at the temple and pray for a pair of shoes. Thirty years later I visited his town and the temple, which was now drowned in an air of desolation. Young boys like the son of the priest now wore shoes. But many others like the ragpickers in my neighbourhood remain shoeless.

**English Paragraph:**
My acquaintance with the barefoot ragpickers leads me to Seemapuri, a place on the periphery of Delhi yet miles away from it, metaphorically. Those who live here are squatters who came from Bangladesh back in 1971. Saheb's family is among them. Seemapuri was then a wilderness. It still is, but it is no longer empty. In structures of mud, with roofs of tin and tarpaulin, devoid of sewage, drainage or running water, live 10,000 ragpickers. They have lived here for more than thirty years without an identity, without permits but with ration cards that get their names on voters' lists and enable them to buy grain. Food is more important for survival than an identity. "If at the end of the day we can feed our families and go to bed without an aching stomach, we would rather live here than in the fields that gave us no grain,” say a group of women in tattered saris when I ask them why they left their beautiful land of green fields and rivers. Wherever they find food, they pitch their tents that become transit homes. Children grow up in them, becoming partners in survival. And survival in Seemapuri means rag-picking. Through the years, it has acquired the proportions of a fine art. Garbage to them is gold. It is their daily bread, a roof over their heads, even if it is a leaking roof. But for a child it is even more.

**English Paragraph:**
“I sometimes find a rupee, even a ten-rupee note,” Saheb says, his eyes lighting up. When you can find a silver coin in a heap of garbage, you don't stop scrounging, for there is hope of finding more. It seems that for children, garbage has a meaning different from what it means to their parents. For the children it is wrapped in wonder, for the elders it is a means of survival.

### Understanding the text (Page 7)

**1. What could be some of the reasons for the migration of people from villages to cities?**
**Answer:** Based on the text, the primary reasons for migration are:
    *   **Natural Disasters:** As in the case of Saheb's family, storms swept away their fields and homes in Bangladesh, forcing them to leave.
    *   **Poverty and Lack of Livelihood:** The fields in their village "gave them no grain." People migrate to cities in search of a livelihood and food for their families. As the women in Seemapuri say, "Food is more important for survival than an identity."
    *   **Hope for a Better Life:** People migrate to big cities "looking for gold"—that is, in search of better economic opportunities and a chance to improve their living conditions.

**2. Would you agree that promises made to poor children are rarely kept? Why do you think this happens in the incidents narrated in the text?**
**Answer:** Yes, I agree that promises made to poor children are rarely kept. In the text, the author makes a "half-joking" promise to start a school, which she admits was "not meant." This happens for several reasons:
    *   **Insincerity:** The promises are often made "glibly" or casually, without any real intention of fulfilling them.
    *   **Powerlessness:** The people making the promises may not have the resources or power to follow through.
    *   **Forgetfulness:** The problems of the poor are so vast that a single person's casual promise is easily forgotten.
    The text states that "promises like mine abound in every corner of his bleak world," suggesting that these children are used to hollow promises and that it is a cruel part of their reality.

**3. What forces conspire to keep the workers in the bangle industry of Firozabad in poverty?**
**Answer:** The text describes two sets of forces that conspire to keep the workers in poverty:
    *   **The Vicious Circle of Exploiters:** This includes the *sahukars* (moneylenders), the middlemen, the policemen, the keepers of law, the bureaucrats, and the politicians. They trap the workers in debt, exploit their labor, and use their power to prevent them from organizing or escaping.
    *   **The Burden of Lineage and Caste:** The workers are "burdened by the stigma of caste" they are born into. They accept their fate ("karam" or "destiny") and believe they cannot break the "god-given lineage" of bangle-making. This self-acceptance of their condition prevents them from daring to change their situation.
Together, these external and internal forces create a trap from which it is almost impossible to escape.

**Answer:** (This question is a repetition of "Think as you read" Q2.) The main hazards are: working in dark, poorly ventilated cells with high-temperature furnaces; inhaling harmful dust from glass polishing, which leads to lung diseases; and severe eye damage, often resulting in blindness at a young age. **3. Why should child labour be eliminated and how?**
**Answer:**
*   **Why it should be eliminated:** Child labour should be eliminated because it is a violation of human rights. It steals a child's "spring" or childhood, denying them the right to education, play, and a healthy life. It traps them in a cycle of poverty, harms their physical and mental development, and exposes them to exploitation and hazardous conditions.
*   **How it can be eliminated:** Child labour can be eliminated through a multi-pronged approach:
    1.  **Strict Enforcement of Laws:** Governments must strictly enforce laws like the Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Act.
    2.  **Education:** Providing free, compulsory, and quality education is the most effective tool to pull children out of labour.
    3.  **Poverty Alleviation:** Governments and NGOs must work to provide financial support and alternative livelihoods to poor families so they are not forced to send their children to work.
    4.  **Awareness Campaigns:** Creating public awareness about the evils of child labour and encouraging people not to employ children.
    5.  **Rehabilitation:** Rescuing children from work and providing them with proper rehabilitation, counseling, and education.

### Thinking about language (Page 8)

**Identify the literary device in each example:**

1.  **Saheb-e-Alam which means the lord of the universe is directly in contrast to what Saheb is in reality.**
    *   **Literary Device:** **Irony.** The name, which means "lord of the universe," is deeply ironic because Saheb is a poor, barefoot ragpicker.
2.  **Drowned in an air of desolation.**
    *   **Literary Device:** **Metaphor.** Desolation (sadness/emptiness) is described as a liquid (like water) in which the temple is "drowned."
3.  **Seemapuri, a place on the periphery of Delhi yet miles away from it, metaphorically.**
    *   **Literary Device:** **Metaphor** (and **Antithesis**). It compares the metaphorical distance (in terms of development and facilities) to a physical distance ("miles away").
4.  **For the children it is wrapped in wonder; for the elders it is a means of survival.**
    *   **Literary Device:** **Metaphor** ("wrapped in wonder") and **Antithesis** (contrasting the children's perspective with the elders').
5.  **As her hands move mechanically like the tongs of a machine...**
    *   **Literary Device:** **Simile.** Her hands are directly compared to the tongs of a machine using the word "like."
6.  **She still has bangles on her wrist, but not light in her eyes.**
    *   **Literary Device:** **Metaphor** ("light in her eyes" represents hope/joy) and **Antithesis** (contrasting the presence of bangles with the absence of light/joy).
7.  **Few airplanes fly over Firozabad.**
    *   **Literary Device:** **Symbolism.** Airplanes symbolize dreams, ambition, and a world of opportunities that is out of reach for the people of Firozabad.
8.  **Web of poverty.**
    *   **Literary Device:** **Metaphor.** Poverty is compared to a web, suggesting it's a trap that is difficult to escape from.
9.  **Scrounging for gold.**
    *   **Literary Device:** **Metaphor.** "Gold" refers not to actual gold, but to anything valuable (like money or saleable items) found in the garbage.
