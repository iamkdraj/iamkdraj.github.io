### The Romantic Period (c. 1798 – 1837)

The Romantic Period in English literature does not deal with "romance" in the modern sense of love stories. Instead, it was a profound artistic, literary, and intellectual movement that originated as a revolt against the core values of the **Neo-Classical Age**. It turned away from the Neo-Classical emphasis on **reason, order, and society**, and instead championed **emotion, imagination, and the individual**.

The period's official beginning is marked by the publication of William Wordsworth and Samuel Taylor Coleridge's ***Lyrical Ballads*** in **1798**. It ends with the coronation of Queen Victoria in 1837, which began the Victorian Era.

---

#### **Context and Influences**

1.  **The French Revolution (1789):** This was the single most important event shaping the Romantic imagination. The revolution's ideals of **"Liberty, Equality, and Fraternity"** and its overthrow of an oppressive monarchy initially inspired the young Romantic poets with a spirit of hope, freedom, and radical change. Though they became disillusioned by the later violence (the Reign of Terror), the revolutionary spirit of radical individualism and freedom remained a core influence.

2.  **The Industrial Revolution:** This was the massive shift from a rural, agricultural society to an urban, industrialized one. The Romantics reacted strongly against the negative effects of industrialization: the polluted, overcrowded cities; the dehumanizing factory work; and the destruction of the natural landscape. This reaction fueled their idealization of nature.

3.  **Reaction Against The Enlightenment (The Age of Reason):** Romanticism was a direct backlash against what it saw as the cold, mechanical, and soulless rationalism of the Neo-Classical period. The Romantics believed that reason alone was not enough to understand human experience and that **imagination and emotion** were the true sources of wisdom and creativity.

---

#### **Key Characteristics of Romantic Literature**

*   **Emphasis on Emotion and Imagination:** The Romantics believed that feelings, intuition, and the creative power of the imagination were more important than logic and reason. Poetry was defined by Wordsworth as **"the spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings."**
*   **The Glorification of Nature:** Nature was not just a pretty backdrop; it was a living, spiritual force. It was seen as a source of solace, inspiration, and divine truth. The Romantics sought a deep, mystical connection with the natural world as an escape from the ugliness of industrial society.
*   **Focus on the Individual:** The poet became the central figure of the poem. Romantic literature celebrates the individual's subjective experience, thoughts, and feelings. The poet is often portrayed as a visionary, a prophet, or an outcast who sees the world differently.
*   **Interest in the Supernatural and the Exotic:** In rebellion against the "common sense" of the Neo-Classical age, the Romantics were fascinated by the supernatural, the mysterious, the medieval (the "Gothic"), and far-off, exotic lands. This is clearly seen in works like Coleridge's "Kubla Khan" and Keats's "La Belle Dame sans Merci."
*   **Use of Simple, "Common" Language:** In the preface to *Lyrical Ballads*, Wordsworth famously declared that he wanted to use the real language of common men, not the artificial, "poetic" diction of the previous century.
*   **Idealization of Childhood and the "Common Man":** The Romantics believed that children were closer to God and nature, possessing an innocence and wisdom that adults had lost. They also celebrated the humble, rustic life of the common man.

### **The Romantic Poets**

The movement is dominated by poetry. The poets are often divided into two generations.

#### **The First Generation (The Lake Poets)**

These were the pioneers of the movement. They are called the "Lake Poets" because they all lived for a time in the scenic Lake District of northern England.

*   **William Wordsworth (1770-1850):** The quintessential poet of nature. For Wordsworth, nature was a teacher, a healer, and a reflection of the divine.
    *   *Key Works:* ***Lyrical Ballads*** (co-authored with Coleridge), ***The Prelude*** (a long, autobiographical poem about the "growth of a poet's mind"), "Tintern Abbey," "Ode: Intimations of Immortality," "I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud."

*   **Samuel Taylor Coleridge (1772-1834):** The poet of the supernatural, the exotic, and the imagination. While Wordsworth found the sublime in the everyday, Coleridge found it in the strange and magical.
    *   *Key Works:* ***The Rime of the Ancient Mariner*** (his masterpiece in *Lyrical Ballads*), ***Kubla Khan*** (a famous dream-like, unfinished poem), "Christabel," and his major work of literary criticism, ***Biographia Literaria***, where he explains his theories of imagination.

*   **Robert Southey (1774-1843):** A prolific writer of poetry and prose. Though he was Poet Laureate, his reputation has not endured as well as that of Wordsworth and Coleridge.

#### **The Second Generation**

These poets were more radical and rebellious. They all died tragically young and lived passionate, unconventional lives.

*   **Lord Byron (1788-1824):** The "celebrity" of the group. He created the figure of the **Byronic Hero**: a dark, handsome, brooding, and rebellious outcast, haunted by a secret past. This figure appears in many of his works and was largely a reflection of his own public persona.
    *   *Key Works:* ***Childe Harold's Pilgrimage***, ***Don Juan*** (a long, satirical epic poem).

*   **Percy Bysshe Shelley (1792-1822):** The most revolutionary and idealistic of the Romantics. He was a radical atheist and political thinker who believed in the perfectibility of humankind and the power of poetry to reform the world. His poetry is known for its lyrical beauty and intellectual passion.
    *   *Key Works:* ***Prometheus Unbound*** (a lyrical drama), "Ode to the West Wind," "To a Skylark," "Ozymandias," and his critical essay, ***A Defence of Poetry***.

*   **John Keats (1795-1821):** Unlike the others, Keats was not concerned with politics or social reform. His poetry is a pure celebration of **beauty, art, and sensory experience**. He is famous for his concept of **Negative Capability**: the ability of a great artist to embrace uncertainty and mystery without needing to reach for facts and reason. He is the master of the **Ode**.
    *   *Key Works:* His great odes: "Ode to a Nightingale," "Ode on a Grecian Urn," "To Autumn." Also "La Belle Dame sans Merci" and his long poem *Endymion*.

---

#### **Romantic Prose**

While poetry dominated, the novel also developed during this time.

*   **Gothic Novel:** This genre, which began in the late 18th century, flourished under the Romantics' love for the supernatural and medieval.
    *   **Mary Shelley:** Her novel ***Frankenstein*** is a masterpiece of Gothic fiction that also explores Romantic themes of creation, nature vs. nurture, and the dangers of unchecked ambition.
*   **The Historical Novel:**
    *   **Sir Walter Scott:** He is considered the father of the historical novel. His novels, set in the Scottish past, were immensely popular across Europe. *Key Work:* ***Waverley***.
*   **The Novel of Manners:**
    *   **Jane Austen:** Though she wrote during the Romantic period, her novels are stylistically closer to the Neo-Classical ideals of reason, order, and social satire. She focused on the lives and manners of the landed gentry. *Key Works:* ***Pride and Prejudice***, ***Sense and Sensibility***, ***Emma***.

---
*The Romantic era's emphasis on emotion and individualism gradually gave way to the Victorian era's focus on social responsibility, morality, and the challenges of a modern, industrial nation.*
