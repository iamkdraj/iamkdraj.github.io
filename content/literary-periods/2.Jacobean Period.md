### The Jacobean Age (1603 – 1625)

The Jacobean Age is named after the reign of **King James I** (Jacobus is the Latin form of James). It is a direct continuation of the Elizabethan Renaissance, but with a noticeable shift in tone and mood. While the optimism and patriotic fervor of the Elizabethan era waned, the literature of this period became more critical, satirical, and psychologically complex.

---

#### **Context and Influences**

1.  **Change in Monarchy:** The transition from Queen Elizabeth I, the strong and iconic "Virgin Queen," to King James I, a Scottish king with different sensibilities, created a sense of uncertainty. James was a learned man but lacked Elizabeth's popular charisma.

2.  **Growing Political and Religious Tension:** The early seeds of the conflict that would later explode into the **English Civil War** were sown here. Tensions grew between the King and Parliament over issues of power and money. Religious conflicts between Anglicans, Puritans, and Catholics also intensified.
    *   **The Gunpowder Plot (1605):** A failed assassination attempt against King James I by a group of provincial English Catholics. This event dramatically increased anti-Catholic sentiment and a general sense of paranoia and conspiracy, which is reflected in the literature's fascination with plots and intrigue.

3.  **A Mood of Disillusionment:** The boundless optimism of the Elizabethan era gave way to a more realistic, and often cynical, view of humanity and society. The literature of this period is marked by a darker tone, a fascination with evil, and a questioning of traditional morality.

---

#### **Key Characteristics of Jacobean Literature**

*   **Darker Tone:** A shift from the romantic comedies and patriotic histories of Shakespeare to darker, more satirical, and often morbid themes.
*   **Satire and Cynicism:** A strong tendency to satirize the corruption of the court and the decay of morals in society.
*   **Psychological Depth:** Deeper exploration of the human mind, particularly its darker aspects like lust, revenge, and madness.
*   **Realism:** A move towards more realistic portrayals of contemporary life, especially in the comedies of Ben Jonson and Thomas Middleton.

---

### **Major Genres and Authors**

#### **1. Jacobean Drama**

Drama remained the dominant literary form, but it evolved significantly.

*   **The Revenge Tragedy:** This genre, started by Thomas Kyd, reached its peak in the Jacobean era. These plays are known for their sensational plots involving murder, ghosts demanding revenge, madness (both real and feigned), and a bloody, often corpse-strewn, final scene.
    *   **William Shakespeare:** Though he straddles both eras, his great, dark tragedies like *King Lear* and *Macbeth* were written in the Jacobean period and reflect its somber mood. *Hamlet*, though written at the cusp of the two ages, is the archetypal revenge tragedy.
    *   **John Webster:** The master of the dark, violent, and poetic revenge tragedy. His plays are filled with a sense of moral decay and human cruelty.
        *   *Key Works:* ***The Duchess of Malfi***, ***The White Devil***.
    *   **Cyril Tourneur:** Known for ***The Revenger's Tragedy***.

*   **City Comedy / Comedy of Manners:** A type of satirical comedy that focused on the lives, vices, and follies of the rising middle class in London.
    *   **Thomas Middleton:** A prolific and versatile dramatist who excelled at city comedy.
        *   *Key Works:* ***A Chaste Maid in Cheapside***, and collaborated with William Rowley on the tragedy ***The Changeling***.
    *   **Ben Jonson:** Continued to write his influential "Comedies of Humours," which were sharp satires of London life.

*   **Tragicomedy:** A new hybrid genre became very popular in this period. These plays mixed serious tragic elements with comedic ones and always ended with a happy, often improbable, resolution.
    *   **Francis Beaumont and John Fletcher:** A famous collaborative duo who perfected the tragicomedy. Their plays were immensely popular and even rivaled Shakespeare's in their time.
        *   *Key Work:* ***Philaster***.

#### **2. Jacobean Poetry: The Metaphysical Poets**

While the movement began with John Donne in the late Elizabethan era, the **Metaphysical Poets** truly flourished in the Jacobean and Caroline periods.

*   **Who they were:** Not a formal "school," but a group of poets who shared a similar style. The term "Metaphysical" was famously applied to them (somewhat critically) by **Dr. Samuel Johnson** in the 18th century. He described their style as one where "the most heterogeneous ideas are yoked by violence together."
*   **Key Characteristics:**
    *   **Intellectual & Witty:** The poetry is highly intellectual, argumentative, and uses logic and reason to explore emotional and spiritual themes.
    *   **The Metaphysical Conceit:** This is their most famous device. A **conceit** is a clever and unconventional extended metaphor that compares two very dissimilar things. For example, in "A Valediction: Forbidding Mourning," John Donne compares two lovers' souls to the two legs of a drafting compass.
    *   **Colloquial Language:** They used plain, direct, and often dramatic speech rhythms rather than smooth, "poetic" language.
    *   **Themes:** Love, death, and religion were their primary subjects.
*   **Major Poets:**
    *   **John Donne:** The leading figure. His poetry is passionate, dramatic, and intellectually rigorous.
    *   **George Herbert:** A deeply religious poet whose work explores his personal relationship with God.
    *   **Andrew Marvell:** Famous for poems like "To His Coy Mistress." (He also wrote in the Caroline and Commonwealth periods).
    *   **Henry Vaughan**, **Richard Crashaw**.

#### **3. Jacobean Prose**

*   **The King James Bible (1611):** Also known as the **Authorized Version**, this translation was a monumental achievement of English prose. Its powerful, simple, and rhythmic language had an enormous and lasting influence on all subsequent English literature.
*   **Francis Bacon:** Continued to write and publish his influential *Essays*, which are masterpieces of concise, aphoristic prose.
*   **Robert Burton:** Known for his huge and eccentric work, ***The Anatomy of Melancholy***, an exhaustive study of the subject of depression.

### **The Caroline Age (1625 – 1649)**

Named for the reign of **King Charles I** (Carolus is Latin for Charles), this period is the final phase of the Renaissance before the outbreak of the Civil War. It is largely a continuation of Jacobean trends.

*   **Context:** The political and religious tensions that began under James I intensified under Charles I, culminating in the **English Civil War (1642–1649)**. In 1642, the Puritan-led Parliament ordered the **closing of the theatres**, effectively ending the great age of English Renaissance drama. The period ends with the execution of King Charles I in 1649.

*   **Poetry: The Cavalier Poets**
    *   **Who they were:** A group of poets who were supporters of King Charles I (the "Cavaliers"). Their poetry was very different from the intellectual wrestling of the Metaphysicals.
    *   **Key Characteristics:** Light, elegant, and polished. They valued wit, charm, and classical grace.
    *   **Themes:** Their primary themes were love, beauty, honor, and loyalty to the king. They famously employed the theme of **"Carpe Diem"** (Latin for "Seize the Day").
    *   **Major Poets:** **Robert Herrick**, **Thomas Carew**, **Sir John Suckling**, and **Richard Lovelace**.

*The era that follows the execution of Charles I is the **Commonwealth Period** or **Interregnum** (1649-1660), a time when England was a republic under Puritan rule. The next major literary period is the **Restoration** in 1660.*
