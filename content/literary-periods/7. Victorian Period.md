### The Victorian Period (c. 1837 – 1901)

The Victorian Period is named after the long reign of **Queen Victoria**. It was a time of immense and rapid change for Britain: the nation was at the peak of its global power as the center of the British Empire, and society was transformed by the full force of the **Industrial Revolution**.

This era of dramatic progress, however, was also filled with deep contradictions and anxieties. The literature of the period reflects this duality: it is a literature of confidence and doubt, faith and skepticism, progress and poverty.

---

#### **Context and Influences**

1.  **The Industrial Revolution and Urbanization:** This is the most dominant social force. Britain became the "workshop of the world." People flocked from the countryside to the cities, which grew at an astonishing rate. This led to incredible wealth for some, but also to widespread poverty, slum-like living conditions, and brutal factory work.

2.  **The Rise of the British Empire:** Britain's empire spanned the globe, bringing immense wealth and a strong sense of national pride and responsibility. This imperial mindset is a frequent theme, but it also prompted questions about race, colonization, and national identity.

3.  **A Crisis of Faith: The Conflict Between Science and Religion:** This is the central intellectual conflict of the age.
    *   **Charles Darwin's *On the Origin of Species* (1859):** This book, which introduced the theory of evolution by natural selection, dealt a seismic shock to traditional religious beliefs. It challenged the biblical account of creation and seemed to suggest that humanity was not God's special creation, but merely another animal shaped by blind, natural forces.
    *   **Influence:** This conflict created a deep spiritual and intellectual anxiety, which is a major theme in the works of poets like **Alfred, Lord Tennyson** and **Matthew Arnold**.

4.  **Intellectual Movements:**
    *   **Utilitarianism:** A philosophy, championed by Jeremy Bentham, which argued that the best action is the one that produces "the greatest good for the greatest number." It was a practical, data-driven philosophy that was often seen as cold and mechanical, ignoring the emotional and spiritual needs of individuals. **Charles Dickens** frequently satirized its effects in his novels.
    *   **The Oxford Movement (or Tractarianism):** A religious movement within the Church of England that sought to bring back some of the older, more ritualistic traditions of Catholicism. Its most famous figure was **John Henry Newman**.

---

#### **Key Characteristics of Victorian Literature**

*   **Realism:** The **novel** became the dominant literary form, and its primary mode was realism. Victorian novelists sought to create a detailed, realistic, and panoramic portrait of their society, exploring its social, economic, and political problems.
*   **Moral Purpose and Didacticism:** Victorians believed that literature should be a tool for social improvement. It was meant to be morally uplifting and to expose social ills like poverty, class inequality, and the exploitation of workers.
*   **A Sense of Social Responsibility:** There was a strong feeling that writers had a duty to comment on the major issues of their day.
*   **Idealism and Doubt:** The literature is marked by a deep conflict. On one hand, there is a belief in progress, duty, and national greatness. On the other, there is a profound sense of anxiety, doubt, and nostalgia for a simpler past, brought on by the rapid changes in society and the crisis of faith.
*   **Strict Morality (The "Victorian Compromise"):** Victorian society placed a high value on public morality, duty, and propriety. However, the literature often exposes the hypocrisy and contradictions that lay beneath this respectable surface.

---

### **Major Genres, Authors, and Movements**

#### **1. The Victorian Novel**

This was the great age of the English novel.
*   **Charles Dickens:** The most popular and iconic novelist of the era. His novels are famous for their unforgettable characters, complex plots, and passionate critique of social injustice.
    *   *Key Works:* ***Oliver Twist***, ***David Copperfield***, ***Bleak House***, ***Great Expectations***.
*   **The Brontë Sisters:**
    *   **Charlotte Brontë:** Known for her passionate and psychologically intense novels. *Key Work:* ***Jane Eyre***.
    *   **Emily Brontë:** Wrote only one novel, the dark, wild, and powerful masterpiece ***Wuthering Heights***, which stands apart from the realism of the era.
*   **George Eliot (Mary Ann Evans):** A highly intellectual novelist known for her deep psychological insight and moral seriousness.
    *   *Key Works:* ***Middlemarch*** (often considered the greatest novel in English), ***The Mill on the Floss***, ***Silas Marner***.
*   **Thomas Hardy:** A late Victorian novelist and poet. His novels are deeply pessimistic and tragic, portraying characters who are victims of fate and a harsh, indifferent universe.
    *   *Key Works:* ***Tess of the d'Urbervilles***, ***The Mayor of Casterbridge***, ***Jude the Obscure***.

#### **2. Victorian Poetry**

*   **Alfred, Lord Tennyson:** The official Poet Laureate for much of Victoria's reign and the representative voice of the age. His poetry perfectly captures the Victorian conflict between faith and doubt, and the nostalgia for the past.
    *   *Key Works:* ***In Memoriam A.H.H.*** (a long elegy that grapples with grief and religious doubt), "Ulysses," "The Lady of Shalott," "The Charge of the Light Brigade."
*   **Robert Browning:** Known for perfecting the **dramatic monologue**.
    *   **Term:** A **dramatic monologue** is a poem in which a single speaker (who is not the poet) addresses a silent listener, and in the process, inadvertently reveals their own character and psychology.
    *   *Key Works:* "My Last Duchess," "Andrea del Sarto," "Fra Lippo Lippi."
*   **Matthew Arnold:** A poet and influential critic. His poetry is marked by a sense of melancholy and alienation, lamenting the loss of faith in the modern world.
    *   *Key Works:* "Dover Beach," "The Scholar-Gipsy."

#### **3. Sub-Movements in Art and Literature**

*   **The Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood (PRB):** A group of painters and poets who rejected the academic, conventional art of their time. They were inspired by the art of the early Italian Renaissance (before Raphael) and created works that were highly detailed, intensely colored, and often sensual.
    *   *Key Figures:* **Dante Gabriel Rossetti** (a leader of the group), his sister **Christina Rossetti** (a major poet), William Morris.
*   **Aestheticism and the "Art for Art's Sake" Movement:** This late Victorian movement was a direct rebellion against the moralistic and didactic view of art. Aesthetes argued that art should be judged on its own terms—for its beauty alone—and did not need to serve any moral or social purpose. Its famous slogan was **"Art for Art's Sake."**
    *   *Key Figures:* **Oscar Wilde** (the most famous representative), **Walter Pater**.

---
*The death of Queen Victoria in 1901 marks the end of this era and the beginning of the **Modern Period**, which brought with it a radical break from Victorian conventions in both art and thought.*
