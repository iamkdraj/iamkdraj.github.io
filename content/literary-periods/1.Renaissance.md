### The Renaissance Period (c. 1500 – 1660)

The word "Renaissance" is French for "Rebirth." This period marks a gradual but profound shift away from the medieval world and a "rebirth" of interest in the classical learning, art, and literature of ancient Greece and Rome. Originating in Italy in the 14th century, its influence slowly spread across Europe, reaching England around the early 16th century.

---

#### **Context and Influences**

To understand the literature, you must understand the world that produced it. Four major factors shaped the English Renaissance:

1.  **Humanism:** This is the single most important intellectual movement of the period.
    *   **What it is:** Humanism was a philosophical and cultural shift from the medieval, God-centered view of the world to a *human-centered* one. It did not reject God, but rather celebrated human beings as God's finest creation. It emphasized human potential, reason, and the importance of life on Earth, not just the afterlife.
    *   **Key Idea:** The ideal "Renaissance Man" was one who developed all his talents—intellectual, artistic, and physical—to the fullest.
    *   **Influence:** It led to a new curriculum focused on classics, history, and rhetoric. Key figures like **Erasmus** and **Sir Thomas More** were central to bringing Humanism to England.

2.  **The Protestant Reformation:**
    *   **What it is:** The religious movement that challenged the authority of the Roman Catholic Church, leading to the establishment of Protestant churches. In England, this was famously initiated by **King Henry VIII's** break from Rome.
    *   **Influence:** The Reformation put immense emphasis on the individual's direct relationship with God, without needing a priest as an intermediary. This spurred the translation of the **Bible into English** (most notably the **King James Version** in 1611), which standardized the English language and made literature's most important text accessible to common people.

3.  **The Age of Discovery:**
    *   **What it is:** A period of extensive overseas exploration by European powers. Figures like Columbus, Vasco da Gama, and England's own **Sir Francis Drake** and **Sir Walter Raleigh** were circumnavigating the globe.
    *   **Influence:** These voyages broadened intellectual horizons, fueled a sense of national pride and adventure, and brought immense wealth and new ideas to England. This spirit of exploration and ambition is visible everywhere in the literature.

4.  **The Invention of the Printing Press:**
    *   **What it is:** Introduced to England by **William Caxton** in 1476.
    *   **Influence:** This was the internet of its day. It allowed for the rapid and widespread distribution of books and ideas. It made literature more accessible beyond the clergy and the very rich, fueling literacy and the spread of Humanist and Reformation thought.

---

#### **Key Characteristics of Renaissance Literature**

*   **Spirit of Humanism:** The literature celebrated human potential and explored complex human characters, their internal conflicts, ambitions, and passions.
*   **Nationalism and Patriotism:** Especially after the defeat of the **Spanish Armada** in 1588, there was an outpouring of nationalistic fervor, evident in Shakespeare's history plays and other works.
*   **Richness and Experimentation:** The period saw a great flowering of the English language. Authors experimented with new words and, most importantly, new literary forms, especially the **sonnet**.
*   **Classical Influence:** Writers drew heavily on Greek and Roman mythology, history, and literary models (like the epic and tragedy).
*   **Optimism and Boundless Spirit:** There was a strong sense of optimism and a belief in unlimited human and national potential.

---

### **Major Sub-Periods and Authors**

The English Renaissance is typically divided into two major phases.

#### **1. The Early Tudor Period / Age of Transition (c. 1500–1558)**

This was a preparatory phase where Renaissance ideas began to take root in England.

*   **Poetry:**
    *   **Sir Thomas Wyatt** and **Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey:** These two are extremely important. **They introduced the sonnet into English literature**, adapting the form from the Italian poet Petrarch. Wyatt established the form, and Surrey refined it, creating the "English" or "Shakespearean" sonnet structure (three quatrains and a concluding couplet) and also pioneering the use of **blank verse**.
    *   **Tottel's Miscellany (1557):** The first printed anthology of English poetry, which included the works of Wyatt and Surrey, making their innovations widely available.

*   **Prose:**
    *   **Sir Thomas More:** A key English humanist. His most famous work, ***Utopia***, describes an ideal, fictional island society and is a classic of political philosophy.

#### **2. The Elizabethan Age (1558–1603)**

Often called the "Golden Age" of English literature, this period saw an incredible explosion of literary genius, particularly in drama.

*   **Drama (The Dominant Genre):**
    *   **The University Wits:** A group of educated, witty, and often wild young playwrights who transformed English drama before Shakespeare. They established the foundations for the masterpieces to come.
        *   **Christopher Marlowe:** The greatest of the University Wits. His plays are known for their powerful protagonists who are consumed by ambition. He perfected **blank verse** into what Ben Jonson called the **"mighty line."**
            *   *Key Works:* ***Dr. Faustus*** (a man who sells his soul to the devil for knowledge), ***Tamburlaine the Great*** (a shepherd who becomes a world-conquering emperor).
        *   **Thomas Kyd:** Famous for ***The Spanish Tragedy***, which established the genre of the **Revenge Tragedy**. This genre, with its themes of murder, ghosts, and bloody vengeance, was hugely popular and influenced Shakespeare's *Hamlet*.
        *   *Other Wits:* Robert Greene, George Peele, John Lyly.

    *   **William Shakespeare (1564-1616):** The towering figure of the age and perhaps of all English literature.
        *   *Tragedies:* Explored the downfall of great men through a fatal flaw (*Hamlet*, *Othello*, *King Lear*, *Macbeth*).
        *   *Comedies:* Often involved love, mistaken identity, and a happy resolution (*A Midsummer Night's Dream*, *As You Like It*, *Twelfth Night*).
        *   *Histories:* Dramatized English history with patriotic flair (*Richard III*, *Henry IV Part 1 & 2*, *Henry V*).
        *   *Poetry:* Famous for his sequence of 154 **Sonnets**.

    *   **Ben Jonson:** A contemporary and rival of Shakespeare. He was a classical scholar who championed order and realism in drama.
        *   *Key Contribution:* He is most famous for his **Comedy of Humours**.
            *   **Term:** *Comedy of Humours* is a type of comedy where each character is dominated by a single trait, or "humour" (e.g., greed, jealousy, foolish pride), leading to satirical and funny situations.
        *   *Key Works:* ***Every Man in His Humour***, ***Volpone, or The Fox*** (a satire on greed), ***The Alchemist***.

*   **Poetry:**
    *   **Edmund Spenser:** Called "the Poet's Poet" for his mastery of verse and his influence on later generations.
        *   *Key Work:* ***The Faerie Queene***, a monumental and unfinished allegorical epic poem celebrating Queen Elizabeth I and Protestant virtues.
    *   **Sir Philip Sidney:** The ideal "Renaissance Man."
        *   *Key Works:* ***Astrophel and Stella*** (a major sonnet sequence that sparked a craze for sonnets) and ***An Apology for Poetry*** (a foundational work of English literary criticism).

*   **Prose:**
    *   **Francis Bacon:** Known as the "Father of the English Essay." His ***Essays*** are famous for their **aphoristic style**—short, witty, and wise statements that are easily quotable (e.g., "Some books are to be tasted, others to be swallowed, and some few to be chewed and digested.").
