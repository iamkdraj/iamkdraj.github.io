### 1. There is an hourly service of trains to Kota, but the buses don't run hourly. The words "Hourly" used in the above sentence are –
> [!tip]- (1) Adverb, Adverb
> ❌ Incorrect. The first 'hourly' modifies 'service' (a noun), so it is an adjective.

> [!tip]- (2) Adjective, Adjective
> ❌ Incorrect. The second 'hourly' modifies 'run' (a verb), so it is an adverb.

> [!tip]- (3) Adverb, Adjective
> ❌ Incorrect. The roles are reversed.

> [!tip]- (4) Adjective, Adverb
> ✅ Correct.
> - **an hourly service:** 'hourly' describes the noun 'service', making it an adjective.
> - **don't run hourly:** 'hourly' describes how the buses run (modifying the verb 'run'), making it an adverb.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> Words like 'hourly', 'daily', 'weekly', and 'monthly' can function as both adjectives and adverbs.
> - As an **adjective**, it modifies a noun: `an hourly wage`, `a daily newspaper`.
> - As an **adverb**, it modifies a verb: `He is paid hourly`, `The paper is delivered daily`.

### 5. Choose the sentence in which verb does not agree with its subject
> [!tip]- (1) Fifty percent of the pages have not been typed well.
> ❌ Incorrect. The verb agrees. When the subject is a fraction or percentage, the verb agrees with the noun in the prepositional phrase. 'Pages' is plural, so 'have' is correct.

> [!tip]- (2) The Reserve Bank of India's decision to waive ATM charges has put banks in a difficult position.
> ❌ Incorrect. The verb agrees. The simple subject is 'decision' (singular), not 'charges' or 'banks'. Therefore, the singular verb 'has put' is correct.

> [!tip]- (3) One of the issues which was discussed in the meaning was raised by Suresh.
> ✅ Correct. The verb does **not** agree. The relative clause "which was discussed" refers to 'issues' (plural). Therefore, the verb should be plural: "One of the issues which **were** discussed...". The main verb 'was raised' is correct because its subject is 'One'.

> [!tip]- (4) There are a lot of schemes for senior citizens to invest money.
> ❌ Incorrect. The verb agrees. In sentences beginning with 'There is/are', the verb agrees with the noun that follows. 'a lot of schemes' is plural, so 'are' is correct.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The error is in subject-verb agreement within a relative clause. The subject of the verb `was discussed` is the relative pronoun `which`. `Which` refers back to its antecedent, `issues`. Since `issues` is plural, the verb in the relative clause must also be plural (`were discussed`).

### 9. Which of the following is not an example of Co-ordination-
> [!tip]- (1) The football game has been postponed so we have to do something else.
> ❌ Incorrect. This is co-ordination. `so` is a co-ordinating conjunction.

> [!tip]- (2) The football game has been postponed; we have to do something else.
> ❌ Incorrect. This is co-ordination. A semicolon (;) is used to join two closely related independent clauses.

> [!tip]- (3) Since the football game has been postponed, we have to do something else.
> ✅ Correct. This is **not** co-ordination. The word `Since` is a **subordinating conjunction**, which makes the first clause ("Since the football game has been postponed") a dependent adverbial clause. This structure creates a complex sentence, not a co-ordinate (compound) one.

> [!tip]- (4) The football game has been postponed therefore we have to do something else.
> ❌ Incorrect. This is co-ordination. `therefore` is a conjunctive adverb, which can be used with a semicolon to link independent clauses.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> Co-ordination joins two equal, independent clauses. Subordination makes one clause dependent on the other.
> - **Co-ordinating:** using a co-ordinating conjunction (`so`), a semicolon (`;`), or a conjunctive adverb (`therefore`).
> - **Subordinating:** using a subordinating conjunction (`since`, `because`, `while`, `if`).
> Sentence (3) is the only one that uses subordination.

### 10. Your story is very ridiculous. I cannot believe it. Combine the above sentences into a complex one –
> (1) I cannot believe you story because it is very ridiculous.
> (2) Your story is so ridiculous that I cannot believe it.
> (3) Your story is so ridiculous so I cannot believe it.
> (4) Your story is so ridiculous therefore I cannot believe it.

> [!tip]- (1) only 4
> ❌ Incorrect. (4) creates a compound sentence.

> [!tip]- (2) 1, 2 & 4
> ❌ Incorrect. (4) is a compound sentence.

> [!tip]- (3) 1 & 2
> ✅ Correct. Both sentences (1) and (2) are complex sentences.
> - (1) uses the subordinating conjunction `because` to create an adverbial clause of reason.
> - (2) uses the `so...that` structure to create an adverbial clause of result.

> [!tip]- (4) 2, 3, & 4
> ❌ Incorrect. (3) and (4) create compound sentences, not complex ones. `so` and `therefore` are co-ordinating.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> A **complex sentence** contains one independent clause and at least one dependent (subordinate) clause.
> - **(1) Complex:** "I cannot believe your story" (independent) + "because it is very ridiculous" (dependent).
> - **(2) Complex:** "Your story is so ridiculous" (independent) + "that I cannot believe it" (dependent).
> - **(3) & (4) Compound:** `so` and `therefore` are co-ordinating conjunctions/adverbs linking two independent clauses.

### 12. ................... I would type it myself. Supply with the correct subordinate clause -
> [!tip]- (1) If I would have a computer
> ❌ Incorrect. We do not use `would` in the `if`-clause of a standard conditional sentence.

> [!tip]- (2) If I have had a computer
> ❌ Incorrect. This is the present perfect tense, which does not fit the structure of the main clause (`would type`).

> [!tip]- (3) Unless I had a computer
> ❌ Incorrect. `Unless` means "if not". The sentence would mean "If I did not have a computer, I would type it myself," which is illogical.

> [!tip]- (4) If I had a computer
> ✅ Correct. This creates a standard **second conditional** sentence. The structure is `If + past simple` in the subordinate clause, and `would + base verb` in the main clause. It is used for hypothetical or unlikely situations in the present or future.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The main clause, "I would type it myself," uses the `would + verb` structure, which signals a second or third conditional. A second conditional sentence describes a hypothetical situation. The correct structure is:
> **If-clause:** `If + Subject + Past Simple` (`If I had...`)
> **Main clause:** `Subject + would + base verb` (`...I would type...`)

### 19. As soon as I saw the advertisement, I e-mailed my biodata. Transform the above sentence into negative one-
> [!tip]- (1) No sooner did I see the advertisement, than I e-mailed my bio-data.
> ✅ Correct. The structure "No sooner... than" is a standard way to transform a sentence with "As soon as...". It requires inversion in the first clause (`did I see` instead of `I saw`) and the use of `than` to connect the two clauses. This structure has a negative word ('No') at the beginning.

> [!tip]- (2) No sooner did I saw the advertisement, than I e-mailed my bio-data.
> ❌ Incorrect. After `did`, the base form of the verb (`see`) must be used, not the past tense (`saw`).

> [!tip]- (3) As soon as I did not see the advertisement, than I emailed my biodata.
> ❌ Incorrect. This changes the meaning completely.

> [!tip]- (4) As soon as I saw the advertisement, I did not e-mail my biodata.
> ❌ Incorrect. This negates the main clause, changing the meaning.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The transformation from `As soon as` to `No sooner... than` is a common exercise. The key grammatical rules are:
> 1.  Start the sentence with `No sooner`.
> 2.  Use subject-verb inversion in the first clause (like in a question): `did + subject + base verb`.
> 3.  Connect the two clauses with `than`.

### 20. She said to him, " Who are you you? Who are you looking for?"
> [!tip]- (1) She asked him who was he and who was he looking for.
> ❌ Incorrect. In reported speech, the word order in the clause should be statement order (subject-verb), not question order (verb-subject).

> [!tip]- (2) She asked him who he was and who he was looking for.
> ✅ Correct. This correctly changes `said to` to `asked`. It uses the correct statement word order (`who he was`, not `who was he`). The present tense (`are`) is correctly backshifted to the past tense (`was`).

> [!tip]- (3) She asked him who are you and who are you looking for.
> ❌ Incorrect. The tenses have not been backshifted from present to past.

> [!tip]- (4) She questioned him who he was and who was he looking for.
> ❌ Incorrect. The second clause has incorrect question word order.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> When reporting a Wh-question:
> 1.  Change the reporting verb to `asked`.
> 2.  Use the same Wh-word to connect the clauses.
> 3.  Change the word order from question form (verb-subject) to statement form (subject-verb).
> 4.  Backshift the tense (e.g., Present -> Past).
> Correct: "Who are you?" -> `who he was`.

### 22. My father said to me, "Do you know where he is opening a new shop?"

> ❌ Incorrect. The second embedded clause (`where was he...`) has incorrect question word order.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation

### 23. The teacher said to students, "Write what is written on the board."
> [!tip]- (1) The teacher ordered students that write what was written on the board.
> ❌ Incorrect. The structure `ordered ... that` is not used for commands. We use `ordered ... to`.

> [!tip]- (2) The teacher ordered students to write what was written on the board.
> ✅ Correct. The imperative command `"Write..."` is reported using the structure `ordered + object + to-infinitive` (`ordered students to write`). The tense inside the `what`-clause is correctly backshifted from present (`is written`) to past (`was written`).

### 48. What should a teacher not do while teaching a prose text in a class?
> [!tip]- (1) Explain all the new words included in the text.
> ✅ Correct. A teacher should **not** do this. Explaining every single new word is inefficient, time-consuming, and discourages students from developing the crucial skill of guessing meaning from context.

> [!tip]- (2) Leave out words whose meaning can be understood from the context.
> ❌ Incorrect. A teacher *should* do this to encourage contextual guessing.

> [!tip]- (3) Explain key word essential for understanding a text.
> ❌ Incorrect. A teacher *should* pre-teach or explain essential keywords that are crucial for overall comprehension.

> [!tip]- (4) Ask students to consult a dictionary at home and come prepared.
> ❌ Incorrect. This is a valid teaching strategy (part of a 'flipped classroom' approach).

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> Effective vocabulary teaching within a prose lesson involves a balanced approach. The teacher should strategically select and explain only the most critical words (key words). For other words, students should be encouraged to use their existing knowledge and the surrounding text to infer the meaning. Explaining every word is a poor pedagogical practice. *Note: This question was unattempted in the test.*

> Modern approaches to teaching poetry focus on helping students to appreciate the language, understand the themes, and respond personally to the work.
