> [!tip]- (4) All the students in the class are as intelligent as Ramesh.
> ❌ Incorrect. This changes the meaning completely.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> To change a comparative sentence like "X is better than most other Ys" to the positive degree, we use the structure "Very few Ys are as good as X." This transformation preserves the original meaning that X is in the top tier of its group. The key is using "very few" to indicate that while some might be equal, the vast majority are not.

### 14. Don't subject the animals to cruelty.

> ❌ Incorrect. This is a possible passive form, but `should` better captures the sense of advice or prohibition in the original command.

> [!tip]- (2) The animals shall not be subjected to cruelty
> ❌ Incorrect. `Shall` implies a very formal command or legal ruling, which is stronger than the original 'Don't...'.

> [!tip]- (3) The animals will not be subjected to cruelty

> [!tip]- (4) The animals should not be subjected to cruelty

> When changing an imperative (command) sentence to the passive voice, we often introduce a modal verb to reflect the mood of the command.

### 15. He was driving the car so fast that it skidded on the snowy road.
> [!tip]- (1) The car was driven by him so fast that it skidded on the snowy road
> ❌ Incorrect. The original sentence is in the Past Continuous tense (`was driving`). This option incorrectly uses the Simple Past passive (`was driven`).

> [!tip]- (2) The car was being driven by him so fast that it skidded on the snowy road
> ✅ Correct. To change the Past Continuous active (`was driving`) to the passive, we use the structure **`was/were + being + past participle`**. So, `was driving` becomes `was being driven`.

> [!tip]- (3) The car had been driven by him so fast that it skidded on the snowy road
> ❌ Incorrect. This is the Past Perfect passive, which does not match the original tense.

> [!tip]- (4) The car has been driven by him so fast that it skidded on the snowy road
> ❌ Incorrect. This is the Present Perfect passive, which does not match the original tense.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The rule for converting an active sentence in the Past Continuous tense to the passive voice is:
> **Active:** Subject + was/were + V-ing + Object
> **Passive:** Object + was/were + being + V3 (past participle) + by + Subject
> `He was driving the car` -> `The car was being driven by him`.

### 16. How much monthly rent did you pay for the house?
> [!tip]- (1) How much monthly rent was paid for the house
> ✅ Correct. The original question is in the Simple Past tense (`did you pay`). The passive form of the Simple Past is **`was/were + past participle`**. The passive form correctly omits the agent ('by you') as it is often done in passive questions when the agent is unknown or unimportant.

> [!tip]- (2) How much monthly rent is being paid for the house?
> ❌ Incorrect. This is Present Continuous passive.

> [!tip]- (3) How much monthly rent was being paid for the house?
> ❌ Incorrect. This is Past Continuous passive.

> [!tip]- (4) How much monthly rent is paid for the house
> ❌ Incorrect. This is Simple Present passive.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> To convert an active voice question in the Simple Past to the passive voice:
> **Active:** (Wh-word) + did + Subject + Verb (base) + ...?
> **Passive:** (Wh-word) + was/were + Object + Past Participle + ...?
> `...did you pay...` -> `...was ... paid...`

### 17. My grandmother believes that drinking a glass of milk at bedtime is essential for health.
> [!tip]- (1) It is believed by my grandmother that drinking a glass of milk at bedtime is essential for health.
> ✅ Correct. The main clause is "My grandmother believes..." which is in the Simple Present tense. The standard passive construction for a reporting verb like 'believes' is to use an introductory 'It'. The passive of `believes` (Simple Present) is `is believed`.

> [!tip]- (2) It is being believed by my grandmother...
> ❌ Incorrect. This is Present Continuous passive, which doesn't match the original tense.

> [!tip]- (3) It has been believed by my grandmother...
> ❌ Incorrect. This is Present Perfect passive.

> [!tip]- (4) It will have been believed by my grandmother...
> ❌ Incorrect. This is Future Perfect passive.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> When the active sentence has a reporting verb (like `believe`, `say`, `think`, `know`) followed by a `that`-clause, one common way to make it passive is with the introductory "It" construction:
> **Active:** Subject + reporting verb (Simple Present) + that-clause
> **Passive:** It + is/am/are + past participle + by + Subject + that-clause
> So, `My grandmother believes that...` becomes `It is believed by my grandmother that...`

### 18. The highest rate of inflation in India.
> [!tip]- (1) M M H M M
> ✅ Correct. This structure represents:
> - **M** (Pre-modifier): The
> - **M** (Pre-modifier): highest
> - **H** (Head): rate
> - **M** (Post-modifier): of inflation
> - **M** (Post-modifier): in India
This is a standard analysis of a Noun Phrase.

> [!tip]- (2) M M H M
> ❌ Incorrect. This was your choice. It misses one of the post-modifying prepositional phrases (`in India`).

> [!tip]- (3) H M M M
> ❌ Incorrect. 'The' and 'highest' are pre-modifiers, not part of the head.

> [!tip]- (4) M H M
> ❌ Incorrect. This misses several modifiers.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> In Noun Phrase (NP) analysis using the M-H-M structure:
> - **H (Head):** The central noun of the phrase (`rate`).
> - **M (Modifier):** Any word or phrase that describes the head.
>   - **Pre-modifiers** come before the head (`The`, `highest`).
>   - **Post-modifiers** come after the head (`of inflation`, `in India`).
> The phrase has two pre-modifiers and two post-modifiers, making the structure M M H M M.

### 19. Which of the following is the formal structure of Genitive Phrase
> [!tip]- (1) {M} H {M}

### 20. Which of the following is not true about Adjective Phrase -
> [!tip]- (1) In a clause AjPs (Adjective Phrases) functions as complement
> ❌ Incorrect. This is true. For example, in "He is **very happy**," the AjP "very happy" functions as a subject complement.

> [!tip]- (2) As subordinate phrases, AjPs can function as premodifiers in NPs
> ❌ Incorrect. This is true. For example, in "a **very tall** man," the AjP "very tall" pre-modifies the head noun "man".

> [!tip]- (3) The head of an adjective phrase is an adjective
> ❌ Incorrect. This is true by definition. The central word in an adjective phrase is an adjective.

> [!tip]- (4) Premodifiers are only PPs (Preposition Phrase)
> ✅ Correct. This statement is **not true**. Premodifiers in an adjective phrase are most commonly **adverbs of degree** (e.g., `very`, `quite`, `extremely`). Prepositional Phrases (PPs) typically function as *post*-modifiers, not pre-modifiers, in an adjective phrase (e.g., "happy **with the result**").

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> An **Adjective Phrase (AjP)** is a group of words with an adjective as its head.
> - **Structure:** {Premodifier} + Head (Adjective) + {Postmodifier}
> - **Premodifiers** are usually adverbs: `[extremely] happy`.
> - **Postmodifiers** can be PPs or clauses: `happy [with his score]`.
> The statement that premodifiers are *only* PPs is false.

### 23. Which of the following is not a characteristic of Epic
> [!tip]- (1) It begins with an invocation to a muse who provides inspiration and guides the poet
> ❌ Incorrect. This is a classic convention of epic poetry (e.g., Homer's *The Iliad*).

> [!tip]- (2) It is written in formal, elevated, dignified style
> ❌ Incorrect. This is a defining characteristic of the epic genre.

> ❌ Incorrect. The epic hero of great stature is central to the genre.

> [!tip]- (4) It is a first person narration.
