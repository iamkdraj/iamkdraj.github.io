### 9. I ......Rashmi since we both ......work on the same day at Thames college about five years ago. She is one of the smartest people I ...........
> [!tip]- (1) have known, started, have ever met
> ✅ Correct.

> - **started:** Simple past is used for a completed action at a specific time in the past ("five years ago").

> - **Simple Past (`started`):** Use for a finished action at a specific past time (indicated by "five years ago").

### 10. When I ..........her house I ...........that she ........all the five children and ...........suicide.
> [!tip]- (1) reached, found, had killed, had committed
> ✅ Correct. The actions of killing and committing suicide happened *before* the speaker reached the house and found out. The Past Perfect ('had killed', 'had committed') is used for the earlier past actions, and the Simple Past ('reached', 'found') is used for the later past actions.

> [!tip]- (2) had reached, found, killed, committed
> ❌ Incorrect. 'had reached' would imply reaching the house was the earliest action, which doesn't fit the sequence. The discovery happened upon arrival.

> [!tip]- (3) reach, found, has killed, has committed
> ❌ Incorrect. This mixes present tense ('reach') and present perfect ('has killed') with past tense ('found'), which is grammatically inconsistent.

> [!tip]- (4) reached, had found, killed, committed
> ❌ Incorrect. 'had found' is illogical. The finding happened when the speaker reached the house, not before.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> When describing a sequence of events in the past, the **Simple Past** is used for the main narrative events. The **Past Perfect (had + past participle)** is used to describe actions that were completed *before* the main narrative events took place.
> - **Main Narrative (Simple Past):** When I **reached** her house, I **found**...
> - **Earlier Action (Past Perfect):** ...that she **had killed** the children and **had committed** suicide. (These events were finished before the arrival).

### 11. Choose the incorrect sentence (Use of Tense)
(1) I had been thinking about that before you mentioned it.
(2) I thought we have won.
(3) The students had just opened their books when the fire alarm went off.
(4) She had written several books of poetry in the last year of her life.

> [!tip]- (1) 1 and 3
> ❌ Incorrect. Sentence (1) and (3) are both grammatically correct. They correctly use the past perfect tense to describe an action that happened before another past action.

> [!tip]- (2) 3 and 4
> ❌ Incorrect. Sentence (3) is correct. While sentence (4) is incorrect, this option omits the other incorrect sentence, (2).

> [!tip]- (3) only 2
> ❌ Incorrect. While sentence (2) is indeed incorrect, sentence (4) is also incorrect, so this option is incomplete. Your choice was incorrect because it failed to identify the error in sentence (4).

> [!tip]- (4) 2 and 4
> ✅ Correct. Both sentences (2) and (4) contain tense errors.
> - **Sentence (2):** "I thought we have won." is wrong. The main verb is in the past ('thought'), so the subordinate clause should also be in a past tense. It should be "I thought we **had won**" or "I thought we **won**".
> - **Sentence (4):** "She had written... in the last year of her life." The phrase "in the last year of her life" specifies a completed time period in the past. This requires the **Simple Past tense**, not the Past Perfect. It should be "She **wrote**...".

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> - **Sequence of Tenses:** When the main verb of a sentence is in the past tense, the verb in the subordinate clause is usually also in a past tense. "I thought (past) we have won (present perfect)" violates this rule.
> - **Simple Past vs. Past Perfect:** The Simple Past is used for actions that occurred and were completed at a specific time in the past (e.g., "in the last year of her life"). The Past Perfect ('had written') is used to show that one past action happened *before* another past action. Since there is only one action mentioned in sentence (4) with a specific finished time, Simple Past is correct.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation

> - **Inductive Teaching (Example-Driven):** Starts with examples and asks students to infer the rule from them. This is a "bottom-up" approach.
> GTM is the classic example of a deductive teaching method.

### 49. Inductive Method in teaching grammar focuses on:
> [!tip]- (1) The teacher telling the rules first to the student and later applying them in different situations.
> ❌ Incorrect. This describes the deductive method.

> [!tip]- (2) The teacher presents certain examples before the students and then frames the rules with their help.
> ✅ Correct. This is the definition of the **Inductive Method**. It is a process of discovery where learners observe patterns in examples and generalize them into a rule.

> [!tip]- (3) Mastery of the rules of grammar.
> ❌ Incorrect. This is the goal of any grammar teaching, but it doesn't describe the *process* of the inductive method. The focus is on the discovery process, not just the end result.

> ❌ Incorrect. Option (1) is the opposite of the inductive method.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The **Inductive Method** of teaching is a "bottom-up" approach. It moves from specific observations to broader generalizations. In language teaching, this means:
> 1.  **Presentation:** Students are exposed to language in context (e.g., through a text or examples).
> 2.  **Noticing:** Students are guided to notice the grammatical pattern.
> 3.  **Generalization:** Students (with the teacher's help) formulate the grammar rule.
