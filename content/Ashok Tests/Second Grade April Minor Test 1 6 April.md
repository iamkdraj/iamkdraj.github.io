### 2. Which of the following is true about the aspects Romanticism?

> ✅ Correct. This describes the introspective and autobiographical nature of much Romantic poetry (e.g., Wordsworth's *The Prelude*), focusing on the poet's personal feelings, experiences, and spiritual development.

> (1) 2 and 4
> (2) 2, 3, and 4
> (3) 1, 2, 3, and 4
> (4) 1, 2 and 3

> All four statements represent central tenets of Romanticism. The movement celebrated:
> 1.  **Untamed Nature:** A shift from manicured gardens to wild, awe-inspiring landscapes.

### 8. ...... involves a situation in a play or a narrative in which the audience or reader shares with the author knowledge of present or future circumstances of which a character is ignorant.

> [!tip]- (1) Dramatic Irony
> ✅ Correct. This is the precise definition of dramatic irony. The audience's superior knowledge creates tension, suspense, or a sense of tragedy.

> [!tip]- (2) Verbal Irony
> ❌ Incorrect. Verbal irony is when a speaker says something but means the opposite (sarcasm is a common form).

> [!tip]- (3) Cosmic Irony
> ❌ Incorrect. Cosmic irony (or irony of fate) is the idea that a higher power (like God or fate) is toying with human expectations, often leading to a tragic or grimly humorous outcome.

> [!tip]- (4) Romantic Irony
> ❌ Incorrect. Romantic irony occurs when the author or narrator breaks the fictional illusion by acknowledging the work's artificiality, reminding the reader that they are reading a story.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The description provided in the question perfectly matches the literary device known as **dramatic irony**. A classic example is in Sophocles' *Oedipus Rex*, where the audience knows from the beginning that Oedipus has killed his father and married his mother, while Oedipus himself spends the entire play trying to discover the killer's identity. This gap in knowledge between the audience and the character is the essence of dramatic irony.

> ❌ Incorrect. This is a classic example of Juvenalian satire, which is a formal, harsh, and indignant critique of societal ills.

> [!tip]- (3) “The Importance of Being Earnest” by Oscar Wilde
> ❌ Incorrect. This is a comedy of manners and a Horatian satire, which gently mocks the follies and absurdities of the Victorian upper class.

> [!tip]- (4) “Candide” by Voltaire

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation

It typically features a prose narrative with a loose structure, frequent digressions, a mix of styles, and a debate-like quality. Voltaire's **_Candide_** fits this description perfectly as it systematically dismantles the philosophy that "all is for the best in the best of all possible worlds" through a series of chaotic and tragicomic events.

### 12. Onomatopoeia is often used to:

> [!tip]- (1) Provide literal descriptions
> ❌ Incorrect. Onomatopoeia is figurative and auditory, not primarily for literal, objective description.

> [!tip]- (2) Convey sensory experiences and create vivid imagery
> ✅ Correct. By imitating sounds (e.g., "buzz," "crash," "hiss"), onomatopoeia directly appeals to the sense of hearing, which in turn creates vivid mental images and a stronger sensory experience for the reader.

> [!tip]- (3) Minimize the impact of a statement
> ❌ Incorrect. Onomatopoeia usually heightens the impact of a statement by making the action more immediate and real, rather than minimizing it.

> [!tip]- (4) Avoid using descriptive language
> ❌ Incorrect. Onomatopoeia is a *form* of descriptive language, specifically auditory description. It doesn't avoid description; it enhances it.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> Onomatopoeia is the process of creating a word that phonetically imitates, resembles, or suggests the sound that it describes. Its primary literary function is to enhance the reader's sensory engagement with the text. By using words like *splash*, *murmur*, or *bang*, a writer can make a scene more vivid and immersive. This directly contributes to creating powerful imagery and conveying a specific sensory experience (in this case, auditory).

The title is a direct and literal translation of the Bengali compound word.
