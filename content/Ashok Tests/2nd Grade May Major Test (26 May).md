### 1. The wind was blowing dead against us. The word ‘dead’ used in the above sentence is –
> [!tip]- (1) Adjective
> ❌ Incorrect. This was your choice. 'Dead' is not modifying a noun here.

> [!tip]- (2) Adverb
> ✅ Correct. The word 'dead' is used here as an adverb of degree, modifying the prepositional phrase 'against us'. It means 'exactly' or 'directly'.

> [!tip]- (3) Noun
> ❌ Incorrect. 'Dead' is not functioning as a noun.

> [!tip]- (4) Preposition
> ❌ Incorrect. 'Dead' is not a preposition.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> While 'dead' is most commonly an adjective (e.g., `a dead tree`), it can also function as an adverb meaning 'completely' or 'absolutely'. It intensifies the word or phrase that follows. Other examples include: `dead right`, `dead serious`, `dead center`. Here, it intensifies the direction `against us`.

### 2. Identify the sentence in which the word ‘direct’ has been used as Adverb -
> (1) This train goes direct to Hyderabad.
> (2) I shall communicate with you direct.
> (3) This door allows direct access from the kitchen to the garage.
> (4) She prefers to act rather than direct.

> [!tip]- (1) 1 and 3
> ❌ Incorrect. In (3), 'direct' is an adjective modifying 'access'.

> [!tip]- (2) 2, 3 and 4
> ❌ Incorrect. (3) is an adjective and (4) is a verb.

> [!tip]- (3) 1 and 2
> ✅ Correct. In both sentences, 'direct' is a flat adverb (an adverb with the same form as the adjective) modifying the verb.
> - (1) `goes direct`: It answers *how* the train goes.
> - (2) `communicate ... direct`: It answers *how* I will communicate.

> [!tip]- (4) 1, 2 and 3
> ❌ Incorrect. (3) is an adjective.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> The word 'direct' can be:
> - **Adjective:** when it describes a noun (e.g., `a direct flight`, `direct access`). This is its function in (3).
> - **Adverb:** when it modifies a verb, meaning 'without stopping or deviating' (e.g., `fly direct`). This is its function in (1) and (2).
> - **Verb:** when it is the action of the sentence (e.g., `to direct a film`). This is its function in (4).

> - **few:** not many (negative implication).
> - **the few:** the small, specific number that exists.
> Because the books are specified by "that he borrowed from me," the correct determiner is `the few`.

### 12. When I ..........my dinner I went to bed.
> [!tip]- (1) had
> ❌ Incorrect. This would mean the two actions happened at the same time, which is less logical than one happening after the other.

> [!tip]- (2) had had
> ✅ Correct. This is the **Past Perfect** tense (`had + past participle of have`). It is used to show that the action of having dinner was completed *before* the action of going to bed.

> [!tip]- (3) have had
> ❌ Incorrect. This is the Present Perfect, which doesn't fit the past tense of the main clause (`went`).

> [!tip]- (4) had have
> ❌ Incorrect. This is grammatically impossible.

> [!info]- 📘 In-depth Explanation
> When describing two sequential actions in the past, we use the Past Perfect for the action that happened first.
> - **First Action:** Having dinner (`had had my dinner`)
> - **Second Action:** Going to bed (`I went to bed`)
> The structure `had had`, while looking strange, is the grammatically correct Past Perfect form of the verb 'to have'.
