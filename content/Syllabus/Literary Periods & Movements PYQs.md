## Literary Periods & Movements
### I. Renaissance Period

#### Q. 66 (STS-32, 2011/13): Which of the following texts does not belong to the Renaissance period ?
> [!tip]- Thomas Malory’s - Le’Morte D Arthur
> ✅ Correct! This work was completed around 1470 and published in 1485. It is a cornerstone of **late Medieval literature** and predates the main English Renaissance period (which began c. 1500).

> [!tip]- Richard Tottel’s - Miscellany.
> ❌ Incorrect. *Tottel's Miscellany* (1557) is a landmark anthology of the **early Renaissance** (specifically the Elizabethan era). It was instrumental in popularizing the sonnet form.

> [!tip]- John-Milton’s - Areopagitica
> ❌ Incorrect. *Areopagitica* (1644) was written by John Milton, a towering figure of the **late Renaissance** (specifically the Caroline/Commonwealth period).

> [!tip]- Holinshed’s Chronicles .
> ❌ Incorrect. *Holinshed's Chronicles* (first published 1577) was a major prose work of the **Elizabethan Renaissance** and a key source for Shakespeare.

#### Q. 70 (ST-22, 2011/12): The most important trait of the Renaissance period was -
> [!tip]- Humanism
> ✅ Correct! Humanism was the central intellectual movement of the Renaissance. It marked a shift from a God-centered to a human-centered worldview, celebrating human potential and reason.

> [!tip]- heroism
> ❌ Incorrect. While heroism was a common theme, it was a product of the period's core philosophy, which was Humanism. Humanism is the broader and more fundamental trait.

> [!tip]- adventure
> ❌ Incorrect. The spirit of adventure was a significant aspect of the Renaissance, but Humanism was the foundational *intellectual* movement that shaped the entire era.

> [!tip]- classical antiquity
> ❌ Incorrect. The "rebirth" of interest in classical antiquity was the *source* of Renaissance Humanism, but Humanism itself was the resulting philosophy that defined the period.

### II. Jacobean Period

#### Q. 14 (TSS-24): Jacobean age is called so after the name of the English King -
> [!tip]- Charles I
> ❌ Incorrect. The age named after Charles I is the **Caroline Age** (1625-1649). *Carolus* is the Latin for Charles.

> [!tip]- Jacob I
> ❌ Incorrect. There was no English King named Jacob I. This option is a distractor.

> [!tip]- James II
> ❌ Incorrect. James II reigned much later (1685-1688), during the Restoration period.

> [!tip]- James I
> ✅ Correct! The Jacobean Age (1603-1625) is named after King James I of England. The name comes from *Jacobus*, the Latin version of James.

#### Q. 64 (STS-32, 2011/13): ‘The Gun Powder Plot’ was the result of –
> [!tip]- Tension between Anglican and Roman Catholic Church.
> ✅ Correct! The Gunpowder Plot of 1605 was a failed assassination attempt against the Protestant King James I by a group of English Catholics. It was a direct result of religious tension.

> [!tip]- Tension between Charles I and Oliver Cromwell.
> ❌ Incorrect. This tension led to the **English Civil War**, which occurred much later (1642-1651).

> [!tip]- Tension between Charles I and his Feudal Lords.
> ❌ Incorrect. The major tension during Charles I's reign was with Parliament over issues of power and religion.

> [!tip]- Tension between English and French armies in 1628.
> ❌ Incorrect. The Gunpowder Plot was an internal, domestic conspiracy rooted in religious strife, not an international military conflict.

#### Q. 74 (STS-32, 2011/13): Who among the following is not a dramatist of the Jacobean period ?
> [!tip]- Thomas Kyd
> ✅ Correct! Thomas Kyd was a prominent dramatist of the **Elizabethan period**. His famous play, *The Spanish Tragedy* (c. 1587), was a major influence on Jacobean playwrights, but he belongs to the earlier generation.

> [!tip]- Thomas Middleton
> ❌ Incorrect. Thomas Middleton was a major Jacobean dramatist, known for his city comedies and tragedies like *The Changeling*.

> [!tip]- Thomas Heywood
> ❌ Incorrect. Thomas Heywood was a prolific playwright whose career spanned the Elizabethan and Jacobean periods, with much of his work produced in the Jacobean era.

> [!tip]- John Webster
> ❌ Incorrect. John Webster is one of the most famous Jacobean dramatists, the master of dark revenge tragedies like *The Duchess of Malfi*.

#### Q. 82 (ST-22, 2011/12): A period of visual and literary arts during the reign of James I of England came to be known as the -
> [!tip]- Tudor period
> ❌ Incorrect. The Tudor period ended with the death of Queen Elizabeth I in 1603, before James I took the throne.

> [!tip]- Jacobean period
> ✅ Correct! The Jacobean period is specifically defined by the reign of King James I (1603-1625).

> [!tip]- Caroline period
> ❌ Incorrect. The Caroline period corresponds to the reign of King Charles I (1625-1649).

> [!tip]- Metaphysical period
> ❌ Incorrect. "Metaphysical" refers to a style or movement of poetry that flourished *within* the Jacobean and Caroline periods, not a chronological period itself.

#### Q. 88 (ST-22, 2011/12): John Webster, Thomas Middleton and Philip Massinger belonged to which period?
> [!tip]- Victorian period
> ❌ Incorrect. The Victorian period was in the 19th century (1837-1901).

> [!tip]- Modern period
> ❌ Incorrect. The Modern period was in the early 20th century.

> [!tip]- Jacobean period
> ✅ Correct! John Webster, Thomas Middleton, and Philip Massinger were all major dramatists who flourished during the Jacobean Age (1603-125).

> [!tip]- Romantic period
> ❌ Incorrect. The Romantic period was from approximately 1798 to 1837.

### III. Metaphysical

#### Q. 68 (STS-32, 2011/13): Who has said that, "about the beginning of the seventeenth century there appeared a race of writers that may be termed metaphysical poets" ?
> [!tip]- Dr. Johnson
> ✅ Correct! Dr. Samuel Johnson coined the term "metaphysical poets" in his work *Lives of the Most Eminent English Poets*.

> [!tip]- John Dryden
> ❌ Incorrect. John Dryden noted that John Donne "affects the metaphysics," which influenced Johnson, but Johnson was the one who named and defined the group.

> [!tip]- Helen Gardner
> ❌ Incorrect. Dame Helen Gardner was a 20th-century critic who was instrumental in the modern revival and positive re-evaluation of the Metaphysical poets.

> [!tip]- Herbert Grierson
> ❌ Incorrect. Herbert Grierson's 1921 anthology, *Metaphysical Lyrics and Poems of the Seventeenth Century*, was a landmark publication that sparked modern interest in these poets.

#### Q. 69 (STS-32, 2011/13): Select the correct option. Two famous metaphysical poets are –
> [!tip]- Donne and Pope
> ❌ Incorrect. John Donne is a key Metaphysical poet, but Alexander Pope was the leading figure of the Neo-Classical age.

> [!tip]- Dryden and Pope
> ❌ Incorrect. Both John Dryden and Alexander Pope are major figures of the Neo-Classical period.

> [!tip]- Donne and Marvell
> ✅ Correct! John Donne is the father of the Metaphysical school, and Andrew Marvell ("To His Coy Mistress") is another central and famous Metaphysical poet.

> [!tip]- Cowley and Tennyson
> ❌ Incorrect. Abraham Cowley was a Metaphysical poet, but Alfred, Lord Tennyson was the quintessential poet of the Victorian Age.

#### Q. 103 (TSS-24): The comment “Most heterogeneous ideas are yoked by violence together” for Metaphysical poets was originally made by -
> [!tip]- Dr. Samuel Johnson
> ✅ Correct! This is Dr. Johnson's most famous and influential critique of the Metaphysical style, describing their use of complex and surprising conceits.

> [!tip]- William Hazlitt
> ❌ Incorrect. William Hazlitt was a prominent critic of the Romantic period.

> [!tip]- John Dryden
> ❌ Incorrect. While Dryden commented on Donne's style, this specific and memorable phrase belongs to Dr. Johnson.

> [!tip]- T.S. Eliot
> ❌ Incorrect. T.S. Eliot was a great admirer of the Metaphysical poets and praised them for their "unification of sensibility," directly opposing Johnson's critique.

#### Q. 107 (ST-22, 2011/12): Which statement is not true of metaphysical poetry?
> [!tip]- It uses conceits.
> ❌ Incorrect. This statement is true. The use of elaborate, intellectual metaphors known as "metaphysical conceits" is a hallmark of the style.

> [!tip]- It has colloquial diction.
> ❌ Incorrect. This statement is true. Metaphysical poetry often uses the rhythm and vocabulary of everyday speech, creating a direct, argumentative tone.

> [!tip]- It employs philosophical explorations.
> ❌ Incorrect. This statement is true. The poetry is deeply engaged with philosophical and theological questions about love, death, and God.

> [!tip]- It talk of heroic deeds.
> ✅ Correct! This statement is not true. Metaphysical poetry focuses on personal, intellectual, and spiritual themes, not the grand, heroic deeds characteristic of epic poetry.

### IV. Neo-Classical Period

#### Q. 76 (ST-22, 2011/12): Which of the following is not a neo-classical characteristics?
> [!tip]- Literature of the period depicted the countryside and the people who lived there
> ✅ Correct! This is not a Neo-Classical characteristic; it is a hallmark of the later **Romantic period**. Neo-Classical literature was overwhelmingly urban, focusing on the city, society, and satire of manners.

> [!tip]- Literature of the period deals with urban themes and fashionable, aristocratic society
> ❌ Incorrect. This is a key characteristic of Neo-Classical literature, especially Restoration Comedy and the satires of Pope.

> [!tip]- Respect of classical rules
> ❌ Incorrect. This is a core tenet of Neo-Classicism. Writers looked to ancient Greek and Roman models for order, structure, and decorum.

> [!tip]- Imitation of the great classical masters
> ❌ Incorrect. This is the very definition of Neo-Classicism. Authors like Dryden and Pope consciously emulated classical writers like Virgil, Horace, and Homer.

#### Q. 126 (Exam 2011): Which one of the following literary periods is termed as the Augustan Age in the history of English Literature ?
> [!tip]- 1649 - 1660
> ❌ Incorrect. This period is the Commonwealth or Interregnum.

> [!tip]- 1660 - 1670
> ❌ Incorrect. This is the early part of the Restoration period (1660-1700).

> [!tip]- 1700 - 1745
> ✅ Correct! The Augustan Age (named after the Roman Emperor Augustus) refers to the early 18th century, the era of Pope, Swift, Addison, and Steele. It is considered the high point of Neo-Classical literature.

> [!tip]- 1745 - 1798
> ❌ Incorrect. This later period of the 18th century is known as the Age of Sensibility or the Age of Johnson.

#### Q. 143 (Sr. Teacher 2018): Dryden and Pope belonged to -
> [!tip]- Metaphysical school of poetry
> ❌ Incorrect. The Metaphysical school (Donne, Marvell) preceded them in the 17th century.

> [!tip]- Neo - classical movement
> ✅ Correct! John Dryden was the dominant figure of the Restoration period, and Alexander Pope was the central poet of the Augustan Age. Both are the leading figures of English Neo-Classicism.

> [!tip]- The romantic revival
> ❌ Incorrect. The Romantic Revival (Wordsworth, Coleridge) was a reaction *against* the Neo-Classical values that Dryden and Pope represented.

> [!tip]- The pre- Raphaelites
> ❌ Incorrect. The Pre-Raphaelites were a group of artists and writers in the mid-Victorian era.

### V. Romantic Period / Romanticism

#### Q. 64 (TSS-24): Certain aspects of Romanticism in the 18th century did not include -
> [!tip]- The cult of noble savage
> ❌ Incorrect. The idea of the "noble savage" (that man is inherently good in his natural state and corrupted by civilization) was a key pre-Romantic and Romantic idea, associated with Jean-Jacques Rousseau.

> [!tip]- Tamed and orderly manifestation of scenery
> ✅ Correct! This is the opposite of the Romantic ideal. Romantics were drawn to the wild, untamed, and sublime aspects of nature, not orderly, manicured landscapes which were more characteristic of the Neo-Classical aesthetic.

> [!tip]- Increasing interest in nature
> ❌ Incorrect. An intense interest in and reverence for nature is one of the most important characteristics of Romanticism.

> [!tip]- Need for spontaneity in thought and action
> ❌ Incorrect. Spontaneity was highly valued as a sign of authentic feeling and genius, famously expressed in Wordsworth's definition of poetry as the "spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings."

#### Q. 65 (STS-32, 2011/13): The Waverly novel (1814) written by Sir Walter Scott deals with –
> [!tip]- Scottish History
> ✅ Correct! *Waverley* is considered the first major historical novel and deals with the Jacobite rising of 1745 in Scotland. Sir Walter Scott is famous for his historical novels set in the Scottish past.

> [!tip]- Pastoral Romance
> ❌ Incorrect. Pastoral romance typically features idealized shepherds and rural settings, which is not the focus of *Waverley*'s historical plot.

> [!tip]- Supernatural Forces
> ❌ Incorrect. While some Romantic works embrace the supernatural (like Coleridge's poetry), *Waverley* is grounded in historical events.

> [!tip]- The Plight of common man
> ❌ Incorrect. While the common man is celebrated in Romanticism (e.g., by Wordsworth), Scott's novel focuses more on historical events, adventure, and the clash of cultures.

#### Q. 73 (STS-32, 2011/13): Select the correct option. Two important novelists of the Romantic Age were :
> [!tip]- Thomas Hardy and Melville
> ❌ Incorrect. Thomas Hardy was a late Victorian novelist, and Herman Melville was an American writer of the same period.

> [!tip]- Francis Bacon and Thomas Moore
> ❌ Incorrect. Francis Bacon and Thomas Moore were major prose writers of the English Renaissance.

> [!tip]- Jane Austen and Sir Walter Scott
> ✅ Correct! Jane Austen (though stylistically more Neo-Classical) wrote during the Romantic period, and Sir Walter Scott was a towering figure of Romantic prose, inventing the historical novel.

> [!tip]- Charles Lamb and Hardy
> ❌ Incorrect. Charles Lamb was a great Romantic essayist, but Thomas Hardy was a Victorian novelist.

#### Q. 77 (ST-22, 2011/12): Two of the important characteristics of romantic age were -
> [!tip]- Imitating the great writers and their works
> ❌ Incorrect. This was a characteristic of the Neo-Classical age. The Romantics valued originality and spontaneity.

> [!tip]- Celebration of classical literature and nature
> ❌ Incorrect. While they celebrated nature, they rebelled against the strict rules and focus on classical literature that defined the Neo-Classical period.

> [!tip]- Celebration of common man and nature
> ✅ Correct! These are two central tenets of Romanticism, most famously articulated by William Wordsworth in his Preface to *Lyrical Ballads*.

> [!tip]- Strict rules for writing poetry and drama
> ❌ Incorrect. This was a feature of Neo-Classicism. The Romantics rejected strict rules in favor of emotional expression and organic form.

#### Q. 79 (Paper 2016-17): The movement which originated in the late 18th century, emphasizing subjectivity, inspiration and the primacy of the individual is known as -
> [!tip]- Renaissance
> ❌ Incorrect. The Renaissance emphasized humanism and the potential of man, but the specific focus on subjectivity and individual feeling as the *primary* source of art defines Romanticism.

> [!tip]- Existentialism
> ❌ Incorrect. Existentialism is a 20th-century philosophical movement that also emphasizes the individual, but within the context of an absurd universe and radical freedom.

> [!tip]- Naturalism
> ❌ Incorrect. Naturalism was a late 19th-century movement that emphasized determinism, viewing individuals as controlled by their environment and heredity.

> [!tip]- Romanticism
> ✅ Correct! This is a perfect definition of Romanticism. It championed subjective experience, individual genius ("inspiration"), and the importance of the individual's emotional life over societal norms.

#### Q. 114 (Exam 2011): Who are associated with the Romantic Revival ?
> [!tip]- Wordsworth, Coleridge, Pope and Dryden
> ❌ Incorrect. Pope and Dryden were the leading figures of the Neo-Classical age, the very movement the Romantics were rebelling against.

> [!tip]- Wordsworth, Coleridge, Keats and Shelley
> ✅ Correct! This list includes the major figures of both the first generation (Wordsworth, Coleridge) and the second generation (Keats, Shelley) of Romantic poets.

> [!tip]- Wordsworth, Southey, Pope and Coleridge
> ❌ Incorrect. This list incorrectly includes Alexander Pope, a Neo-Classical poet.

> [!tip]- Wordsworth, Keats, Byron and Pope
> ❌ Incorrect. This list incorrectly includes Alexander Pope, a Neo-Classical poet.

> [!tip]- Romanticism

### VIII. Modernism (including Symbolism, Stream of Consciousness)

#### Q. 25 (TSS-24): Who coined the term ‘Stream of consciousness’?
> [!tip]- William James
> ✅ Correct! William James, an American psychologist and philosopher, first used the term in his book *The Principles of Psychology* (1890) to describe the continuous, flowing nature of human thought. Modernist writers then adapted this psychological concept into a literary technique.

> [!tip]- Virginia Woolf
> ❌ Incorrect. Virginia Woolf was a master practitioner of the stream of consciousness *technique* in her novels, but she did not coin the term.

> [!tip]- Henry James
> ❌ Incorrect. Henry James, the brother of William James, was a novelist whose focus on the psychological interior of his characters paved the way for Modernism, but he did not coin this specific term.

> [!tip]- George Meredith

#### Q. 49 (TSS-24): Which among the following statements related to Modernism is not correct?
> [!tip]- The Golden Bough had profound influence on Modernism.
> ❌ Incorrect. This statement is correct. Sir James Frazer's *The Golden Bough*, a comparative study of myth and religion, was hugely influential. It provided a "mythical method" for writers like T.S. Eliot to structure their work.

> [!tip]- The working of the conscious-mind became an important subject.
> ✅ Correct! This statement is not correct because it is too limited. Modernism was fascinated with the workings of the **unconscious** and **subconscious** mind, not just the conscious mind. Influenced by Freud, they explored the hidden drives, irrational impulses, and dream-like states of their characters.

> [!tip]- It is essentially post-Darwinian.
> ❌ Incorrect. This statement is correct. Modernism developed in a world where Darwin's theories had already challenged traditional views of humanity's place in the universe, contributing to the era's sense of uncertainty.

> [!tip]- It was influenced by the psychological works of Sigmund Freud.
> ❌ Incorrect. This statement is correct. Freud's theories of the unconscious, psychoanalysis, and the Oedipus complex had a profound impact on Modernist writers' approach to character and motivation.

#### Q. 71 (STS-32, 2011/13): Choose the correct option. ‘Fragmentation and Inconclusiveness’ are a mark of the :
> [!tip]- Modern period
> ✅ Correct! Fragmentation (of plot, character, and perspective) and a sense of inconclusiveness are hallmark characteristics of Modernist literature. They reflect the perceived shattering of a coherent world after World War I and the collapse of traditional certainties. *The Waste Land* is a prime example.

> [!tip]- Post-modern period
> ❌ Incorrect. While Post-Modernism also features fragmentation and inconclusiveness, it often treats them with playfulness and irony, whereas Modernism treats them with a sense of tragedy and loss. Given the options, "Modern period" is the primary answer for this set of characteristics.

> [!tip]- Victorian period,
> ❌ Incorrect. The Victorian period, especially in the novel, valued coherent plots and clear moral resolutions.

> [!tip]- Neo-classical period
> ❌ Incorrect. The Neo-Classical period prized order, clarity, and logical structure above all else.

#### Q. 91 (ST-22, 2011/12): Major themes of the modernist movement were -

#### Q. 93 (ST-22, 2011/12): Which literary movement came into being by rejecting romanticism?
> [!tip]- Neo-classicism
> ❌ Incorrect. Neo-Classicism preceded Romanticism. Romanticism was, in fact, a rejection of Neo-Classicism.

> [!tip]- Supernaturalism
> ❌ Incorrect. This is not a distinct movement in the same way. An interest in the supernatural is a feature of Romanticism and Gothic literature.

> [!tip]- Modernism
> ✅ Correct! Modernism rejected the perceived sentimentality, emotionalism, and focus on the self that were characteristic of Romanticism. Modernists like T.S. Eliot favored a more impersonal, intellectual, and ironic approach to art.

> [!tip]- Hedonism
> ❌ Incorrect. Hedonism is a philosophical pursuit of pleasure, associated more with the Aesthetic movement than a full literary period.

#### Q. 108 (ST-22, 2011/12): The works - 'Heart of Darkness' by Joseph Conrad, 'The Waste Land' by T.S. Eliot, 'Ulysses' by James Joyce and 'In a Station of the Metro' by Ezra Pound are examples of -

> [!tip]- Modernist literature
> ✅ Correct! This is a list of the cornerstone texts of Modernism. *Heart of Darkness* is an early example, *The Waste Land* is the quintessential Modernist poem, *Ulysses* is the landmark Modernist novel, and Pound's poem is a perfect example of Imagism, a key Modernist poetic movement.

> [!tip]- Victorian literature
> ❌ Incorrect. These works were a radical break from Victorian literary conventions.

> [!tip]- Gothic literature

#### Q. 121 (ST-22, 2011/12): ‘Stream of Consciousness’ gives you -
> [!tip]- Each thought is orderly and logical
> ❌ Incorrect. This is the opposite of the technique's aim. The mind is portrayed as associative and non-logical.

> [!tip]- A narration devoted to an external occurrence
> ❌ Incorrect. The focus is on *internal* consciousness, not external events.

> [!tip]- A flow of thought not having coherent structure or cohesion
> ✅ Correct! This accurately describes the technique. It attempts to capture the jumbled, non-linear, and often incoherent flow of thoughts, sensations, and memories as they occur in a character's mind.

> [!tip]- Easy to read literature
> ❌ Incorrect. Due to its fragmented and non-linear nature, literature using stream of consciousness is famously difficult to read.

#### Q. 126 (ST-22, 2011/12): Which among the following works does not employ the ‘Stream of Consciousness’ technique?
> [!tip]- Pride and Prejudice by Jane Austen

> ❌ Incorrect. This is a classic example of the stream of consciousness novel, moving fluidly through the minds of Clarissa Dalloway and other characters.

#### Q. 126 (Spl Edu 2015): identify the following literary movements : poetry distinguished by its far fetched images and conceit is
> [!tip]- symbolic
> ❌ Incorrect. Symbolic poetry uses symbols to suggest mood, but "far-fetched images and conceit" is the specific definition for another style.

> [!tip]- metaphysical
> ✅ Correct! This is the classic definition of Metaphysical poetry. A "conceit" is a clever, complex, and often "far-fetched" extended metaphor that yokes together very dissimilar ideas (e.g., lovers' souls and a drawing compass).

> [!tip]- romantic
> ❌ Incorrect. Romantic poetry focuses on emotion, nature, and spontaneity.

> [!tip]- modernist
> ❌ Incorrect. While Modernist poetry uses complex imagery, the specific combination of "far-fetched images and conceit" points directly to the Metaphysical poets.

### IX. Post-Modernism (including Existentialism)

#### Q. 2 (TSS-24): The term ‘Existentialism’ was coined by -
> [!tip]- Soren Kierkegaard
> ❌ Incorrect. Søren Kierkegaard is considered the "father of existentialism" due to his 19th-century philosophical work on individual choice and subjective truth, but he did not use the term itself.

> [!tip]- Jean-Paul Sartre
> ✅ Correct! The term was popularized by the French philosopher Jean-Paul Sartre in the mid-20th century. He adopted it to describe his own philosophy after it was initially used by Gabriel Marcel.

> [!tip]- Martin Heidegger
> ❌ Incorrect. Martin Heidegger was a major German philosopher whose work on "Being" (*Dasein*) was central to existential thought, but he did not coin the term and later distanced himself from it.

> [!tip]- Friedrich Nietzsche
> ❌ Incorrect. Nietzsche was a key 19th-century forerunner of existentialism, but he wrote before the term was created.

#### Q. 23 (TSS-24): To describe the mentality of the non-industrialized subject or 'Primitive man', Claude Levi-Strauss used the term -
> [!tip]- Bripolar
> ❌ Incorrect. This is not a recognized term in Levi-Strauss's work.

> [!tip]- Nomadological
> ❌ Incorrect. This term is associated with the post-structuralist philosophers Deleuze and Guattari.

> [!tip]- Rhizome
> ❌ Incorrect. This is another key term from Deleuze and Guattari, describing a non-hierarchical network of connections.

> [!tip]- Bricolage
> ✅ Correct! The anthropologist Claude Lévi-Strauss used the term "bricolage" to describe the thought process of so-called "primitive" societies. A "bricoleur" is someone who creatively uses whatever materials are at hand to solve a problem, much like mythical thought pieces together existing cultural elements. This concept was influential in post-structuralist and post-modern theory.

#### Q. 43 (TSS-24): Which of the following statement is True?
> [!tip]- Theatre of the Absurd belongs to Victorian Period.
> ❌ Incorrect. The Theatre of the Absurd (e.g., Samuel Beckett) emerged after World War II and is a key expression of Post-Modern and Existentialist ideas.

> [!tip]- Many works of post-modern literature – Borges, Beckett, Vladimir Nabokov - adhere to traditional literary rubrics.
> ❌ Incorrect. This is the opposite of the truth. These authors are famous for *breaking* with traditional literary rubrics through techniques like metafiction, unreliable narration, and non-linear plots.

> [!tip]- The term post modernism is applied to literature and art after World War I.
> ❌ Incorrect. The period after World War I is **Modernism**. Post-Modernism is generally applied to the period after **World War II**.

> [!tip]- Post modernism in literature and the arts has parallels with the movement known as post-structuralism in linguistics and literary theory.
> ✅ Correct! This is a perfect definition. Post-Modernism is the artistic counterpart to the philosophical movement of post-structuralism. Both share a skepticism of stable meaning, a focus on language as a system of signs, and a desire to deconstruct traditional structures.

#### Q. 68 (TSS-24): The Postmodern Condition (1986) was written by -
> [!tip]- Jean-Francois Lyotard
> ✅ Correct! Jean-François Lyotard's book, *The Postmodern Condition: A Report on Knowledge* (published in French in 1979), is a foundational text of Post-Modern theory. It is famous for defining postmodernism as "incredulity toward metanarratives."

> [!tip]- Michel Foucault
> ❌ Incorrect. Michel Foucault was a major post-structuralist thinker, but he wrote other works like *Madness and Civilization* and *Discipline and Punish*.

> [!tip]- Jean Baudrillard
> ❌ Incorrect. Jean Baudrillard was a postmodern theorist known for his work on simulacra and hyperreality.

> [!tip]- Jacques Derrida
> ❌ Incorrect. Jacques Derrida was the father of deconstruction.

#### Q. 79 (STS-32, 2011/13): Choose the correct option. A narrative mode that attempts 'to depict the multitudinous thoughts and feelings which pass through the mind of a narrator' is:

#### Q. 120 (ST-22, 2011/12): ‘Inconclusiveness’ is the mark of -
> [!tip]- Post-modern literature
> ✅ Correct! While Modernism also features inconclusiveness, it is a defining trait of Post-Modernism. Post-modern works often resist closure, offering multiple endings or no ending at all, to undermine the idea of a single, authoritative truth.

> [!tip]- Metaphysical literature
> ❌ Incorrect. Metaphysical poetry is known for its logical arguments, which lead to a conclusion, however surprising.

> [!tip]- Modern literature
> ❌ Incorrect. While Modernist works can be inconclusive (e.g., the end of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*), they often do so with a sense of tragic fragmentation. Post-modernism embraces inconclusiveness more programmatically and playfully. Given the starkness of the term, it's most strongly associated with Post-Modernism.

> [!tip]- Jacobean literature
> ❌ Incorrect. Jacobean tragedies, for example, have very conclusive, often bloody, endings.

> [!tip]- Hypertextuality
> ❌ Incorrect. Hypertextuality refers to the interlinking of texts in a non-linear format, as on the web.

> [!tip]- Simulacrum
> ❌ Incorrect. A simulacrum is a copy without an original, a concept from Baudrillard.

> [!tip]- Aporia
> ✅ Correct! An aporia is a term used in philosophy and deconstruction (by Jacques Derrida) to refer to an irresolvable internal contradiction or logical impasse within a text. It is a point of undecidability that undermines any stable meaning.
