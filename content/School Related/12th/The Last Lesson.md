**1 The Last Lesson**

**About the author**
Alphonse Daudet (1840-1897) was a French novelist and short-story writer. *The Last Lesson* is set in the days of the Franco-Prussian War (1870-1871) in which France was defeated by Prussia led by Bismarck. Prussia then consisted of what now are the nations of Germany, Poland and parts of Austria. In this story the French districts of Alsace and Lorraine have passed into Prussian hands. Read the story to find out what effect this had on life at school.

I started for school very late that morning and was in great dread of a scolding, especially because M. Hamel had said that he would question us on participles, and I did not know the first word about them. For a moment I thought of running away and spending the day out of doors. It was so warm, so bright! The birds were chirping at the edge of the woods; and in the open field back of the sawmill the Prussian soldiers were drilling. It was all much more tempting than the rule for participles, but I had the strength to resist, and hurried off to school.

When I passed the town hall there was a crowd in front of the bulletin-board. For the last two years all our bad news had come from there — the lost battles, the draft, the orders of the commanding officer — and I thought to myself, without stopping, “What can be the matter now?”

> जब मैं टाउन हॉल (सभा भवन) के पास से गुज़रा तो सूचना-पट्ट के सामने भीड़ थी। पिछले दो सालों से हमारी सारी बुरी खबरें वहीं से आई थीं - हारी हुई लड़ाइयाँ, सेना में भर्ती के आदेश, कमांडिंग ऑफिसर के आदेश - और मैंने बिना रुके मन ही मन सोचा, "अब क्या बात हो सकती है?"

Usually, when school began, there was a great bustle, which could be heard out in the street, the opening and closing of desks, lessons repeated in unison, very loud, with our hands over our ears to understand better, and the teacher’s great ruler rapping on the table. But now it was all so still! I had counted on the commotion to get to my desk without being seen; but, of course, that day everything had to be as quiet as Sunday morning. Through the window I saw my classmates, already in their places, and M. Hamel walking up and down with his terrible iron ruler under his arm. I had to open the door and go in before everybody. You can imagine how I blushed and how frightened I was.

**हिंदी अनुवाद**

I jumped over the bench and sat down at my desk. Not till then, when I had got a little over my fright, did I see that our teacher had on his beautiful green coat, his frilled shirt, and the little black silk cap, all embroidered, that he never wore except on inspection and prize days. Besides, the whole school seemed so strange and solemn.

**हिंदी अनुवाद**
> मैं बेंच के ऊपर से कूदा और अपनी डेस्क पर बैठ गया। जब मेरा डर थोड़ा कम हुआ, तब मैंने देखा कि हमारे शिक्षक ने अपना सुंदर हरा कोट, अपनी झालरदार कमीज़, और छोटी काली रेशम की टोपी पहन रखी थी, जो पूरी तरह से कढ़ाई की हुई थी, जिसे वह निरीक्षण और पुरस्कार वितरण के दिनों के अलावा कभी नहीं पहनते थे। इसके अलावा, पूरा स्कूल बहुत अजीब और गंभीर लग रहा था।

But the thing that surprised me most was to see, on the back benches that were always empty, the village people sitting quietly like ourselves; old Hauser, with his three-cornered hat, the former mayor, the former postmaster, and several others besides. Everybody looked sad; and Hauser had brought an old primer, thumbed at the edges, and he held it open on his knees with his great spectacles lying across the pages.

**हिंदी अनुवाद**
> लेकिन जिस बात ने मुझे सबसे ज़्यादा हैरान किया, वह यह थी कि पीछे की बेंचें, जो हमेशा खाली रहती थीं, पर गाँव के लोग हमारी तरह ही चुपचाप बैठे थे; बूढ़ा हॉसर, अपनी तिकोनी टोपी के साथ, पूर्व मेयर, पूर्व पोस्टमास्टर, और कई अन्य लोग भी। सब उदास दिख रहे थे; और हॉसर एक पुरानी प्राइमर (बालपोथी) लाया था, जो किनारों से मुड़ी हुई थी, और उसने उसे अपने घुटनों पर खोलकर रखा था और उसका बड़ा चश्मा पन्नों पर पड़ा था।

While I was wondering about it all, M. Hamel mounted his chair, and, in the same grave and gentle tone which he had used to me, said, “My children, this is the last lesson I shall give you. The order has come from Berlin to teach only German in the schools of Alsace and Lorraine. The new master comes tomorrow. This is your last French lesson. I want you to be very attentive.”

**हिंदी अनुवाद**
> जब मैं इस सब के बारे में सोच ही रहा था, एम. हैमेल अपनी कुर्सी पर बैठे, और उसी गंभीर और कोमल स्वर में, जिसका उन्होंने मेरे लिए इस्तेमाल किया था, कहा, "मेरे बच्चों, यह आखिरी पाठ है जो मैं तुम्हें दूँगा। बर्लिन से आदेश आया है कि अल्सास और लॉरेन के स्कूलों में केवल जर्मन पढ़ाई जाएगी। नया मास्टर कल आएगा। यह तुम्हारा आखिरी फ्रांसीसी पाठ है। मैं चाहता हूँ कि तुम बहुत ध्यान से सुनो।"

What a thunderclap these words were to me!
Oh, the wretches; that was what they had put up at the town-hall!

**हिंदी अनुवाद**
> ये शब्द मेरे लिए बिजली की कड़कड़ाहट की तरह थे!
> ओह, दुष्ट लोग; यही तो उन्होंने टाउन-हॉल पर लगाया था!

My last French lesson! Why, I hardly knew how to write! I should never learn any more! I must stop there, then! Oh, how sorry I was for not learning my lessons, for seeking birds’ eggs, or going sliding on the Saar! My books, that had seemed such a nuisance a while ago, so heavy to carry, my grammar, and my history of the saints, were old friends now that I couldn’t give up. And M. Hamel, too; the idea that he was going away, that I should never see him again, made me forget all about his ruler and how cranky he was.

**हिंदी अनुवाद**
> मेरा आखिरी फ्रांसीसी पाठ! क्यों, मुझे तो ठीक से लिखना भी नहीं आता था! मुझे अब और कभी नहीं सीखना चाहिए! मुझे वहीं रुकना होगा, फिर! ओह, मैं कितना दुखी था कि मैंने अपने पाठ नहीं सीखे, पक्षियों के अंडे ढूँढ़ने या सार नदी पर फिसलने के लिए चला गया! मेरी किताबें, जो थोड़ी देर पहले इतनी बकवास लगती थीं, ले जाने में इतनी भारी थीं, मेरी व्याकरण, और मेरे संतों का इतिहास, अब पुराने दोस्त थे जिन्हें मैं छोड़ नहीं सकता था। और एम. हैमेल भी; यह विचार कि वह जा रहे हैं, कि मैं उन्हें फिर कभी नहीं देखूँगा, ने मुझे उनके डंडे और उनके चिड़चिड़े स्वभाव के बारे में सब कुछ भुला दिया।

Poor man! It was in honour of this last lesson that he had put on his fine Sunday clothes, and now I understood why the old men of the village were sitting there in the back of the room. It was because they were sorry, too, that they had not gone to school more. It was their way of thanking our master for his forty years of faithful service and of showing their respect for the country that was theirs no more.

**हिंदी अनुवाद**
> बेचारे! यह इस अंतिम पाठ के सम्मान में था कि उन्होंने अपने रविवार के अच्छे कपड़े पहने थे, और अब मैं समझ गया कि गाँव के बूढ़े लोग कमरे के पीछे क्यों बैठे थे। ऐसा इसलिए था क्योंकि उन्हें भी दुख था, कि वे अधिक स्कूल नहीं गए थे। यह हमारे मास्टर को उनकी चालीस साल की वफादार सेवा के लिए धन्यवाद देने और उस देश के प्रति अपना सम्मान दिखाने का उनका तरीका था जो अब उनका नहीं रहा।

What would I not have given to be able to say that dreadful rule for the participle all through, very loud and clear, and without one mistake? But I got mixed up on the first words and stood there, holding on to my desk, my heart beating, and not daring to look up.

> जब मैं यह सब सोच रहा था, मैंने अपना नाम पुकारे जाते हुए सुना। सुनाने की मेरी बारी थी। मैं उस भयानक नियम को, पार्टिसिपल के नियम को, बिना किसी गलती के, बहुत ज़ोर से और साफ-साफ कहने में सक्षम होने के लिए क्या कुछ नहीं दे देता? लेकिन मैं पहले ही शब्दों में उलझ गया और वहीं खड़ा रहा, अपनी डेस्क को पकड़े हुए, मेरा दिल धड़क रहा था, और ऊपर देखने की हिम्मत नहीं कर रहा था।

I heard M. Hamel say to me, “I won’t scold you, little Franz; you must feel bad enough. See how it is! Every day we have said to ourselves, ‘Bah! I’ve plenty of time. I’ll learn it tomorrow.’ And now you see where we’ve come out. Ah, that’s the great trouble with Alsace; she puts off learning till tomorrow. Now those fellows out there will have the right to say to you, ‘How is it; you pretend to be Frenchmen, and yet you can neither speak nor write your own language?’ But you are not the worst, poor little Franz. We’ve all a great deal to reproach ourselves with.”

**हिंदी अनुवाद**
> मैंने एम. हैमेल को मुझसे कहते सुना, "मैं तुम्हें डाँटूगा नहीं, छोटे फ्रांज़; तुम्हें काफी बुरा लगना चाहिए। देखो यह कैसा है! हर दिन हम खुद से कहते हैं, 'बाह! मेरे पास बहुत समय है। मैं इसे कल सीख लूँगा।' और अब तुम देखते हो कि हम कहाँ आ गए हैं। आह, यही अल्सास के साथ बड़ी समस्या है; वह कल पर सीखना टाल देती है। अब बाहर के वे लोग तुम्हें यह कहने का अधिकार रखेंगे, 'यह कैसा है; तुम फ्रांसीसी होने का दिखावा करते हो, और फिर भी तुम अपनी भाषा न तो बोल सकते हो और न ही लिख सकते हो?' लेकिन तुम सबसे बुरे नहीं हो, छोटे फ्रांज़। हम सभी को खुद को बहुत कुछ धिक्कारना है।"

“Your parents were not anxious enough to have you learn. They preferred to put you to work on a farm or at the mills, so as to have a little more money. And I? I’ve been to blame also. ## Page 8: Understanding the text

**1. The people in this story suddenly realise how precious their language is to them. What shows you this? Why does this happen?**
> *   **What shows this:** The realization is shown through several things: M. Hamel's emotional and passionate speech about the beauty of the French language; the presence of the village elders who came to pay their respects to the language they would no longer hear; Franz's sudden regret and his newfound interest in his lessons; and old Hauser crying as he tried to read from his primer. > *   **Innocence and Protest:** It is an innocent but profound question from a child, highlighting how unnatural and wrong the imposition of German feels. Just as it's unnatural for a pigeon to sing in German, it's unnatural to force people to abandon their mother tongue.
> *   **Symbol of Freedom:** The pigeons' cooing is a symbol of natural freedom. Franz wonders if the Prussians will try to take away even this natural freedom, questioning the extent of their control.

## Page 8-9: Talking about the text

**1. “When a people are enslaved, as long as they hold fast to their language it is as if they had the key to their prison.” Can you think of examples in history where a conquered people had their language taken away from them or had a language imposed on them?**
> The quote means that language is a powerful symbol of identity and unity. It keeps a culture's spirit alive and serves as a tool for resistance against oppression.
>
> **Examples:**
> *   **Catalonia under Franco's Spain:** The public use of the Catalan language was banned to suppress regional identity. However, people continued to speak it in private, and it became a powerful symbol of their resistance and cultural survival.
> *   **Native Americans in the US:** For many years, Native American children were forced into boarding schools where they were punished for speaking their indigenous languages, in an attempt to forcibly assimilate them into American culture.
> *   **British India:** English was made the language of administration and higher education, which, while uniting a certain class, also marginalized many native languages. These same native languages, however, became crucial tools for the independence movement to spread ideas and unite the masses.

**2. What happens to a linguistic minority in a state? How do you think they can keep their language alive?**
> A linguistic minority often faces challenges such as lack of education in their mother tongue, difficulty in accessing services, and social pressure to assimilate into the dominant culture. This can lead to a gradual decline in the use of their language.
>
> **They can keep their language alive through:**
> *   **Speaking it at Home:** This is the most critical step to pass the language to the next generation.
> *   **Community Institutions:** Establishing community centers, places of worship, or schools that offer classes in their language and culture (e.g., Gurdwaras offering Punjabi classes in Bangalore).
> *   **Cultural Events:** Celebrating their traditional festivals and events publicly.
> *   **Media and Arts:** Creating and supporting music, films, newspapers, and websites in their own language.

**3. Is it possible to carry pride in one’s language too far? Do you know what ‘linguistic chauvinism’ means?**
> Yes, it is possible. **Linguistic chauvinism** is the aggressive and unreasonable belief that one's own language is superior to all others. It turns healthy pride into prejudice and intolerance, often leading to the discrimination and suppression of other languages and their speakers. The order from Berlin to impose German on the French-speaking people of Alsace and Lorraine is a clear example of linguistic chauvinism.

## Page 10: Noticing form

**Pick out five sentences from the story with this form of the verb (past perfect) and say why this form has been used.**
> The past perfect tense (`had` + `past participle`) is used to describe an action that happened before another action in the past.
>
> 1.  "M. Hamel **had said** that he would question us on participles."
>     > **Reason:** M. Hamel's announcement happened *before* the morning of the story began.
>
> 2.  "For the last two years all our bad news **had come** from there."
>     > **Reason:** The bad news appeared on the bulletin board *before* Franz passed by it on that particular day.
>
> 3.  "I **had counted** on the commotion to get to my desk without being seen."
>     > **Reason:** Franz's planning or relying on the noise happened *before* he actually arrived and found the school silent.
>
> 4.  "But the thing that surprised me most was to see... the village people... old Hauser, with his three-cornered hat... **had brought** an old primer..."
>     > **Reason:** Old Hauser brought the primer with him *before* Franz saw him sitting in the classroom.
>
> 5.  "...the hopvine that he **had planted** himself twined about the windows..."
>     > **Reason:** M. Hamel planted the hopvine many years *before* the day of the last lesson.
